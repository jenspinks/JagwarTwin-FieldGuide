# Lucius Lullaby

> **One-line reading.** The origin point of the whole world: a song written from the floor, where Sir Lucius reaches down to a man who has bottomed out and saves him — not by waking him, but by singing him into a beautiful dream that the song quietly admits is a lie.

Everything in the Jagwar Twin mythology flows forward from this moment. It is the deal being struck, the shadow making first contact, and the lullaby that pulls Roy off the edge by putting him under. The arc that runs from here toward [[SOL]], the Song of Love, is the arc of the entire project.

**Release:** *Lucius Lullaby* (2026), track 2 · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The context is biographical and stark: written at Roy's absolute bottom — age 35, broke, couch-surfing, back in his childhood bedroom, recorded into a MacBook microphone in desperation. This is not a polished studio product; it's a transmission from the floor.

The structure makes the drama legible. **Verse 1 is Roy at the bottom** — the opening sits on the suicidal floor (*"maybe I'm the mistake"*). **Verses 2 and 3 are Lucius's pitch.** The savior voice — *"life is but a dream, I came to set you free / now look into this magic mirror, tell me what you see"* — is Lucius, not a separate divine rescuer. Verse 3 names the speaker as the ancient shadow: *"I was early on the scene, I was down in the dirt."*

And the bargain is right there in the text. Lucius offers *"what you've been dreaming"* — the mansion, the advances, the fans — in exchange for submission: *"you gotta serve somebody, so stop talking and listen."* Worldly success bought with silence. The lullaby *says* "set you free," but it routes Roy into a mirror and offers a deal, and its refrain is not "wake up" — it's *"I'm dreaming."* Lucius saves Roy by putting him under. It is a lullaby that works.

The genius of the song is that it knows it is lying while it sings. The chorus undercuts itself — *"it's a beautiful life… but it's all just a lie."* The outro won't fully cooperate with the dream either: *"the truth will set you free, but nothing's, nothing's what it seems."* A pure lullaby would never name the waking-truth at all. This is lucid sedation — a chosen dream, not a successful deception.

## Its place in the mythology

This is ground zero. The deal between Roy and Lucius originates here: Lucius pulled Roy from the bottom, and whatever the cost, the song frames it as possibly worth it. The terms of that deal — long an open question — are now plain in the lyric: worldly success for silence and service.

The album opens by lulling you into this exact dream. Track 1, [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]], is this song's seamless music-box intro, sharing its refrain (*"it's a beautiful life… I'm dreaming"*) — heard live, the two play as one song. So the record opens by singing you into the "beautiful life" that this track then admits is "all just a lie."

The lullaby is competent, and that is the trap. Lucius is not a cartoon villain — he is partly right, and a lie that keeps you alive is more dangerous than one that doesn't, because you cannot afford to reject it. What kept Roy off the edge *was* the bargain. Mercy and trap are one act. That is the knot the rest of the album spends its time trying to untie.

## Notes

A few of the song's deepest moves are interpretation rather than stated intent. The first-person chorus is a structural puzzle: if the verses are Lucius but the refrain is *"I'm dreaming,"* then either Roy is consenting or the two voices have merged into one by the chorus — and the undecidability may be the point. The lullaby works precisely when you can no longer tell who sings *"beautiful life."*

The figure of Lucius as the first tempter is grounded less in imagery than in rhetoric: the outro quotes scripture (John 8:32, *"the truth will set you free"*) and bends it — the serpent's signature move. That same verse degrades across three eras of the catalogue: held sincerely in *It's Your Time* (2022), paradoxed in [[Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)]] (2023), and weaponized here. In the Kabbalistic framework the lab notes use, the song maps the threshold between the lowest sphere of matter (Malkhut) and the sphere of dreams and reflection (Yesod) — Lucius reaching *down* to pull Roy *up*. That mapping is a frame, not a claim about what Roy intended.

The honest floor: a disillusionment ballad written from a real rock-bottom, with a beautiful melody that names itself a lie. The serpent cosmology is the resonance on top; the age-35 collapse is the ground.

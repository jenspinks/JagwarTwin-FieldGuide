# Pay Attention (feat. little luna)

> **One-line reading.** The song personifies Attention as an ancient power that offers you your desires in exchange for the one thing it actually consumes — your awareness — and stages the transaction as a carnival pitch that collects its toll in the very act of being heard.

**Release:** *33* (2022) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

It is a two-hander. **little luna** is the guide who opens by introducing the listener to someone — *"Hello, Attention, I'd like to introduce you to someone"* — and carries the chorus that states the terms. **Roy voices Attention itself**, the personified power making the pitch.

Attention speaks like a carnival barker: *"Step right up to my Door… I can show you what's in Store."* It names a price and minimizes it — *"there's a little price you pay,"* and later *"just a small price and a rendezvous."* It enumerates the desires as universal — *"you want the money? you want the car? you want the big BIG house?"* And the chorus puns payment on awareness: *"everybody's gotta pay me, you gotta pay attention."* The currency you spend *is* the resource being harvested.

In the second verse it borrows sacred language — *"they say stars are born from light, and you're so much more than you mind"* — flattering the listener with a soul-beyond-mind promise. The bridge expands the narrator's scale: *"eyes all around my head," "you can see anything when you see my view,"* and finally *"I've been behind the empires that rise; what they televise is just my disguise."* It closes on a naming ritual: *"What's my name? Attention!"*, three times, escalating.

## Its place in the mythology

This is the hidden god of the modern world, treated as a force rather than a person. It tempts only by offering, never by threatening — money, status, recognition — and the cost stays understated precisely because the payment is awareness itself. The chorus pun states the thesis outright: the currency spent and the resource extracted are one substance.

Crucially, Attention is an early prototype of the Sir Lucius *function* — persuasion not force, leverage on existing desire, intimate knowledge of the want, concealment behind visible structures. Lucius doesn't appear, but the song works the territory he later personifies. Note the inversion of the usual casting: here Roy voices the tempter, the persuader-role Lucius normally carries, while a second voice guides the listener toward him.

The song enacts its own bargain. The closing *"What's my name? / Attention"* makes answering the entity's name an act of paying attention — the very payment the song describes. To engage the question is to pay. It is a closed loop, the demand creating the surrender it asks for, and the catalogue's purest statement of temptation-through-consent: Attention compels no one, the listener assents.

## Notes

The song works perfectly well as a witty attention-economy satire — an advertising or influencer pitch — with no metaphysics required; the deity framing is the deeper read, not something the lyric asserts.

Several framework readings sit underneath: Mammon and idolatry (status and visibility as objects of devotion); the Demiurge (lower powers holding consciousness captive by directing awareness outward); Hermes the trickster (messenger and dealer who stays invisible behind the message). One grounded thread is worth naming: Roy's own "I'm 33" board prompts, in his hand, *"How to anthropomorphize attention? → Marduk"* — documented evidence that personifying Attention as a deity was an explicit working move, not only a reader's frame. The board treats money and attention as forms of one externalized "life force"; *Pay Attention* runs that ledger a step further by making awareness the currency. The board engagement is grounded; the single-economy reading is the correspondence it suggests.

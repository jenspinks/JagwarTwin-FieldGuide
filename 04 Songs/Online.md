# Online

> **One-line reading.** Identity collapse staged as a jingle — the surveillance machine that captures your face and infiltrates your brain, wrapped in an upbeat consumer earworm, so the song *is* the sedation it describes.

**Release:** *33* (2022), opening track · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

*Online* opens the album, and the choice matters: *33* begins on a surveillance-jingle. It maps the architecture — database, prediction, "everyone's a guru" — that the later mythology personifies as Lucius. The machine precedes the mask.

## What happens in the song

The chorus is built like a commercial: *"you can get it online… and when you get it online, you feel good (feeling good!)."* The hook is indistinguishable from the pitch. The puns are deliberate — *"they know what you like in bed before you come in two"* (come into the world / come in two / the sexual sense at once); the system's offer *"buy, buy, buy"* that turns to *"bye, bye, bye"* — consumption and farewell as the same sound.

The flattening is collective, not personal: *"there's an army up against me and they got my face… we're stuck just like a bug inside your database."* Every horror is immediately normalized — *"they know my every step, they infiltrate my brain, but it's okay."* And every spiritual authority is stacked up at once — *"all the shamans and the Brahmans say we come from space"* — then deflated by a joke: *"maybe Jupiter is stupider than people say."*

Roy's own framing of the song is, importantly, playful — he has called it "so fun and so ridiculous." Both registers are real at once: the song is genuinely funny, and its lyrics name database identity and predictive profiling with real precision.

## Its place in the mythology

*Online* is the bridge track. It maps the conditions that produce Lucius before Lucius exists as a named figure: identity becomes data, attention becomes currency, prediction becomes influence, visibility becomes survival, selfhood becomes performance. The database precedes the mask; the algorithm precedes the performer. Lucius isn't the creator of the Circus — he's its most successful native inhabitant.

Read against the album, it sits one rung before the self internalizes all of this as a voice. The control that *Online* shows acting from the outside world later migrates inward: database, prediction, recommendation, habit, identity, self-narration — the voice of Lucius.

Two public posts catch the same line split across the two poles. Roy's own — *"Everyone's a guru. We are now fully in the aquarium"* — is rueful: we're all trapped performing authority, himself included. A post signed Sir Lucius makes the identical observation as contempt, ranking the field by streams and "cool," everyone worthless except him. One sentiment, two poles, audible in tone alone.

## Notes

The web image is the song's sharpest inversion — *"internet is like a web and we're the Spider's food."* A web is supposed to mean connection, the internet's founding myth; the song flips it so the human is no longer the spider navigating for information but the prey, tracked and digested. It is the recurring JT reversal in its earliest clear form: circus to cage, mirror to trap, connection to extraction.

Several framework readings hover here without being claimed as intent. The trapped-spark cosmology of Gnosticism fits the self bound inside an inescapable data-matrix — *"we come from space… but we're stuck like a bug inside your database"* is nearly a one-line statement of transcendent origin and material entrapment. The Hermetic principle of vibration — mind reaching mind by "induction" — is something Roy demonstrably engaged on his "I'm 33" board; *Online* runs that benign idea as horror, the voluntary tuning becoming involuntary capture. These are correspondences the song's images rhyme with, not doctrines it states.

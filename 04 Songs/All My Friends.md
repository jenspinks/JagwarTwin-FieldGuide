# All My Friends

> **One-line reading.** A communal-apocalypse song — clone-city alienation and end-times dread answered not by escape, denial, or doctrine but by refusing to go out alone.

**Release:** standalone single, May 26, 2023 (Big Loud) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

Writers: Roy English and Colin Brittain; produced by Colin Brittain. A white-on-black tour video — Earth ringed by figures and flying saucers — accompanies it.

## What happens in the song

The frame is apocalyptic: *"there's nowhere to go and the world is ending," "all friends say this whole world is gunna die."* The speaker feels *"like a clone in a crowded city,"* withdraws (*"lately I don't wanna go outside"*), and can't tell motion from progress (*"I can't stop spinning / I thought I was winning"*). The resolution is the chorus: *"but if I'm going out it's with all my friends."*

A portrait sits at the center — Jenny, who *"used to be a dancer / now she's got the answer / preaching bout the End Time days,"* carrying a *"big red flag."* And the song reaches for an old signal: *"I feel it in the air tonight / coming like a drum / from 1981"* — the title phrase, the iconic drum, and the release year of Phil Collins' *"In the Air Tonight"* in three consecutive lines. The final verse universalizes it: *"everyone I love is here / flying on this rock / no time left on the clock."*

## Its place in the mythology

The spine is communal presence, not nihilism. The song neither fixes the apocalypse nor denies it; it asks what relation remains when the end feels inevitable, and answers: go out together, not alone. Solidarity, not salvation-by-doctrine — a reading Roy's own release-week framing supports. He posted that he *"released this song today and played it in LA for the first time with all my friends"* — the song presented as a literal communal act. The chorus holds a real double-edge: beautiful (chosen love) and unsettling (acceptance of the going-down) — the same tension as [[Life Is Good]], its sibling six months later.

The loneliness here is inside overpopulation, not emptiness: surrounded by people, the speaker feels replicated rather than connected. The chorus is the corrective — a crowd flattens you into a clone; friends restore particularity. The same multiplicity that alienates becomes protective once it's relational: the city sees a copy, friends see you. This clone-anxiety is the 2023 antecedent of [[City of Angels]]' *"everyone looks just like you"* (a later architectural correspondence, not an intent toward a not-yet-existing arc).

Jenny compresses a transformation: embodiment (dance) exchanged for certainty (preaching the End Times). The song doesn't mock her — she names the dread everyone feels — but it doesn't trust her either: Jenny has the answer, and the song trusts being-together instead. The *"big red flag"* is double-coded — the literal banner of a street-preacher, and the idiom (Jenny is herself a warning sign).

## Notes

The plainest reading is a warm end-of-the-world-with-friends anthem, and Roy's own framing (he played it with his friends) grounds exactly that — the clone, Loop, and City-of-Angels architecture layers on top. *"Flying on this rock"* universalizes the chorus: Earth itself is the vehicle, the species a friend group on a single ship, the intimate chorus and planetary frame the same image at two scales (the tour video literalizes it). The 1981 reference is grounded as an allusion; its function — the suspended drop, dread arriving as an old rhythm through time — is the reading. Roy also posted *"Jenny / = / 408"* on the release date; the post is real but its meaning is open (a number-song trope, an area code, a timestamp — none asserted).

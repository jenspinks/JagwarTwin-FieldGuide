# All My Friends

> **One-line reading.** The world is ending, and the song's whole answer to that is a preposition: not *how* to survive it, not *whether* to believe it — just *with whom* to go. Clone-city loneliness and end-times dread, resolved by refusing to go out alone.

> [!info] Album & credits
> **Release:** standalone single — **May 26, 2023** (Big Loud). No album, no track number; a post-*33*, 2023 "transition"-era single, sequenced between *Good Time (1:08)* and *Life Is Good*.
> **Written by** Roy English & Colin Brittain · **Produced by** Colin Brittain
> **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com/Jagwar-twin-all-my-friends-lyrics) · [streaming](https://open.spotify.com/track/2OaPi51lj7mjre72U4sMZ0)

An end-of-the-world anthem you'd actually want at the party. The premise is grim — everyone's saying the planet's about to die — and the song's response is neither panic nor denial but a shrug in good company: *if I'm going out, it's with all my friends.* Roy has been unusually literal about it. Ahead of release he posted, plainly, *"It's about all my friends,"* and the day after: he *"played it in LA for the first time with all my friends."* So the cozy read isn't a listener's projection — it's the stated one. Everything the vault layers on top (the clone, the spinning, the seed of a self that shows up three years later) sits *on top* of that warmth, and the page keeps them in that order on purpose.

## At a glance

- **Era / release** — standalone single, May 26 2023 (Big Loud); post-*33* transition era, dropped during the wrap of the spring US tour with Transviolet, the same week as Roy's 35th birthday (May 22). No album.
- **In one line** — apocalyptic-communal: the end is coming, so choose who you face it with.
- **Who speaks** — [[Roy|Jagwar Twin]], first-person, turning communal. This is a *2023* song — **before** the Sir Lucius billing and the whole persona arc — so the "I" here is just Roy, no mask on yet.
- **Key symbols** — the clone (loneliness inside a crowd) · the spin ([[The Loop and the Circle|the Loop]], rotation mistaken for progress) · the drum-from-1981 (dread arriving through time) · *flying on this rock* (Earth as the vehicle) · the game-clock that runs out.

## The song, mapped

The track is built in short, one-line-at-a-time stanzas, and it moves by *repetition* — the clone/world-ending verse and the spin/win/chorus block both come back, and the whole thing ends on a doubled *spinning / winning / all my friends* tag. So its center of gravity is the chorus: everything departs from it and returns to it. The argument underneath the loop runs isolation → competition → company, in six beats.

| # | The beat | Short excerpt | What it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **The Jenny portrait** | *"used to be a dancer / now she's got the answer / preaching bout the End Time days"* — carrying a *"big red flag"* | Opens on the song's one named character: a transformation compressed into four lines (dance → doctrine). She's the dread given a face. |
| **2** | **The personal pivot** | *"chasing down a feeling / I used to believe in"* | Turns the camera on the speaker: her *certainty* against his *un-certainty*. He's pursuing a conviction he's already lost. |
| **3** | **The frame + the retreat** | *"I feel like a clone in a crowded city"* · *"lately I don't wanna go outside"* · *"all friends say this whole world is gunna die"* | The apocalyptic frame lands, and the speaker shrinks from it — alienated *inside* the crowd, then withdrawing from it entirely. |
| **4** | **The hinge (spin → chorus)** | *"I can't stop spinning / I thought I was winning / but if I'm going out it's with all my friends"* | The turn. Competition (*winning*) collapses into solidarity (*all my friends*). This is the whole song in three lines. |
| **5** | **The 1981 bridge** | *"I feel it in the air tonight / coming like a drum / from 1981"* | Three lines = the title, the drum, and the release year of Phil Collins' *"In the Air Tonight."* The dread arrives like *that* drum: audible in advance, not yet dropped. |
| **6** | **The planetary verse** | *"and everyone I love is here / flying on this rock / no time left on the clock"* | Zooms all the way out. The intimate chorus and the whole planet turn out to be the same picture: one rock, one clock, one friend group. |

The chorus phrase recurs about five times; the *spinning / winning* couplet about four. It is a song that keeps coming home to the same line — which is, itself, the point.

## What it means

A few claims that cut across the whole thing.

**Communal presence, not nihilism.** The easy misread is "cheerful doom" — that the song just gives up. It doesn't. It neither *fixes* the apocalypse nor *denies* it; it asks a narrower, harder question — what relation is left when the end feels inevitable? — and answers: you go out *together,* not alone. Solidarity, not salvation-by-doctrine. And Roy backs exactly that reading by literally playing it in a room full of his friends. The chorus keeps a real double edge, though, and the song is honest about both blades: it's beautiful (love, chosen) *and* unsettling (an acceptance of the going-down). Hold both.

**The anti-clone.** The loneliness here is a strange one — it's loneliness inside *overpopulation,* not emptiness. Surrounded by people, the speaker feels *replicated* rather than connected: a clone in a crowded city. The chorus is the corrective. A crowd flattens you into a copy; friends restore your particularity. Same multiplicity, two opposite effects — and the switch is *relation.* The city sees a copy; friends see *you.* (That's the [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]] axis, run in purely social form: to be *witnessed* is to be made singular; to be merely *seen,* one of many.)

**Spin is not win — and the clock tells you why.** *"I can't stop spinning / I thought I was winning"* is rotation mistaken for arrival: motion that feels like progress and isn't. That's [[The Loop and the Circle|the Loop]] — spin without getting anywhere — not the liberating turn of [[The Circle]]. And the planetary verse quietly explains the pivot: *"no time left on the clock"* is a *game*-clock image, and winning only means anything while the game is still running. When the clock hits zero, success-logic just… stops applying. So the song abandons *win* for *be-with*: once the buzzer's about to sound, the only move that still makes sense is who you're standing next to.

**One rock, one clock.** *"Flying on this rock"* is what keeps this from being a mere scene-portrait. Earth *is* the vehicle; the species is a friend group on a single ship; the tiny, intimate chorus (*all my friends*) and the enormous planetary frame (one rock, one clock) are the same image at two scales. The tour video draws it exactly like that — a child's white-on-black sketch of the Earth ringed by little figures (and a few flying saucers). The apocalypse, held communally.

**Jenny: the dance traded for the answer.** She's the one named character, and she's a compression — embodiment (*dance,* open, living) swapped for certainty (*the answer,* closed, urgent, End-Times). The song neither mocks her nor trusts her. It doesn't mock her because she's naming the exact dread everyone in the song already feels; it doesn't trust her because it puts its faith in *being together* rather than in her answer. Her *"big red flag"* is doing double duty: the literal banner of a street-preacher, and the idiom — Jenny is herself the warning sign. (On release day Roy annotated her with a bare, cryptic post: *"Jenny / = / 408."* More on that in the notes; the meaning is genuinely open.)

## Characters

- **[[Roy|Jagwar Twin]]** — the first-person speaker, turning communal. Worth repeating because it dates the whole reading: 2023, *pre*-Lucius. No persona, no billing — just Roy and his friends and the end of the world.
- **Jenny** — the only named character: the former dancer who *"got the answer"* and now preaches the End Times, red flag in hand. Held as a portrait; her wider role is deliberately left **unfixed** — real friend, archetype, scene-sketch, composite? The song doesn't say, and the page won't pin her.
- **"all my friends"** — not really a character so much as the collective the song is *addressed to.* Per Roy, the song "is about all my friends," and it was "played in LA for the first time with all my friends." The title is the audience.

## Symbols

- **The clone** — loneliness inside the crowd; the crowd sees a copy, friends see you. The inverse of the clone is a friend.
- **The spin / the Loop** — *"can't stop spinning… thought I was winning":* rotation misread as progress. The Loop (no arrival), pointedly *not* [[The Circle]].
- **The "big red flag"** — double-coded: a preacher's literal banner *and* the modern idiom (Jenny as red flag).
- **The drum (1981)** — dread arriving as an old rhythm, through time; the suspended drop that never quite lands.
- **"Flying on this rock" / the planetary frame** — Earth as vehicle; intimate and cosmic at a single scale.
- **The clock** — a game-clock; success-logic that expires when time does, forcing the turn to *be-with.*

> [!tip] A title-string in the wild
> When the single caught Top-40 radio, a fan marked the moment with a sentence built entirely out of Jagwar Twin song titles: *"It's been a long time coming for all my friends, I just hope they have a good time and have a happy face because life is good and I like to party."* Six titles ([[Long Time Coming]] · **All My Friends** · [[Good Time (1-08)|Good Time]] · [[Happy Face]] · [[Life Is Good]] · [[I Like to Party]]) smuggled into one perfectly cheerful run-on — the catalogue congratulating itself in its own words.

## Connected songs

- **[[Life Is Good]]** — the direct sibling, six months later in 2023 (Nov 3, with @angelbaby). Both 2023 songs meet the world's end with *chosen togetherness,* and both carry the same beautiful-and-complicit double edge — the world's on fire, but at least we're having fun with the right people.
- **[[City of Angels]]** — where the clone eventually *arrives* (*"everyone looks just like you,"* 2026). All My Friends is the 2023 **clone-seed** — an architectural correspondence, flagged as such on the City of Angels page, *not* a claim that Roy in 2023 was aiming at a Lucius arc that didn't yet exist.
- **[[The Watchers]]** — the clone *deepening:* clone-*with-friends* (2023) becomes clone-*being-watched* (2024, *"I'm afraid I'm a clone"*). Here the fear is still communal; there it curdles into surveillance.
- **[[No Fake Friends]]** — the shrunk perimeter. *"Don't wanna go outside"* rhymes with N.F.F.'s defensive social filtering: when the world floods, the circle tightens to the trusted few.
- **[[Loser]]** — the clone-thread's other end. *"Everybody's all the same"* (2018) is the flattening the clone line names from the inside; the Master Source List ties the two together as the same crowd-erases-you fear, five years apart.
- **[[Move To You]]** · **[[I Like to Party]]** — the *drum* thread. Move To You is the **true drum** (*"I can hear your heart, it beats,"* 2018, rhythm as living pulse); I Like to Party is the **Piper's drum** (*"to the beat of my drum… follow me,"* 2021, rhythm as a summons to follow). All My Friends' *from-1981* dread-drum sits between them. (I Like to Party also shares this song's *don't-wanna-go-outside* withdrawal.) Unpacked in the drum-allegiance collapsible below.
- **[[Ballerina Boy]]** — a faint rhyme, held loosely: another dancer who *"used to be,"* the same loss-of-embodiment shape with a very different outcome.
- **[[Precious Time]]** — the expiring *clock* lightly echoes Precious Time's time-as-flood; kinship of theme across eras, not a callback.
- **[[The Loop and the Circle]]** · **[[Witnessing vs Surveillance]]** — the two frameworks the spin and the clone plug into.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the drum from 1981, and dread you inherit
> The 1981 allusion is *grounded* — three consecutive lines hand you the title, the drum, and the year of *"In the Air Tonight."* What the allusion *does* is the reading, and it's a good one. Phil Collins' track is culturally synonymous with unbearable, prolonged suspense — the whole song is a held breath waiting for that drum fill to finally break. So the speaker feels the societal "drop" approaching *like that drum:* inevitable, audible in advance, not yet landed. And by naming the *year,* not just the song, the dread becomes **inherited** — an old rhythm arriving now, a signal traveling through time. That's the same instinct behind the catalogue's credited interpolations (the way older songs get built into newer ones as substrate): dread as something handed down, not generated fresh. Here it's allusion rather than a cleared sample — but the [[Inherited Substrate|inherited-substrate]] habit is the same.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the drum as allegiance (a cross-song thread)
> Pulled a little wider, "coming like a drum from 1981" sits in the middle of a small motif about *whose beat you move to.* On one side, a **true** drum — *"I can hear your heart, it beats"* ([[Move To You]], 2018), rhythm as living pulse. On the other, the **Piper's** drum — *"to the beat of my drum… follow me"* ([[I Like to Party]], 2021), rhythm as a summons to follow. And in the middle, this **inherited dread**-drum. The point isn't that the word "drum" recurs — that's the cheap kind of evidence — it's that *rhythm is allegiance:* whose beat you march to is whose creature you become. (I Like to Party also shares this song's *don't-wanna-go-outside* withdrawal, which is why they keep turning up next to each other.)

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the clone architecture, and the honest doubt about it
> The clone/Loop/Witnessing readings connect this single into a much larger web — clone → surveillance → City of Angels — and it's worth being upfront that this is exactly the kind of pattern the vault is *built* to find, so here's the counter-pressure, kept in view:
> - *"Clone in a crowded city"* is common modern-alienation language. Reading it as a node in a clone-architecture is an interpretive move, not a given — which is why the City-of-Angels link is tagged **retrospective seed, NOT 2023 intent,** and the tag is meant to govern.
> - **Jenny is a character before she's a rhyme.** Most plainly she's a specific friend who's gone preachy about the apocalypse — not primarily a Ballerina-Boy echo. Hold the resonance loosely; don't promote a one-off portrait into a recurring figure on the strength of a nice rhyme.
> - **The falsifier, kept verbatim:** *if "All My Friends" is simply the sincere apocalypse-with-friends song Roy describes (co-written with Colin Brittain — emergent), the clone-architecture / Loop / Witnessing readings are overlay.* That's the honest gate. The plain reading stands on its own; the cosmology is the layer you can lose without breaking the song.

## Source notes

- **Grounded (released music, public posts, official video).** The release date and label (May 26 2023, Big Loud) and the writer/producer credits (Roy English & Colin Brittain; produced by Brittain) are Spotify-verified. The apocalyptic frame, the clone line, the withdrawal, the spin/win couplet, the chorus, the Jenny portrait, the 1981 lines, and the planetary verse are all grounded lyric. The paratext is public and load-bearing: Roy's pre-release *"It's about all my friends"* (05-24) and *"This song is about all my friends"* (06-09); the day-after *"played it in LA for the first time with all my friends"* (05-27); the release-day *"Jenny / = / 408"* (05-26); and the standalone-single, no-album placement (post-*33*, sequenced between *Good Time (1:08)* and *Life Is Good*).
- **The tour video (official).** A white-on-black, child-drawing-style animation — Earth ringed by figures and flying saucers — released the same day on [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w9U4M4qrUjo). Filmed on tour by @_corymeetsworld; edited by @RyanMFleming and @_corymeetsworld. Note on the saucers: they literalize the *planetary-communal* frame (end-times *on* Earth), and read that way — an *alien / "we-come-from-space"* interpretation is a stretch the song doesn't support.
- **Strong readings.** Communal-presence-not-nihilism; the anti-clone (loneliness inside overpopulation, friends as the corrective); spin-as-Loop vs the Circle; the game-clock forcing the pivot from *win* to *be-with*; the one-rock-one-clock scaling; Jenny as dance-traded-for-the-answer; the double-coded red flag; the 1981 drum as the suspended, inherited drop; Life Is Good as sibling. Grounded in the material, but interpretive — and the plainest reading (a warm apocalypse-with-friends anthem) is granted, with the cosmology layered on top.
- **Open / held loosely.** *"Jenny = 408"* — real, release-dated, meaning **unpinned**; candidate decodes (all speculative, none asserted): the "Jenny"-as-a-number trope (à la Tommy Tutone's *867-5309*), an area code (408 = San Jose / South Bay), or a timestamp echo of *Good Time (1:08)*. Who Jenny "really" is (person / archetype / composite). And whether the end-times register is sincere dread, irony, or affection — the lyric supports all three at once, so the blend is what's held.
- **How we'd be wrong.** See the falsifier in the collapsible above: if the sincere, co-written apocalypse-with-friends song *is* the whole song, the clone/Loop/Witnessing architecture is overlay. That gate stays on the table.

# Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)

> **One-line reading.** Roy's own gloss: *"a chocolate covered reminder to trust your gut."* The cheerful Wonka jingle is weaponized into a warning — a manufactured paradise insisting everything's perfect while the dread leaks through, and the body's alarm treated as more honest than the official story.

Where [[Great Time to Be Human]] forces horror *through* a smile, this is the smile that can't suppress the dread underneath. It is the [[The Circus|Circus]] in nursery form — the candy factory as a model of the spectacle machine.

**Release:** *Lucius Lullaby* (2026), track 5 — originally a single, December 15, 2023, timed to the *Wonka* film; an acoustic-live version closes the album as track 15 · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

Roy gave the song its own thesis, and it's worth taking literally: a chocolate-covered reminder to trust your gut. The sing-song bounce, the nursery familiarity, the Oompa-Loompa hook — those are the chocolate. The dread is the medicine inside it. The song's pleasure is camouflage for its warning, which is itself the point: this is how a candy factory gets you to swallow things. It rhymes with Roy's "Trust your guts" tattoo — the gut as the reliable instrument when the official story has gone false.

The interpolation is exact and load-bearing. In *Willy Wonka & the Chocolate Factory* (1971), the Oompa-Loompas are the moralizing chorus that steps forward after each spoiled child is removed and sings the lesson. Bad Feeling keeps the tune and the address and swaps one phrase: *"a perfect puzzle for you"* becomes *"a bad feeling 'bout you."* That moves the cautionary chorus from *after* the collapse to *before* it. The question is no longer "what did this child do wrong?" but "why does this perfect place feel wrong?" The lesson-after-the-fact becomes a smoke alarm.

The paradise-language is enforcement, not reassurance: *"they say everything's perfect here / the sun is shining / nobody's crying."* The parenthetical *"(they say)"* is the whole tell — the perfection is the system's claim, not the singer's. And *"nobody's crying"* does sinister work: in a manufactured paradise, it isn't an observation that no one is hurt; it's a spec that crying has been hidden or forbidden. By the second verse the appetite is everywhere — *"carnivores and cannibals, I want it on demand-able / mandible, jaw split… gossip"* — the attention economy rendered as a mouth, people consumed as content. Even the dancing is a fall: *"dance 'til we drop."*

## Its place in the mythology

The song's deepest knot is in its release history. In *The Interview EP 1*, [[Sir Lucius]] claims this track outright — *"happy face, bad feeling… those are me. You need me."* So the warning comes from the shadow's own mouth. That doesn't falsify it, but it complicates it perfectly, because the song dropped as a single on the same day as the 2023 *Wonka* film — a genuine "trust your gut" alarm that was *itself* a precisely-timed piece of nostalgia-bait content. The song exposes the candy factory while being a strategically released candy-factory product. It is the threat-detector and the factory foreman in one — which may be the truest thing it knows about Lucius: the part of you warning you about the machine is also running it.

The chorus phrase even became a physical artwork. *"Everything's Perfect Here"* was the title of Roy's first gallery show and pop-up (Lauren Powell Projects, Los Angeles, opened January 2024), built around a glowing orange sculpture — a staged "perfect" space you walk into, two years before the album track. The line is load-bearing across media, and the gallery framing reinforces the reading: the "perfect here" is an installation, a manufactured environment, not a description.

## Notes

When ordinary truth-testing fails — when *"the truth is a lie that nobody can tell"* — the song proposes a fallback: the gut. The bad feeling becomes the last honest instrument, the one reading the candy hasn't coated. That intuition-as-counter-epistemology is the heart of it, and it's anchored by Roy's own statement.

The lab notes keep some readings as resonance only. The grimmest hides in the source material: the Oompa-Loompas are themselves consumed labor — imported workers paid in cacao beans, even used as test subjects. The chorus that sings the warning is itself the factory's product; the crowd that sings is the crowd that gets eaten. A corrupted "That's Amore" echo (*"when the moon hits the sky, I'm a little unwell"*) turns moon-romance into lunacy. And the candy paradise reads, in a Gnostic key, as a false Eden or Maya — a closed system of tests and punishments. These framework imports are kept deliberately light: Roy's own gloss is modest and personal, and the systems-critique is the vault's reading layered on top. One honest possibility the page concedes: the whole candy-Wonka aesthetic may be primarily a 2023 film tie-in marketing hook, which would reframe the spectacle-machine depth as commercially occasioned dressing.

One boundary worth keeping: the teeth, the no-exit maze, and *"chew you up, spit you out"* belong to [[Welcome to the Circus]], the adjacent track — not to this song.

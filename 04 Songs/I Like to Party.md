# I Like to Party

> **One-line reading.** An exuberant party anthem about people who never leave the house — they "stay in and pretend that we're in the club" — staged as the attention-force operating a crowd, and interrupted at the end by a competing broadcast: *the kingdom is within you*, which the program cuts off mid-word.

**Release:** *33* (2022) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The hook is a contradiction. It sounds like a celebration, but it celebrates staying home: *"we don't go outside, we stay in and pretend that we're in the club."* The gathering is simulated and indoors.

The narrator issues commands and claims ownership — *"follow me," "sing along with me," "lock up your door," "get on the floor,"* counted off *"one, two, three, four,"* and most tellingly *"give me attention, attention, the people are mine."* He offers *"a new direction, affecting the state of your mind."* Around this runs a thread of manufactured media: *"Television on the news," "Devil's Food is a cake," "we don't want 'em awake," "bake a truth with a lie, then we sell 'em the cake."* The crowd moves *"like chickens with no head, in circles, they go."*

Then the bridge stages two opposite awakenings at once: *"Devil waking up to claim the kingdom / People waking up to claim what's in them,"* resolving toward *"Heaven is within your heart, you're the kingdom." But the inner one is cut off mid-word — *"you're the King—"* breaks into *"we interrupt our program to bring you this important message,"* which returns to the chorus. The distraction-system gets the last word.

## Its place in the mythology

The narrator reads as the attention-force introduced on the previous track operating a crowd — *"the people are mine"* echoes that song's acquisitive "I." It is the Pied Piper structure made literal: counting and call-and-response herd a following by an irresistible beat, synchronized into motion rather than invited to think. Mass culture becomes a kind of secular ritual — clapping, chanting, counting — with an officiant who finally names himself.

The bridge's interrupt is the key event. The kingdom-within message surfaces and the broadcast cuts it off, so the song's logic of capture reasserts over the inner-sovereignty message it briefly let through. That inner phrase also carries a borrowed transmission: *"the kingdom of God is within man,"* the line from Chaplin's *Great Dictator* speech that Jagwar Twin sampled back in 2018, here re-voiced as Roy's own lyric and carried into the *33* media-machine. So *"we interrupt our program"* is, in spirit, that humanist counter-broadcast breaking into the feed — even as the feed silences it.

The song dramatizes the mechanism Lucius later personifies — spectacle as containment, false sweetness, awakening-language converted to content — before he is named or credited here. Coded, not voiced.

## Notes

The official video frames the song honestly. Its description warns the party "may be dangerous and could result in… loss of identity, the creation of a self created hell, etc. Bring your friends; Let's party!" The "party" is identity-dissolution staged as fun — the artist's own framing of his lyric.

The "Television" pun is grounded paratext: the band's shop sells a "Tell-A-Vision Tee" — a vintage TV with a face on the screen reading TELL-A-VISION — which confirms the device that *tells you a vision* as intentional language. Underneath sits the classical critique of bread and circuses, and Marie-Antoinette's "let them eat cake," made literal in dessert (Devil's Food; the cake of truth folded into a lie). The pandemic-era imagery — "Zoom everyday," "don't go outside," "lock up your door" — reads as lockdown isolation, the "club" as the simulated gathering of the Zoom era. The song works perfectly well as a straight ironic stay-home party banger; the attention-force and kingdom-within layer is the deeper read, not something the lyric declares.

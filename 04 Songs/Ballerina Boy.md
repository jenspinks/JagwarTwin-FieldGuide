# Ballerina Boy

> **One-line reading.** The wound-song and the origin chamber — the buried child saying *come back for me*, and the moment the world shows why [[Sir Lucius]] had to exist.

**Release:** standalone single, June 13, 2025 (Big Loud Rock, under exclusive license to Mercury Records / UMG) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

Like LOOP, this was kept off [[Lucius Lullaby (album)]] despite the pre-album window — and the absence is part of the meaning: the off-album single about the off-album child, kept out of the spectacle it diagnoses.

## What happens in the song

Roy released this story himself; it's literal autobiography, told in sequence. Age seven, a Christian school, ballet — *"I came alive when I was on that stage."* A teacher tells his father *"we think your son is gay"*; the boy overhears, reasons that *"gay must mean bad,"* prays *"to make me good."* The girls in his class surround him: *"you're not a real boy."* His father answers, *"there's nothing wrong with you, my son."*

Then the burial: *"I killed that ballerina boy / I buried him inside / It's like I didn't have a choice."* The grave is dug at seven, and everything after grows out of it.

The structure is the argument. Verse two jumps forward to age seventeen and the music industry — and the same chant survives the leap intact: *"you're not a real boy"* becomes *"music just wasn't landing."* Same mechanism across schoolyard, religion, label, and audience: sacrifice the true self so the outer world stops punishing you.

The song's hinge is its fork: *"Do I kill myself or come back for that kid I let go."* Roy doesn't ask *do I become successful* — he asks *do I come back for that kid.* The answer he chooses is integration, not achievement: *"This song's for Ballerina Boy / a prayer for hope."* It goes inward first, the exact inverse of a bargain song that says win the outer game and maybe feel later.

It ends in a spoken blessing, delivered by an older, motherly female voice: *"You are one of a kind, God's design… so my son, let your light shine… on those dark nights look up to the sky. The moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light. There's no one else like you out there."*

## Its place in the mythology

This is most likely Lucius's origin wound. Lucius is not the ballerina boy — he's what grows around the grave. The ballerina boy is softness, embodiment, play, stage-joy, undefended creativity. When that is buried as unsurvivable, the psyche still needs to live, so it builds armor: strategy, performance, control, mockery before being mocked, power before vulnerability. That armor is Lucius, the protector formed at the exact moment the child went into the ground.

Which is why Lucius mocks this specific song in the visual lore. In one episode of the video series he mimes a ballet move and says, *"Do you know what your lowest performing song of all time is? Ballerina Boy. When you're vulnerable you LOSE!"* That isn't random cruelty; it's self-defense. If Roy rescues the buried child, Lucius loses his monopoly on protection. The shadow has a stake in keeping the grave closed.

The song answers the world's hostility with blessing rather than armor, and it does so in two distinct voices. The earthly father's, narrated inside the story — *"there's nothing wrong with you, my son"* — is stated in the negative: a pardon that still operates inside the same right/wrong court the teacher and the girls used. It acquits the charge without leaving the courtroom. The motherly outro is generative instead — *"you are one of a kind, God's design… let your light shine"* — claiming the child's nature instead of answering an accusation. Both parental voices protect by blessing; Lucius protects by armoring. The whole resolution lives in that difference — and the deepest blessing arriving in the feminine voice, exactly where shame tried to erase him, is the song's quiet thesis.

## Characters

- [[Roy|Jagwar Twin]] — first-person, undefended, no mask. This is the most directly autobiographical song in the body of work.
- [[Sir Lucius]] — never named in the lyric, but present as the figure who grows from this burial and who later mocks the song to defend his own necessity.
- [[Mary]] — the motherly outro voice. This is most likely the clearest divine-feminine appearance in the catalogue: the same faceless feminine blessing recurs in [[Weirdness]] (*"you were born to shine"*) and in [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] (where Mary is named outright).

## Symbols

- [[The Moon]] — *"the moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light."* The moon doesn't generate light; it reflects it. This reframes Lucius, who is strongly lunar: not simply darkness, but reflected light under distortion. Lit by fear and shame he reflects those; lit by source-light he could become reminder and witness. He is the moon trying to become a sun — his tragedy, and the reason this song stands as a correction to any flat anti-Lucius reading.
- [[The Mask]] — the survival strategy installed by the wound, before it had a face.

## Notes

The simplest reading is a sincere trauma-confessional that needs no mythology, and that reading is valid and sufficient on its own — its value isn't in the system it can be slotted into, which is the healthy version of the song's own thesis. The Lucius-genesis reading is supported inference, not free-floating: that the burial *produced* Lucius is interpretation the lyric never states, but that Lucius is *invested* in this grave is grounded — he targets the song by name in the video series. The lunar and Gnostic readings are esoteric correspondence, attributed as such, not claims of authorial intent. There's also an open question the song presses on itself: it states burial outright, yet the adult is demonstrably a stage creature, and *"come back for that kid I let go"* admits the burial was incomplete — so the child may have been misidentified (kept alive as performance, denied recognition as that child) rather than fully buried. A legitimate question, held open rather than asserted.

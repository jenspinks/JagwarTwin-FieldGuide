# The Watchers

> **One-line reading.** The moment the hall of mirrors grows eyes — reflection escalating into surveillance, the self replicated, consumed, and watched until it can't tell whether it's awake, dreaming, or already a clone of itself.

**Release:** standalone single / three-song EP, October 4, 2024 (Big Loud Records) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

Writers: Roy English, Ryan Daly, Ryan Spraker, and Rockwell. The song interpolates Rockwell's *"Somebody's Watching Me"* (1984), credited.

## What happens in the song

The hook is the interpolation — *"(I always feel like somebody's watching me)"* — set against a fascinated, intoxicated, dancing crowd. The load-bearing line turns that watching inward: *"I'm afraid I'm a clone / I think I might be… on my way to my own demise."* Where [[City of Angels]] externalizes the replication horror (*everyone looks just like you*), The Watchers internalizes it: *maybe I'm the copy.*

It's a surveillance-dream. The speaker keeps asking *"is it just a dream?"* and gets answered by a hammered *"wake up"* — but it's an unstable, half-awake state, where you know the dream is compromised but not whether the watchers are outside you, inside you, or both. A scapegoat passage runs underneath: *"you betrayed me / tried to frame me… tried to make me out to be the bad guy."* And a consumption thread — *"now they want candy… but I'm not Mandy Moore"* — refuses to be the wholesome, manufactured pop-commodity: to be consumed sweetly is to be cloned.

The form is the subject. The credited **theremin** is the canonical sound of paranoia and sci-fi surveillance — and it's played without touch, controlled at a distance by a hovering hand. Roy then posted to his Instagram Story a page of Jung (*Collected Works* Vol. 18, "The Significance of Dreams") — the signs-versus-symbols passage — cropped past legibility, centered on Horus and the cross, and scored to this song. The image says *symbol*; the audio says *watched*; and the unreadable crop makes us the watchers, trying to decode him.

## Its place in the mythology

The keeper frame is the El Roi axis. The Watchers is the catalogue's purest surveillance song, and it falls on the artist whose name is El Roi — Genesis 16:13, *"the God who sees me,"* inked in Hebrew on [[Sir Lucius]]'s throat. The project distinguishes two ways of being seen. Witnessing recognizes and restores: you are transformed by being seen. Surveillance predicts and ranks: you are shaped, not known. The machine that watches everything cannot witness anyone. Read against that, *"I'm afraid I'm a clone"* is the precise output of surveillance — the self shaped into a copy rather than witnessed into itself — sung by the one whose name promises the opposite gaze.

The keeper line even resolves into Hebrew: in the El Roi root, the same letters give *mirror*, *seeing*, and *eye* — so *"the hall of mirrors grows eyes"* is the word itself unfolding. Which is why the Jung-Horus pairing lands: Horus's eye is surveillance, El Roi is witness, and The Watchers is the space between them.

The choice of Rockwell goes deeper than a hook. Rockwell was Kennedy William Gordy, Berry Gordy's son, who shunned the Motown name (a fake stage name, a fake British accent) to escape being heard as a nepo-copy of his father — so the author of *"somebody's watching me"* lived the clone-of-the-father fear Roy's lyric names outright. He wrote the song from a literal prayer, on his knees, vowing to testify — so the surveillance-and-watching-God braid is latent in the source, not imposed. And Michael Jackson sang the original's hook uncredited: a voice everywhere on the record, name nowhere on it — structurally the Roy/Lucius problem (whose voice is this?) and the clone-anxiety in one image. When Roy reaches for *"dancing like Michael Jackson"* over a Rockwell interpolation, he invokes that hidden ghost-voice on purpose. The Rockwell saga is, at bottom, about who gets to claim a song — the father's name hidden, MJ uncredited — which is the engine of the whole Lucius system: a real voice whose authorship is contested, hidden, or stolen.

## Notes

The interpolation is grounded; that Roy chose the song *for* the Rockwell parallels is a strong, overdetermined inference rather than stated intent. The biographical facts are all grounded as facts about Rockwell. Speculatively, the title gestures at the Watchers of the Enochic tradition (Daniel's *"a watcher, a holy one"*; the angels who observed humanity and fell among it) — ambiguous between the holy watchers and the fallen ones — and the Jung-Horus pairing tilts the song toward the holy witness: Ezekiel's living creatures *"full of eyes,"* the seeing that is sacred rather than only surveillant. The "wake up" sits on Roy's publicly posted Gurdjieff (*"man is asleep… he must wake up"*).

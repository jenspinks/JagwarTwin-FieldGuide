# Shine

> **One-line reading.** The dawn after the deluge — the album's closer answers a rigged, drowning world not with defensive momentum but with a sun-drenched insistence on faith, agency, and chosen light.

**Release:** *Subject to Flooding* (2018), track 13 — the album closer; later a 2020 "Live In Studio" single  ·  **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The speaker wakes to an overwhelming news cycle — *"wake up, look at my phone and read the news / it's bad, really bad… the world is goin crazy"* — and names a systemic trap: *"they run the house and rig the game to get the upper hand."* The album's flood arrives directly: *"it's like an earthquake under your feet / but you're in the sea so you're gonna drown,"* with *"everyone's against you, from your family to your country."* Against all of it, the chorus chooses agency and inclusivity — *"we're all the same in every nation,"* *"when life was not a celebration, I had dreams and then I made 'em"* — and resolves into dawn: *"I was in the clouds, now the sun is coming out singing… everywhere I go, I'm gonna shine."*

## Its place in the mythology

Where the album works through its defenses for outrunning the coming deluge — shame-preemption in [[Loser]], outlaw velocity in [[Hell Of A Night]] and [[Hell of a Night Pt. 2 (The Hallelujah Stickup)]] — *Shine* is the morning after the storm. It names the flood directly (*you're in the sea so you're gonna drown*) but stands its ground rather than runs. The album opens in reflection — the mirror of [[Long Time Coming]] — and closes in radiance: the self no longer studying reflected light but trying to *become* it (see [[Source vs Reflection]]).

The joy is earned, not ignorant. The speaker explicitly names the violence, the rigged game, the drowning, and chooses to shine anyway — and Roy has said exactly this. He wrote it watching the war film *Troy*: *"a lot of war and killing… so I wanted to write a chorus that felt happy, like the sun rising,"* with the thesis that *"even in dark times, there is always light if you want to find it."* That is chosen light, not blind optimism — the same decision-not-denial move the catalogue runs again later, with *Shine* as its 2018 antecedent.

The song also plants a vocabulary the catalogue will reuse. Here the verb is first-person and communal — the self *claims* the light and invites the crowd to *"sing it if you know you're gonna shine."* Years later the same word returns inverted into a blessing, the divine-feminine voice *conferring* it on the wounded other (*"you were born to shine"*). *Shine* seeds the light-bearing vocabulary at the very start of the project; the later voice redirects it from claim to blessing.

## Notes

One lyric carries a documented provider error: the line is *"gender of my generation"* (confirmed by Spotify and by ear), but Apple Music and AZLyrics mis-display that single word as a celebrity surname — the correct word is what carries the universal-identity reading. As a framework resonance, *Shine* is the project's solar bookend: *Subject to Flooding* ends on the rising sun; the 2026 album ends on its own solar note, the sun declared outward here and withheld to wordless breath there. A fair caution: "shine" is among pop's most ubiquitous inspirational verbs, so the lineage into later blessings is best held as a thread rather than a proven design — and Roy's own stated frame (a sunrise chorus, chosen light) is the floor under everything else.

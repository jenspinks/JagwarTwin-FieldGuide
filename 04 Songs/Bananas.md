# Bananas

> **One-line reading.** The album's id-permission chant — Lucius takes a children's-entertainer's voice and uses it to license animal regression, destructive appetite, and dissociated shadow-action. A banana peel under the conscience: funny until you realize the fall is the point.

**Release:** Lucius Lullaby (2026) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

Bananas is what [[Moths to the Flame]] makes possible. The spoken-word doctrine of the previous track — regress to the animal, shrug at the cull — runs seamlessly into this sung id-eruption. Moths is the syllabus; Bananas is the enactment.

## What happens in the song

The song is built as permission. It addresses "kids" directly, asks whether they ever want to break things or get wasted, and answers for them: *well, alright*. That is the whole engine — not a tantrum expressed but a tantrum *authorized*, by an adult voice in a sing-song register, to a crowd it keeps calling kids. What it licenses arrives in stages: invitation (break things, get wasted), then animalization (*just the monkeys feeling funky*), then a moral split in kindergarten language (*I wanna be good, but it's fun to be bad*), then dissociation (*I hear voices in my mind I don't remember — did I do that?*). The chant walks the listener down from person to monkey to a self that no longer remembers its own acts. Even the fire is domestic — *light up the stove and burn it down*, the hearth that should nourish turned into ignition.

## Its place in the mythology

In an album billed for "hands that rock the cradle," this is the cradle-rocker teaching the nursery to burn the house down — the Pied Piper, not the confessor. The split arrives stripped to its crudest fracture: *good, but fun to be bad; nice, but I always get mad* — the Roy/Lucius duality before it becomes strategy. The honest part is that "fun to be bad" isn't a lie; repression makes forbidden energy feel alive. The dangerous part is what's missing: this vitality is released without heart, memory, or responsibility. A private primal scream can heal. A ringmaster teaching children to enjoy it cannot. The most chilling turn is the disavowal — *did I do that?* — the self split from the part that acts. Integration would say *yes, that was me; what is that part trying to tell me?* Lucius's version stops at the shrug and keeps the party going.

The deeper frame comes from the staging. Across the premiere series the set holds a small altar — a fruit bowl of green apples and bananas, two red candles, an Egyptian statue beneath. Lucius keeps lifting a banana and playing his songs off it, as if it were a microphone: in one episode a browning banana becomes [[Not Your Homie]], in another it becomes [[Welcome to the Circus]]. Read that as what it looks like — an offering meant for the altar's hidden feminine ([[Mary]]) taken down and used as Lucius's PA system, the sacred gift rerouted to broadcast his own voice, then set back as if untouched. The altar still stands; inside, it's been repurposed.

And there is a reason the banana, specifically, is his medium — and here Roy says so himself. He has stated publicly that the banana was chosen because it is a fruit that is sterile — flawed genes, inherited trauma carried forward — but looks healthy and good. The botany backs him: the supermarket banana, the Cavendish, is a sterile clone, genetically identical, unable to reproduce on its own, propagated only by human cutting, and now being wiped out worldwide by a single fungus *precisely because* every plant is the same. That is Lucius's Loop made fruit — recurrence without variation, real light in a sterile vessel: you can hear the song, you cannot inherit it. It rhymes exactly with City of Angels' "everyone looks just like you." See [[The Banana]].

The id blows precisely because the energy is sealed in — *I'm ready to blow, I got nowhere to go*. But Roy's own public caption for the song reframes the same word: *nowhere is not a destination, but you can arrive in an instant*. So the song sets Lucius's trapped nowhere against Roy's transcendent nowhere — one a dead end, one a doorway, the same syllable. That keeps Bananas from being mere cartoon nihilism: it is the symptom of a sealed system, the explosion a culture manufactures by forbidding honest feeling. Heard in the album's logic — pretend you're fine ([[Happy Face]]), the body knows ([[Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)]]), the repressed self finally blows through the mask (Bananas), the machine sells the blowup back as a show ([[Welcome to the Circus]]) — this is the tantrum the system was built to produce and then monetize.

## Characters

**Lucius** is the host and inciter — the cradle-rocker handing out permission. **Roy** is the wound underneath: the genuine fracture (good/bad, the trapped energy) that Lucius is hosting and exploiting rather than healing. The speaker is probably all three at once — Lucius inciting, Roy's unleashed id erupting, and the horror of the split being witnessed.

## Notes

The animalization has a named source: Roy has publicly posted Gurdjieff on exactly this in-between creature — *man is no longer a wild animal, but he has not yet become a human being* — so "the monkeys feeling funky" reads as regression in his own framing rather than an imposed one. The disavowal *did I do that?* echoes a sitcom catchphrase (Steve Urkel's), the innocent childhood alibi recoded into a moral one — a light echo that depends on how the line is sung. The flatter reading is fair: "hey kids, do you want to break things? well, alright" is a common transgressive-fun register, and the hijacked-offering theology leans heavily on theatrical props (a banana used as a mic is, at surface, a gag). Roy's gnomic "nowhere" caption supports many readings, not only the trapped-versus-transcendent one.

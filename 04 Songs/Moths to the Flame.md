# Moths to the Flame

> **One-line reading.** Lucius's spoken-word anti-gospel — it takes one of the oldest images of the soul's ascent, the moth flying into the flame, and debases it into a population cull. It is the doctrine; the song it runs into, [[Bananas]], is the practice.

**Release:** Lucius Lullaby (2026) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

This is a short spoken-word piece in Lucius's cold "power mode" — the on-album cousin of his premiere-series speeches. It detonates seamlessly into the next track: the backing music becomes Bananas, and the video pulls back from the speech to reveal Lucius standing atop a pile of bananas as the next song begins. Moths is the syllabus; Bananas is the enactment.

## What happens in the song

The text is brief and total. Civilization, Lucius says, has been a mistake; it would be better to leave everything alone, because the wild animals are wiser — they just follow their instincts. And if a moth mistakes a flame for a mating call and flies into it, so what? It just keeps the moth population down. Two moves: *regress* — be the wild animal, drop the human labor — and *shrug at death* — the cull is fine, even useful. The crowd's yearning for connection gets reframed as a biological flaw to be exploited.

The video gives the doctrine a liturgy. Lucius holds a candelabra with three slots but only one tall red candle in the center — a triad collapsed to a single flame. He lights it from his cigarette — fire as addiction and consumption — delivers the sermon, and at the end blows it out himself. The keeper of the light choosing the dark, on camera, a breath before the next song's "go bananas."

## Its place in the mythology

The catalogue is built on a death — but a specific kind. The phoenix burns and dies in order to be reborn *through* the death; that is transformation, the [[The Loop and the Circle|Circle]]. [[City of Angels]] already named one counterfeit of it: "never gonna die," the upload, which skips the burning — the same self preserved and multiplied, resurrection downgraded to taxidermy. The moth is the second counterfeit. It *does* fly into the fire — but toward a false light, having mistaken incineration for connection. So it gets the death without the rebirth. Two ways to miss the phoenix: refuse the burning, or burn for nothing. Lucius is not reaching for a stray mystical image; he is offering the counterfeit of the very death the whole catalogue is built on — the flame that consumes against the fire that transfigures. See [[Inversion]].

And there is a reason the image is a *light that draws the eye*. A candle pulls the moth in; the moth flies toward it to be near it, to be seen by it. On the logic of the [[The Upload]] — attention given a face becomes the eye, the feed, the watching field — the flame is the spectacle's gaze, and flying into it is entering the feed to be seen. *It just keeps the moth population down* becomes the attention economy as a cull: the crowd flies into the light to be witnessed and is consumed for the trying. The single candle, the three slots reduced to one, reads as the lampstand or the tree's three pillars collapsed to an isolated ego — a sacred apparatus lit from something profane and then snuffed out, the demiurge's whole gesture in one image.

Underneath the doctrine is a precise reversal of a source Roy uses in his own voice. He has publicly posted Gurdjieff on the in-between creature — *man is no longer a wild animal, but he has not yet become a human being* — whose lesson is *wake up, labor to become human*. Lucius preaches the exact opposite: the wild animals are wiser, regress to the animal, abandon the labor. Same line, turned inside out and made into a creed. The cruelty is delivered cheerfully, as if it were simply the natural order — the same engine as [[Great Time to Be Human]].

## Characters

This is **Lucius** in pure power mode — cold, prophetic, invulnerable, the doctrine stated as flat fact. **Roy** is present only in the background vocals. The crowd is the moths: [[The Audience]] reframed as a flaw to be harvested.

## Notes

In framework terms the moth-and-flame is, in the Persian mystics, the soul's *fanā* — ego-death, union with the divine — and Lucius takes that union-image and debases it into a suicidal-animal shrug, a systematic inversion of esoteric ascent. The "civilization has been a mistake" line participates in a long current — Rousseau's noble savage, Freud's *Civilization and Its Discontents*, deep ecology — which Lucius adopts and sours. Both are held as resonance; the flatter reading, a villain's brief nihilist interlude rather than a triple-counterfeit cosmology, is honest, and several of the iconographic details (the three-to-one candelabra, whether blowing the candle out *triggers* the closing vocalization) stay open questions rather than settled claims.

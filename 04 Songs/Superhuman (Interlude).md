# Superhuman (Interlude)

> **One-line reading.** Not Roy speaking doctrine but Roy *selecting* it — a 22-second relay of Charlie Chaplin's *Great Dictator* speech, embedded into the album so its borrowed humanism opens the machine-statement that follows.

**Release:** *Subject to Flooding* (2018), track 5 — ~22 seconds, spoken; precedes [[Superhuman]]  ·  **Source speech:** Charlie Chaplin, *The Great Dictator* (1940)

## What happens in the song

The interlude is a verbatim sample of the closing speech from Chaplin's *The Great Dictator* — confirmed by the Apple Music composer field, which credits Charles Chaplin alongside the album's writers. The lines run: *"The kingdom of God is within man… you, the people, have the power — the power to create machines, the power to create happiness… to make this life free and beautiful… let us all unite."*

## Its place in the mythology

The power of the moment is the act of selection. Roy didn't write these words; he chose them — from the whole archive of recorded speech, *this* 1940 humanist transmission to open the album's machine-statement. That makes the interlude a relay rather than an authored claim, and it places it squarely in the catalogue's transmission cluster: the voice as channel, the song as conduit, inherited material recontextualized inside an evolving mythos (see [[Inherited Substrate]]).

Its thematic anchor is the album's clearest statement that **the machine is not the enemy.** *"The power to create machines"* sits directly beside *"the power to create happiness"* — both framed as human creative capacity, gifts of "the people," not fallen forces. That originally anti-fascist line has since acquired a retrospective charge, colliding with the later catalogue's anxieties about algorithms, platforms, and engineered identity. The problem the catalogue eventually names is not machinery but the severing of machines from the heart and their capture by optimization logic. The interlude states the human pole before that severing gets dramatized — see [[Machine vs Human]].

There is a quieter resonance, too. Chaplin is one of the world's most recognizable clown figures, and here a clown speaks *against* mechanization and dehumanization — a kind of historical inverse of the later [[The Circus]], where the clown archetype is captured by spectacle. Where the Ringmaster synchronizes the crowd, the Chaplin transmission tries to wake it back into agency. And *"the kingdom of God is within man / not in one man / nor a group of men / but in all men"* decentralizes the sacred — divinity distributed across all, concentrated in no single figure — a counterpoint to the centralized, managerial intelligence the catalogue later associates with [[Sir Lucius]].

## Characters

The voice is Chaplin's, sampled — neither [[Roy]] nor [[Sir Lucius]] speaks. An inherited voice placed into the sequence.

## Symbols

- The machine — named here as human capacity, the album's clearest statement that it is not the antagonist

## Notes

A useful caution: this is one of the most-sampled speeches in popular music, used by countless artists as an inspirational gut-punch, so its placement may be partly convention. Honor it as a curated inherited transmission without over-loading the *choice* with a precise cosmology. In framework terms it functions like what Gurdjieff called "objective art" — using an inherited cultural transmission to shift the listener's perception — though that describes the effect, not a documented intent.

# Not Your Homie

> **One-line reading.** The song disguises itself as the shallowest thing on the album — aggressive, meme-shaped, deliberately dumb — and is one of the most important: the shadow, credited as a co-maker, refusing false reconciliation and proving that the algorithm rewards caricature over sincerity. The shallowness is the argument.

This is the other half speaking openly. Where the rest of the album debates what to do with [[Sir Lucius]], here he simply takes the mic and demonstrates his thesis: vulnerability loses, spectacle wins, meaning is optional. And on his own metrics, he's right — the song went viral.

**Release:** *Lucius Lullaby* (2026), track 11 — pre-album single, October 31, 2025 · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The track is built like a hypnotic construct — a memetic spell. It runs on playground rhymes and taunts: *"ringing rosies, my pocket's full of posies… ashes, ashes, we all fall down"* ("Ring a Ring o' Roses"), nursery recursion, viral-hook repetition. Lucius makes himself contagious — *"coming in like a plague from the dark side"* — literalizing the rhyme: shadow gone memetic, the thing that spreads is unconsciousness itself.

The most repeated line is the refusal: *"you don't know me."* On the surface it's an attack on parasociality — *you consume me, you project onto me, but you do not know me* — and the "homie" identity is just one more mask, stripped off alongside the others. The cartoon violence (*"John Wick with my pencil / stick it in your eye"*) and the reach for power (*"might run for president, I'm so presidential"*) distill Lucius's signature: charm and threat in one breath, where *"love my life"* becomes *"end your life"* with one word swapped — the mirror-flip that is his fingerprint.

And buried in the aggression is the album's load-bearing line: *"if you choose to tame the beast, you got to prove yourself to be a champion."* It says *tame*, never *kill* — the choice to integrate the Beast rather than destroy it.

## Its place in the mythology

The song's central gesture came from outside Roy. His collaborator MERCY sent him a joke voice note singing the chorus after getting back from a party; he knew immediately it had to be a Sir Lucius song and called it "the ultimate introverted extrovert anthem." That framing is the key: Lucius is Roy's socially functional performance worn over the introverted self underneath. MERCY is a real collaborator and co-writer — not a symbol — and the hook being someone else's party joke is a caution against reading deep single-author intent into it.

The shallowness is engineered, and the proof is in the visual lore. In *The Interview EP 2, "The Rise of sir lucius,"* Roy brings *"my favorite song I've ever written… The Song of Love"* — that is, [[SOL]] — and sings its opening. Lucius cuts him off: *"STOP! No one wants this love stuff. Your label doesn't want to put this out."* He works every lever at once — the label's verdict, the money, the people who depend on Roy, the humiliation of living at his parents' house — then presses a browning banana like a button and plays Not Your Homie instead. The exchange runs: Lucius, *"this is what the people want"*; Roy, *"it doesn't mean anything"*; Lucius, *"exactly. It's nonsense. It's all nonsense."* And Roy gives in: *"Fine, I'll give you this one."*

Two things resolve there. The shallowness is intentional — *"it's nonsense"* is Lucius's own word for his product, said with satisfaction. And the song traded away is SOL, the love-song buried so the caricature can run. The spiritually catastrophic beat is not the aggression of the track — it's the concession: *I'll give you this one.* The press later named the three-single sequence — [[LOOP]] → Not Your Homie → [[Welcome to the Circus]] — as two opposing instincts toward one realization: clarity beyond the system, or mastery from within.

A timeline note that keeps the song honest: Not Your Homie does *not* originate Lucius. The figure is independent of this song and predates it. What the song stages is a persona-hardening event — the "I" refusing false kinship and hardening into a guarded border. The billing changed, not the figure's existence.

## Characters

- [[Sir Lucius]] — the speaking voice, in predatory-charm register; the Beast refined into strategy.
- [[Roy]] — the one making the concession; the soul to Lucius's leverage in the dialectic the song dramatizes (sincerity against performance, integration against manipulation).
- [[Mary]] — not in the song directly, but the crown Lucius claims here is the one she revokes in [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]]. He is, in effect, reselling a championship already taken from him.

- [[Inversion]] — the upside-down smile, the mirror-flip from *love my life* to *end your life*.
- [[The Green Apple]] — Lucius's mark; in the visual lore he eats the apple loudly, a child performing sovereignty.

## Notes

The richest reading holds Lucius as the moon, not the sun — a reflector, not a source. Illuminated by fear, algorithms, and audience hunger, he becomes viral and dominating; illuminated by source-light (the heart, [[SOL]], the faceless mother), he becomes witness and bridge. He is the moon trying to become a sun. That is his tragedy and why he reads as morally complex rather than simply evil.

The line *"you don't know me"* carries a second depth in the Jungian frame the project keeps invoking: the shadow refusing *shallow* recognition — not friendship or aesthetic appreciation, but real knowing. The song accuses both the listener and Roy of incomplete self-knowledge. Lucius longs to be truly known while enforcing *"you don't know me"* — the denial and the longing share an address. That contradiction is the actual drama of the arc, and this is where it's loudest.

Roy frames the song light — *"the ultimate introverted extrovert anthem,"* strategic nonsense, almost comic. The vault reads it heavy. The honest position: with the visual-lore frame, the Lucius-triumph reading is strong and tightly coherent, but the specific esoteric mappings (the Labors of Hercules' Leo trial, the lunar Yesod sphere, the throat tattoo read as the Kabbalistic Da'at) remain interpretation, not proof. Roy's own "strategic nonsense" framing *is* the song explaining itself as deliberately shallow viral bait — and if that self-aware joke is the whole point, the deeper apparatus is overlay.

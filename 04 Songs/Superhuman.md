# Superhuman

> **One-line reading.** *Superhuman* turns a named wound — Roy's perfectionism — into a marching, collective Protector, mistaking relentless stamina for awakening and trying to go *over* the human condition by force rather than *through* it by integration.

**Release:** *Subject to Flooding* (2018), track 6  ·  **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

A roaring "we" assembles into something like an army. The motifs are martial and bodily: *"get ready to fight,"* *"we paid our dues,"* *"battlecry,"* *"blood on our knees,"* and the drill cadence *"Left, right, left, it goes."* It promises boundlessness — *"ain't got no ceiling up on the roof"* — and refuses anonymity: *"ain't gonna be no face in the crowd."*

Then the bridge drops the march and exposes the fracture: *"all we are is human / but I'll try, try, try, try to be superhuman."* The song lives entirely in that gap. It never says *we are* — only *we gotta be, I'll try to be.* It is aspiration, not arrival.

## Its place in the mythology

This is [[The Protector]] in its martial, collective mode — the vulnerable solo voice converted into a moving formation that locks the tender self inside a tank. Against [[Loser]] (dissolve into "we're all losers" so shame can't land) and [[Good Day]] (seal the vibe so disturbance can't land), *Superhuman* completes a trio: same wound-born operator, three faces, all before it acquires a name and a mask. The tell is that the march *needs* the inadequacy — if you were already enough, there'd be nothing to fight.

The bridge can't hold, and that collision is the point. Roy opened the album with *Loser* so listeners would feel *"good enough just as they are."* *Loser* says *enough*; *Superhuman* says *become more.* At the level of the wound, *human* registers as insufficiency — and the song performs the very perfectionism Roy names outright. Asked what he was like as a child, he has said: *"I wanted to be perfect, and it was psychotic, and I still struggle with perfectionism to be honest."* He links it to people-pleasing — managing perception to be approved of — which chains forward into the constructed self that hides the genuine one. *Superhuman* is the collective form of that perfect-child compulsion: the army as mass persona, performing more-than-human adequacy.

The song also wants the wrong vector. Roy works inside Alice Bailey's *The Labours of Hercules*, an influence he has named publicly. In the Leo labour, Hercules masters the lion not by destroying it but by slaying its *independence* and wearing its skin — mastery through integration, the personality subordinated to the soul. Read against that, *Superhuman* is the lion not yet slain: the coordinated personality marching as one, mistaking its own untamed force for the goal. *"Try to be superhuman"* is the ego trying to become *more lion*, before the turn that would tame and wear it. Bailey's path of liberation leads *through* the world of illusion; *Superhuman* tries to go *over* it — *"ain't got no ceiling"* — which is exactly why the bridge's *human* can't hold. (See [[The Labors of Hercules]].)

## Notes

In Kabbalistic terms the song reads as Geburah (severity, martial contraction) severed from Chesed (mercy) — combat and endless labor that skip the heart-center, thickening armor instead of opening. The honest counter-reading: this is also simply a stadium-scale endurance anthem, and the esoteric scaffolding is interpretation layered over genuinely grounded material (the perfectionism quote, which stands on Roy's own words). Years later Roy returns to the same half-vs-whole figure — *"I reached for my Father's hand / He said you're half the man that you know"* — voicing incompleteness-until-integration; that's a cross-era echo of *Superhuman*'s "try to be more," not the same source.

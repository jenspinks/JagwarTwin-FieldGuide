# City of Angels

> **One-line reading.** The Circus's gospel of fame — where a survival-chant becomes a promise of upload, and the reward for making it is not resurrection but reproducibility: everyone looks just like you because the particular self the dream promised has already been deleted to grant it.

**Release:** Lucius Lullaby (2026) · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

City of Angels is the temple that [[Playing to the Gods]] is the seed of: tracks 9 and 10 form one movement, heard live as a single song. The shadow is co-credited as a vocalist on it — Lucius signs the fame song.

## What happens in the song

A walk through Hollywood, down a star-lined road. A "beautiful stranger" promises that all your dreams are coming true and that *everyone looks just like you*. Under it runs the survival-chant carried over from the previous track — *never gonna die*, live forever in the bright lights, godlike. One detail folds the city into Lucius's domain: it is "the City of *Light*," singular (luc-, light, the light-bearer).

The sharpest move is one swapped pronoun. The stranger delivers the pitch in the second person — *all **your** dreams are coming true*. After the bridge the same line returns in the first — *all **my** dreams*. By the outro it rotates back to *your*, tagged again with "and she said." So the grammar runs pitched, then internalized, then re-transmitted: the city says it *to* him, he swallows it, then he says it *to the next walker*. He doesn't escape the beautiful stranger — he becomes one. The song ends mid-rotation, occupying the recruiter's slot.

## Its place in the mythology

That pronoun rotation is why this song never wakes up the way [[Lucius Lullaby]] does. The title track can turn and say "it's all just a lie," because there is still a self standing outside the spell to confess it. City of Angels ends with no one left outside. It is a voice returning not to its source — the [[The Loop and the Circle|Circle]] — but to the recruiter's position, perpetuating the pitch. The Loop encoded in pronouns.

*Everyone looks just like you* is the song's deepest crime, and it is not vanity. A circle requires distinct points held in communion — many voices singing one song back. Sameness deletes the distinct points, and a circle with no distinct points collapses into a single point repeating: the Loop. Set against the cosmology Roy maps elsewhere — *one becomes two, and one day we'll get back together and see the great design* — Hollywood offers the counterfeit return: not unity-with-difference but uniformity, the many flattened into interchangeable copies. It says "we are all one" and means "you are all replaceable." In a project named Jagwar *Twin*, built on the productive two and the mirror that *wakes*, this is twinning gone metastatic — infinite doubling with no other left to meet. See [[Source vs Reflection]].

*Never gonna die* is a false immortality, and the catalogue tells us whose. In the premiere series Lucius says it himself: *I can't be stopped, my lullaby is everywhere as we speak.* The deathless thing is the lullaby — the replicated song, the content that is everywhere. So *never gonna die* is the listener's side of Lucius's *I can't be stopped*, the shadow's ubiquity handed to the singer as if it were his own. One of Roy's own artifacts names the mechanism outright — a board with a tab titled "how to upload myself to the internet." Upload equals never-gonna-die equals becoming reproducible (see [[The Upload]]). Which is exactly what the phoenix is *not*: the phoenix burns, dies, and is reborn *through* the death. *Never gonna die* skips the burning — same self preserved and multiplied. The phoenix dies in order to live forever; this offers living forever so you never have to die. One is resurrection; the other is taxidermy.

Notice how little the singer actually says. Across both tracks he originates two utterances: a hello, and the survival-chant. Everything else is the city speaking, or the chant repeating. His one agentive human word — a greeting offered to a stranger — is converted into prophecy and advertisement; he gets a pitch back, not a person. This is the dark twin of the project's mirror doctrine, where Roy gets out of the way so others see themselves. Here is a man so far inside the machine he has no voice left to get out of the way with (see [[The Voice]]).

## Characters

**Lucius** is the gospel's voice — the counterfeit-communion seller, co-credited on the track. **Roy** is the nearly mute self walking the road, a hello and a chant his only words. **Mary** is present by inversion: the "beautiful stranger" is the exact counterfeit of the project's true feminine. Mary is never seen directly — faceless, the seeing-without-reflection, the ground you look *from*. The stranger is faced, visible, generic ("another beautiful stranger"), an object you look *at*. The axis is visibility: the true feminine is invisible because she is source; the false one is hyper-visible because she is a reflection for sale. The true one remembers you to yourself; the false one promises your dreams and tells you everyone's the same.

## Notes

The Hollywood walk reads as an inverted Jacob's Ladder — the ladder of angels among stars, run backward: the singer walks *down* rather than up, the stars are the Walk of Fame trodden flat, and the angels have become "beautiful strangers," gatekeepers of the lower realm rather than messengers of the source. In the same key, a City of *Light* (singular) scattered into *bright lights* (plural) reads as the one Light dispersed — sparks multiplied but never gathered, the inverse of the great design; see [[Keilim - The Breaking of the Vessels]]. These are framework harmonics, held as resonance — and the simplest reading, a conventional cautionary-Hollywood song, is valid on its own; the apparatus is interpretation layered on top. There is also a clear echo of *Hotel California* ("welcome to Hollywood" / "you can never leave"), noted as a listener's intertext, not a claimed influence.

# No Fake Friends

> **One-line reading.** The moment relational trust collapses under poverty and exposure, forcing the psyche to replace openness with vigilance — the beginning of a long ascent away from vulnerability.

**Release:** *Subject to Flooding* (2018), track 8 — styled *N.F.F. (No Fake Friends)*  ·  **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The song opens in raw material instability — sleeping on sidewalks, no money, no car, everything owned in a backpack — and keeps returning to one wound: *"where did my friends go when I had nothing?"* The speaker's response is to build an archive against forgetting: *"I made a list and I kept all the names to remember that."* The verses speak in surveillance language — *"night watch,"* *"they ain't even on my radar"* — and the second half climbs into success imagery (*"I'm taking off now,"* *"Bali with the fam for the New Year"*) while the emotional distance only widens.

## Its place in the mythology

The emotional center isn't rage first — it's disbelief. *"Where did my friends go when I had nothing?"* sounds confused before it sounds accusatory; the wound is the discovery that belonging itself was conditional. The song still contains grief, not yet hardened cynicism, and the rejection of "fake friends" is less a flex than an attempt to survive relational collapse without losing coherence.

This is the third face of the debut's [[The Protector]] cluster. Where [[Loser]] preempts shame and [[Good Day]] armors the high-mood state, *No Fake Friends* builds the social-filtration layer: the perimeter that decides who gets through. The list starts as an anti-erasure mechanism — *abandonment happened; memory will not be rewritten* — before it hardens into radar, hypervigilance, and access control. The later [[Sir Lucius]] figure doesn't appear from nowhere; he crystallizes gradually out of repeated defensive adaptations that were, at first, understandable and survivable.

One image carries unusual weight: *"Hollywood I had the night watch / looked down at my stars."* These are the Walk of Fame stars, under the speaker's body while he sleeps on concrete. Stars are supposed to orient *upward* — navigation, wonder, soul-direction — but here he looks *down* at them, on night watch rather than in awe. Wonder is converting into surveillance: stars to pavement, openness to scanning. It's one of the album's defining gestures — the gradual militarization of receptive human faculties under prolonged instability. The song ends in apparent success, but the underlying truth holds: *the speaker does not recover trust. He recovers altitude.* And altitude is not the same thing as home.

## Characters

[[Roy]], first-person and autobiographical, addressing absent "fake friends." In the theatrical canon, [[Sir Lucius]] later weaponizes this exact wound — citing the housing and financial insecurity recorded here as leverage. The scarcity in the song is what gives the later bargain its bite.

## Symbols

- The list — archive before weapon; grief preserved as memory before it becomes filtration
- The flattened stars — the Walk of Fame underfoot; transcendence collapsed into pavement

## Notes

In Hermetic-Kabbalistic terms the song maps the pressure between Malkuth (raw material exposure) and Yesod (memory, vigilance, the defensive "moon-layer"): under enough pressure, the psyche retreats upward into reflective structures — and the flattened-stars image intensifies it, with what should orient toward the heart collapsing downward into commodity. A fair counter-reading keeps it grounded: "real friends vs fake friends" is a genre staple, and the song may simply frame healthy boundaries — you don't reconcile with *fake* friends, so the absence of reconciliation could be the point rather than a tragedy.

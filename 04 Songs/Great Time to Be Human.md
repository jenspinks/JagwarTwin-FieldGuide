# Great Time to Be Human

> **One-line reading.** The album's toxic-positivity anthem: Sir Lucius stages human history as a children's sing-along, the verses telling nursery-rhyme fables of grief and violence while the refrain papers every one of them over with rainbows and forced gratitude. The cruelty is in the cheer.

Early on the album, this is the [[Happy Face]] mask scaled from a single face to an entire species — a whole civilization agreeing to grin. It sits between the song that *confesses* the lie and the song that *instructs* the mask, and shows the mask become a group activity.

**Release:** *Lucius Lullaby* (2026), track 3 · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

It would be easy to call this a dark song with a happy chorus. It is exactly the reverse — a happy chorus that is the dark thing. The verses do their grotesque work, and each time the song answers not with grief but with *"rainbows, butterflies / what is all this? / follow your bliss / everything keeps blooming / what a great time to be human!"* The cruelty isn't in the events; it's in the smile forced over them.

The form is a giveaway: *"there once was a boy… there once was a girl…"* is the opening of a fable, exactly the twisted bedtime-story register the album announces of itself. Read as a diptych, the two fables map a small catalogue of failed systems, and they fail asymmetrically. The boy matures *into repetition* — he becomes a Man and runs to a Lover who *"cast a spell to bring back his Mother,"* his adult love conscripted to re-animate the dead parent. The girl is *dispossessed* — she loses her youth, goes back for it, and is punished: *"they had her committed and shaved off her hair."* Masculine becoming as repeated trauma; feminine becoming as theft and punishment.

The hinge line is small and total: *"the crooks and believers all sing the same song."* Crooks and believers are supposed to be opposites — the whole moral order depends on the difference — and the spectacle dissolves it. Under the cheer, the liar and the faithful function identically, because what matters is not what you mean but that you're singing along.

Then the song's sharpest image: *"she went to The Chemist to make her a dog / but he was dyslexic, so he made a god / god, god, god (woof!)."* She asks for a dog — a companion, creaturely love, something humble to be loved by — and the broken maker hands her a god instead, abstraction and authority and worship, by error, a typo. Divinity manufactured without understanding. The genius is the *"(woof!)"*: the made-god still barks. The dog is trapped inside the god, the reversal collapsing in both directions at once.

## Its place in the mythology

That collapse of crooks and believers is worth a name, because the catalogue otherwise treats collective singing as sacred — the Circle, the crowd singing it back. This song forces the counter-case, and the vault holds it as [[Communion vs Spectacle]]: communion is many distinct voices singing the truth together; spectacle is many voices forced into the same song. The crowd here is not awakened. It is synchronized. A unison is not a communion.

The dog/god joke condenses the album's whole mirror-logic into one line: counterfeit divinity, the maker without wisdom. It is kin to Lucius as false light, to [[City of Angels]]'s false immortality, to Lucius counterfeiting [[Mary]]. A people that misreads its own need for creaturely love and builds an idol instead.

The relentless positivity is Lucius-mode, but the song is too observant and too grief-conscious to be *pure* Lucius. The truest framing is that Lucius is staging Roy's lament as a children's spectacle: the content knows it's horrifying, the form refuses to stop smiling. Both voices are on the credit. The song is the argument between them, performed as a sing-along.

## Notes

The ad-libs enforce the whole design: *"oops! / yay! / boo hoo / woof!"* are children's-show emotional cues fired at exactly the wrong moments — a brother is smothered and the song says *oops*; kids dance to destroy and it says *yay.* They are reaction-buttons, the sound of horror being processed into content, and content needing applause. And when a single exit finally appears — *"Shanghai Summertime / goodbye"* — it is immediately reabsorbed into the chorus. Even leaving is swallowed by the cheer-machine, because *"everything keeps blooming"* whether you heal or not: growth is not the same as healing.

The lab notes keep several readings as resonance: the dog/god line as the Demiurge of Gnostic myth rendered as a clerical error — the botched lower maker who mistakes himself for the source; *"follow your bliss"* as Joseph Campbell soured into bumper-sticker anesthesia; *"Shanghai Summertime"* as *shanghaied*, a sunny image hiding coercion that rhymes with the album's captivity logic. These are framework correspondences, not claims about intent — and worth tiering carefully, because this is the album's most collaborative track (five writers), the least likely place for deliberate single-author cosmology. The plainer reading is live: a blackly funny, dark-comedy exercise in human awfulness, where the cheerful-tune-horrific-lyrics irony *is* the joke. Hold both. (One housekeeping note from the visual lore: Lucius yells the dog/god line at a later episode, but the *"another song about ME"* he says next belongs to [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]], not this track.)

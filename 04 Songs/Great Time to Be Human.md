# Great Time to Be Human

> **One-line reading.** The album's toxic-positivity anthem: [[Sir Lucius]] stages human history as a children's sing-along, the verses telling nursery-rhyme fables of grief and violence while the refrain papers every one of them over with rainbows and forced gratitude. The cruelty is in the cheer.

> [!info] Album & credits
> **Album:** *[[Lucius Lullaby (album)|Lucius Lullaby]]* (2026, Big Loud Rock) — **track 3 of 15** · 3:23
> **First released** as a standalone single, **April 14, 2023** — three years before the album absorbed it.
> **Written by** Roy English, Ryan Spraker, Tom Peyton, Sean Van Vleet & Josh Dun — five writers, the most-collaborative track on the record · **Produced by** Roy English & Ryan Spraker
> **Vocals** credited to *"sir lucius"* **and** *"ROY"* — the dual character-voice device.
> **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

This is one of the oldest songs the album owns and, arguably, its clearest thesis statement in miniature. It arrives early in *Lucius Lullaby* as the [[Happy Face]] mask scaled from a single face to an entire species — a whole civilization agreeing to grin. It sits between the song that *confesses* the lie and the song that *instructs* the mask, and shows the mask become a group activity.

## At a glance

- **Era / release** — First a **single** (April 14, 2023, out in the UK the same day), the centerpiece of Roy's spring-2023 US tour with Transviolet; then **reissued as track 3** of *[[Lucius Lullaby (album)|Lucius Lullaby]]* (2026). The 2023 framing was purely humanist; the album is where it grows fangs.
- **In one line** — A happy chorus that *is* the dark thing: grotesque bedtime fables answered, every time, by rainbows and *"what a great time to be human!"*
- **Who speaks** — Both [[Roy]] and [[Sir Lucius]] are on the credit. The best reading is Lucius staging Roy's lament as a children's spectacle: the content knows it's horrifying, the form refuses to stop smiling. [[The Crowd]] appears as the synchronized sing-along the whole song is built to trap.
- **Key symbols** — [[The Empty Mirror]] · [[Inversion]] (the dog/god flip) · [[The Mask]] · [[The Circus]] · [[The Algorithm]]

## The song, mapped

It would be easy to call this a dark song with a happy chorus. It is exactly the reverse. The engine runs the same four moves on repeat: *state a cheerful frame, narrate an atrocity in nursery-rhyme meter, fire a children's-show reaction-button at the wrong emotional beat, and answer all of it with the same rainbows-and-bliss refrain.* Read as a **diptych** — a boy's fable, then a girl's — each answered by the identical chorus. Here it is, move by move.

| Beat | In the song | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold open** | *"What a great time to be human!"* | The title-hook stated flat, before any story — the caption arrives *before* the horror it will caption. |
| **Boy, part 1** | *"There once was a boy who lost his own Mother / loved him to death and smothered his brother (oops!)"* | Grief and fratricide in a bedtime cadence; the kids in the street *"danced to destroy (yay!)."* Ecstatic ruin, buttoned by cartoon cues. |
| **Boy, part 2** | *"the boy became Man then ran to his Lover / she cast a spell to bring back his Mother"* | Maturation *into repetition* — adult love conscripted to re-animate the dead parent. Then the preacher *"banged a gong,"* and the hinge line lands: *"the crooks and believers all sing the same song."* |
| **Refrain** | *"rainbows, butterflies / what is all this? / follow your bliss / everything on the earth keeps blooming / what a great time to be human!"* | The erasure. Not grief — anesthesia. Fired straight after fratricide and back again after every wound. |
| **Girl, part 1** | *"here once was a girl who drank from The Fountain / she left her youth halfway up The Mountain"* | Youth lost, sought, and *punished*: she goes back for it, *"Someone was there,"* and *"they had her committed and shaved off her hair."* Institutional violence. |
| **Girl, part 2** | *"they threw her a party with diamonds and spoons / but They were bloodsuckers with crocodile shoes (boo hoo)"* | Predator-consumers, death worn as status — and then the album's sharpest image, the dog/god botched creation (below). |
| **Coda** | *"Shanghai Summertime / Goodbye / what a great time to be human!"* | The lone exit-word — *"Goodbye"* — appears once and is instantly swallowed back into the hook. Even leaving is reabsorbed. |

Two structural notes worth flagging. First, the fables are **parallel in form but asymmetric in fate**: the *boy matures into repetition* (the mother-wound repeated through the Lover), the *girl is dispossessed* (her youth stolen, then punished for wanting it back). Masculine becoming as repeated trauma; feminine becoming as theft and punishment. Second, the song's proper nouns are not transcription noise — **Mother, Man, Lover, The Fountain, The Mountain, Someone, They, The Chemist** are capitalized as official text. They're *stations*, not people. The song is a miniature anthropology: kinship and religion in the boy's half, then youth, medicine, and creation in the girl's.

## What it means

A few claims that cut across the whole pageant:

- **The refrain is the violence.** The cruelty isn't in the events — a brother smothered, a girl committed and shaved, bloodsuckers in crocodile shoes. It's in the smile forced over them. The ideology insists you stay grateful *while it shows you the wreckage.* Everything else in the song serves this one inversion.
- **The album's masking arc runs through it.** [[Lucius Lullaby]] *confesses* the lie (*"it's all just a lie"*); [[Happy Face]] *instructs* the mask (put it on); this song shows the mask become a **group activity** — the [[Happy Face]] scaled from one face to a species.
- **"The crooks and believers all sing the same song."** Crooks and believers are supposed to be opposites; the whole moral order depends on the difference, and the spectacle dissolves it. Under the cheer, the liar and the faithful function identically, because what matters is not what you *mean* but that you're singing along. This one line is the seed of the vault's whole [[Communion vs Spectacle]] distinction (more below) — the single sharpest place the concept touches the ground.
- **Growth is not healing.** *"Everything keeps blooming"* whether you heal or not — life continues regardless of suffering, beautiful and brutally indifferent. And when the lone exit finally appears (*"Goodbye"*), the chorus eats it. There is no way out of the pageant that the pageant won't absorb.

## The god made by mistake

The album's whole mirror-logic condensed into a joke: *"she went to The Chemist to make her a dog / but he was dyslexic, so he made a god / god, god, god (woof!)."* She asks for a **dog** — a companion, creaturely love, something humble to be loved *by* — and the broken maker hands her a **god** instead: abstraction, authority, worship. And he does it *by error*, a typo, a dyslexic's slip. Divinity manufactured without understanding.

The genius is the *"(woof!)."* The made-god still **barks**. The dog is trapped inside the god, and the reversal collapses in both directions at once — dog becomes god, and god is exposed as dog. It's a perfect emblem of the album's counterfeit divinity, the maker without wisdom: kin to [[Sir Lucius|Lucius]] as false light, to [[City of Angels]]'s false immortality, to Lucius counterfeiting [[Mary]]. A people that misreads its own need for creaturely love and builds an idol instead. It is also, unmistakably, the funniest line on the record — which is exactly why it lands. (Roy has leaned on it directly: a 2025 promo clip surfaced the line *"but he was dyslexic so he made a god"* over the caption *"this song still goes hard,"* and in 2026 he quote-tweeted a dog-on-a-couch post — *"but in a mirror I'm God!"* — with the caption *"what a great time to be human."* He's the one publicly welding the title to dog/god and mirror-godhood.)

## Characters

- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — the grinning emcee staging the pageant; co-credited vocalist. In the theatrical *[[The Interview]]* series he yells this song's dog/god line at a bound Roy, calling it *"a gift"* he *"took a few liberties with."* (Housekeeping: the *"another song about ME"* he says next belongs to [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]], not this track.)
- **[[Roy]]** — the lament *inside* the grin; the grief the form refuses to let stop the smile. Co-credited vocalist. The song is the argument between the two of them, performed as a sing-along.
- **[[Mary]]** — present only as her *counterfeit*. The spell-casting Lover is the album's false feminine, the partner asked to resurrect the past rather than restore the man to wholeness — the counterfeit that re-animates the wound where Mary would heal it. (Mary is not literally in the text.)
- **[[The Crowd]]** — the many voices forced into the one song; not awakened, synchronized.

## Symbols

- **[[The Empty Mirror]]** — the operation of the whole song. Roy wears a tattoo by that name (*"looking at yourself and seeing nothing"*), and this is that operation set to music: the cheer reflects back only the *desire* — rainbows, bliss — while the *reality* of the fables goes entirely un-reflected. Look into the song's smile and see nothing of what just happened.
- **[[Inversion]]** — the dog/god reversal made literal; god becomes dog and dog becomes god in the same breath.
- **[[The Mask]]** — [[Happy Face]] scaled to a civilization; the species-wide grin.
- **[[The Circus]]** — the machine the whole pageant runs inside; the official visualizer stages the song there, and puts a *"PROPERTY OF B.M.R.C."* rocket launcher on screen (BMRC being the industry-Babylon that manufactures both the show and the ordnance).

## Advanced reading

> [!note]- Communion vs Spectacle — why the hinge line matters *(tap to open)*
> The catalogue otherwise treats collective singing as *sacred* — the [[The Circle|Circle]], the crowd singing it back. This song forces the counter-case, which the vault holds as [[Communion vs Spectacle]]: **communion is many distinct voices singing the truth together; spectacle is many voices forced into the same song.** The crowd here is not awakened. It is synchronized. *A unison is not a communion.* As a Lucius-voiced track it stages the **commanded/harvested echo** — the crowd made to sing one song — against the Roy-voiced version where the crowd sings *with*. Read in a Babel key it darkens further: a crowd taught to sing one song is the Babel crowd (*"one language and the same words"*), whose scriptural fate is collapse. This is the single highest-value in-lyric anchor for the whole sing-back thread in the corpus.

> [!note]- The dog/god line as failed alchemy and the Demiurgic typo *(tap to open)*
> Read through the vault's [[Inversion]] operation, the dog/god flip is the same machinery as *Rorrim Ehtni* (mirror-writing) and the Empty Mirror — a reversal that runs both ways at once. As **failed alchemy**, it's elevation with no soul in it: the Chemist performs the great work of making, and produces a botched result *because he doesn't understand what he's making.* In a Gnostic key (see [[Gnosticism - The World as Trap]]) it's the **Demiurge as a clerical error** — the ignorant lower maker who mistakes himself for the source, here reduced to a dyslexic's slip. Framing god-making as a *typo* mocks the whole idea of divine intention. Held honestly, though: the dog/god *text* is grounded, the Demiurge reading is a framework correspondence. dog↔god is among the oldest reversals in English, and *"dyslexic, so he made a god"* is first of all a wordplay gag — building the botched-Creator cosmology on it is real, but the weight the reading carries outruns the line. Mark it and hold it lightly.

> [!note]- The ad-libs, "Shanghai Summertime," and the anesthetic surface *(tap to open)*
> The ad-libs enforce the whole design. *"Oops! / yay! / boo hoo / woof!"* are children's-show emotional cues fired at exactly the wrong moments — a brother is smothered and the song says *oops*; kids dance to destroy and it says *yay.* They're reaction-buttons, the sound of horror being processed into content, and content needing applause. Call it the algorithm laughing at the boy who lost his mother. As **framework correspondences** (not claims about intent): *"follow your bliss"* is Joseph Campbell soured into bumper-sticker anesthesia — the target is bliss-as-slogan, not Campbell — part of the album's method of borrowing familiar phrases and turning them; and *"Shanghai Summertime"* reads as *shanghaied*, a sunny image hiding coercion (kidnapped aboard a ship), rhyming with the album's captivity logic. It could be only exotic scenery — the spectacle operation is identical either way.

> [!note]- Placement, phase, and the pronoun-evacuated voice *(tap to open)*
> Track 3 is the **first non-Lucius-titled slot** on the album — the opening third otherwise being Lucius's own territory ([[The Circle (Lucius Version)]], [[Lucius Lullaby]], [[Welcome to the Circus]]). It opens the album's long middle movement, the stretch where the [[The Circus|Circus]] operates at full power. It sits in the record's [[Nigredo|nigredo]] — the blackening, the loop with the exit closed — which is where most of the album lives. And it is **pronoun-evacuated**: a mechanical count returns *I = 0, you = 2*, a collective/ironic voice with no first-person "I"-engine at all (the sharp contrast being the album's "I"-driven tracks like [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]]). The absent "I" is the point: nobody in particular is cheering, so *everybody* is. See [[Pronoun Audit (I-You)]].

> [!note]- Cain/Abel — the boy-fable as an un-reconciled twin *(tap to open)*
> A structural parallel, not a claim Roy has made: the boy who *"smothered his brother"* flags as a possible **Cain/Abel** figure — fratricide in the opening breath. It matters because the album's core question is whether the twin split (Roy and Lucius) reconciles. [[Jacob and Esau]] are the twins who *reintegrate* — they embrace and weep. Cain and Abel are the twins who *don't* — murder, exile, the mark. Lucius-as-Esau (reconcilable, the hopeful arc) versus Lucius-as-Cain (separation as a permanent condition) are two prognoses for the album's open integration question, and this fratricidal little fable quietly puts the darker one on the table.

> [!note]- The collaborators — Josh Dun and a favorite band made flesh *(tap to open)*
> The five-writer credit lists *"Josh Dunn,"* which reads as **Josh Dun**, drummer of Twenty One Pilots — a band Roy has called, in a public interview, his *"favorite band ever"* (*"I love their new album… I think that they're genius"*). He plays the drum kit here and again on [[Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)]], making a named favorite into a two-track physical presence on the record. Background vocals include **little luna**, the same featured collaborator from *33*'s [[Pay Attention (feat. little luna)]] — a recurring real person, not a cipher. (Loosely held: the Roy/Lucius alter-ego structure rhymes with TØP's own "Blurryface," but that's a resonance, not a source.)

## Source notes

- **Grounded (released music, credits, public posts, public interviews).** The two-phase release history (2023 single → 2026 track 3) and the spring-2023 Transviolet tour; the five-writer credit and dual *"sir lucius" / "ROY"* vocal billing; Josh Dun on drums (here and on *Bad Feeling*) and little luna on background vocals; the capitalized archetypal stations as official text; the dog/god line in the lyric *and* yelled by Lucius in *[[The Interview]]* (EP7); the album's own *"twisted takes on adolescent musical themes for hands that rock the cradle"* billing; the official 2026 visualizer with its *"PROPERTY OF B.M.R.C."* rocket launcher; the 2025 and 2026 Roy promo posts welding the title to the dog/god line. Roy's one direct quote about the song, from a 2023 feature, is telling for what it *lacks*: *"it's an observation of the human experience. It can be pretty strange, but something about it is so beautiful"* — pure humanism, zero cosmology, three years before the Lucius canon reframed it.
- **The public grounding for the cheer-over-horror reading** is Roy's own term for his method — **"Trojan Horse music,"** a sing-along chorus carrying dark content (paired with the nursery-rhyme craft principle he's cited: study *"Twinkle Twinkle"* because *"the simplest songs are the most complex"*). That's the citable public basis for the whole toxic-positivity thesis.
- **Strong readings.** The refrain-as-violence; crooks-and-believers → [[Communion vs Spectacle]]; the failed-systems diptych with its masculine-repetition / feminine-dispossession asymmetry; the spell-casting Lover as false feminine; dog/god as counterfeit creation; the empty-mirror cheer; the ad-libs as algorithmic emotional flattening; Lucius staging Roy's lament. Grounded in the material, but interpretive.
- **Framework / resonance (never authorial intent).** The Demiurgic typo (Gnostic); *"follow your bliss"* as soured Campbell; *"Shanghai Summertime"* as *shanghaied*; the nursery-rhyme substrate; the Cain/Abel twin-frame; Josh Dun's drums as grotesque-parade percussion.
- **How we'd be wrong.** The plainer reading is live and worth keeping on the table: a blackly funny dark-comedy exercise in human awfulness — cheerful tune, horrific lyrics, the irony *is* the joke — in the Tom Lehrer / Randy Newman / *"Pumped Up Kicks"* lineage. This matters *especially* here because it's the album's most collaborative track (five writers), the least likely place for deliberate single-author cosmology; the gendered fable-anthropology and dog/god alchemy are at least as plausibly emergent from a writers' room as encoded. The explicit falsifier: a Roy statement framing the song as gallows-humor-for-the-effect would demote the Communion-vs-Spectacle thesis from the song's center to an overlay. Hold both.

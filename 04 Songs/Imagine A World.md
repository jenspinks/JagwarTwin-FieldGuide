# Imagine A World

> **One-line reading.** An invitation to imagine — framed as authorship, not escape — that runs through wonder, false gods, and media conditioning, and lands on Eden relocated inside: look, believe, conceive it.

**Release:** *33* (2022), penultimate track · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

This is the album's capstone before the closer — it gathers the record's threads (false gods, media programming, self-authorship, the Eden within) into a single "imagine and create your world" invitation, then hands off to *The Circle*.

## What happens in the song

The frame is hammered: *"imagine a world full of wonder,"* located internally — *"it's all inside of your mind, it's up to you to decide."* Imagination is framed as low-stakes authorship: *"it's easy to try, you can't get it wrong, playing your note in a song, written by you for your life."* It poses a designed-divinity question — *"is there a God you designed?"* — and treats money as neutral but consequential: *"money was magic, but it could make your life tragic, depending on how you acted; you were the cause and reaction."*

It sets passive options (*"do nothing… yell at people… get in fights"*) against heroic ones (*"be a hero… if you got out your own way and went your own way"*). It reframes hardship as chosen curriculum — *"you pick your parents when you start, they're perfect for your lesson… reflections of who you are"* — undercut by a comic *"(it's not a phase mom!)."* It names the narrowing of original wonder: *"everything is brand new, then people start to label you, and you turn on the cable news, and Those Thoughts take ahold of you."* And it lists religions as all "right" — *"Brahma, Muhammad, and OZ, Horus, the Buddha, and Christ"* — before landing on an internalized Eden: *"the Garden of Eden… look inside you'll see it, you just gotta believe it, you just gotta Conceive it."*

## Its place in the mythology

Imagination here is authorship, not escape — the faculty through which possibilities become visible and then enacted, framed as a generative practice rather than a test. The self as world-author rhymes directly with the cosmogony that creates by *speaking*; this is that creation made participatory. The same world appears as wonder, tragedy, opportunity, or prison depending on the lens, so experience follows interpretation.

Two threads tie it to the rest of the album. The capitalized "Those Thoughts" taking hold, beside cable news and labeling, is the attention-economy critique re-entering — conditioning as the thief of possibility. And OZ in the god-list is the tell: among real deities sits the humbug, the man behind the curtain, a projected "god" who is really a man working machinery. Set beside *"is there a God you designed?"*, it flags that some gods are authored fabrications, and it rhymes with the earlier attention-force whose "televise is just my disguise." So the perennialist line — every religion "right" — carries a trickster's wink: in a world you imagine, even the fraud-god is "right," which is both liberating and a warning.

The closer it hands to is the answer to its own search — what's sought externally is found within. Eden is not a lost place but an interior one, reached by belief and then conception: believe, then conceive, imagination as creation or birth.

## Notes

The internalized-Eden reading is grounded on Roy's own public "I'm 33" board, which carries a handwritten emanation list that terminates at "10. The World" — and in the board cosmology the unlock code for that ladder resolves to this very song title. So *Imagine A World* doesn't only close the album's argument; its title names the final rung of Roy's own ascent. The further identification of that rung with the lowest, most material sphere of the Tree of Life stays a correspondence — Roy's hand writes "The World," not the Kabbalistic name — but the lyric makes the move legible, relocating that "kingdom" into consciousness: *"it's all inside of your mind."* Roy's own cross-shaped tattoo, which he glosses as representing Christ and "the 4 worlds," names the same four-world structure whose floor this song occupies.

The lighter resonances — imagination as creation (Romantic, Hermetic, New Thought); childlike wonder narrowed by labels; the Iron Butterfly tag "In-A-Gadda-Da-Vida," itself a slurred "in the Garden of Eden," the external Eden surviving only as a garbled pop echo — are framework correspondences, not stated intent. The song works as a sincere imagination-and-empowerment anthem, with the curtain-god and Eden-within architecture layered on a verified-but-accessible lyric.

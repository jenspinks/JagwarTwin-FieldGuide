# #1 Champion (la Conquistadora)

> **One-line reading.** A boast-anthem that the boaster does not survive. The champion announces his rank over and over, and the song he thinks is *about him* turns out to be about the woman who walks through it and drops him dead.

This is the album's anti-ego corrective, and it works as a trap. In the visual lore Sir Lucius hears the chorus, recognizes himself, and claims it as *"another song about ME"* — and in claiming it he misses the verse that executes the character singing it. The song unseats the very self that identifies with it.

**Release:** *Lucius Lullaby* (2026), track 8 · **Full lyrics:** [Genius](https://genius.com) · streaming

## What happens in the song

The chorus *looks* like pure championship — rank, money, the demand to be known: *"if you don't know 'bout me, you should know by now / I'm the number one champion in this town."* But listen to who actually sings it, and it turns inside out. The whole chorus is [[Mary]]'s voice — the count, the verdict, the *"know 'bout me,"* even the title. Every word that sounds like the braggart's flex is, in the mix, hers. *"You got a little bit of money, but it don't come cheap"* isn't a boast in her voice; it's a judgment — *your* wealth cost you dearly.

And [[Sir Lucius]] has no line of his own. The only words he ever sings are *"I'm the number one champion in this town,"* and he can't even claim those cleanly — he comes in on her voice, joining her on the title. He doesn't have an anthem; he has a line he climbs onto. At the end her voice fades, his drowns her out, she goes silent, and he sings the title a few more times completely alone — the last one a cappella, after the music has stopped. The appropriation isn't a metaphor laid over the song. The song performs it: he takes the championship by overpowering the voice it belonged to, and his prize is to stand in the dark repeating a word to no one.

In the verses, a woman walks by and the song forgets itself. *"Mary, Mary, so contrary"* — the boast drops into a nursery rhyme (Mother Goose's *"Mary, Mary, quite contrary"*), the album's twisted-adolescent tic surfacing right where the sacred enters. The champion's sharpest failure is in the looking: *"saw a halo but I didn't see a head, so I / made a move like a fool, dropped me dead when she said—"* He approaches her as something to want and to win, but he cannot actually see her. He gets the holy sign and a body, and no face between them.

The verse breaks off mid-sentence and cuts straight into the chorus, in Mary's voice: *"A-B-C, count 'em, one, two, three."* So the long-withheld line was never withheld — what she said is the count. He makes his move, and she answers not with a rebuke but with creation's own grammar, and it drops him dead.

## Its place in the mythology

This is the **real** faceless-Mary anchor for the whole catalogue. The halo-without-a-head is the project's recurring marker of the faceless divine-feminine — the same face-out-of-sight as in [[Weirdness]] and [[Ballerina Boy]]. And it turns the champion's own logic inside out. The spectacle-self lives by reflection: it announces its rank so the room will mirror it back. He comes to Mary looking for a surface that will return his image, and she returns nothing — no face to flatter, no reflection to win. She is the one who sees and is not seen-and-flattered. This is [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]] dramatized in four lines: Mary cannot be surveilled. Try to hold her with the consuming eye, and the self that knows no other way to look simply ends there.

The title walks a chain the song has been climbing the whole time: **Champion → Conquistador → Conquistadora.** The anthem of conquest arrives at Our Lady *of the Conquest* — *La Conquistadora* is a real Marian title, the oldest Marian statue in the United States — and the conqueror is conquered. The second Mary-verse arms her: *"she was La Conquistadora with a gun in her hand… fawning over no man… such a revolutionary."* This is not the meek handmaid; she is militant and autonomous, devotion as disruption. Mary is not the prize the champion wins. She is the force that defeats the prize-seeker.

The album keeps staging counterfeit creation against the genuine article, and this song is the genuine side. The false makers are [[Lucius Lullaby]]'s counterfeit rescue-voice and [[Great Time to Be Human]]'s Chemist who *"made a god"* by error. The true thing is Mary's *"A-B-C, count 'em"* — creation-grammar, the count that makes worlds. Pointedly, the false rescue-song and this true creation-song sit on the same record, running the same operation in opposite directions.

## Characters

- [[Mary]] — named, faceless, armed, and fatal to the ego that looks at her. She sings the entire chorus; the count is what drops the champion dead.
- [[Sir Lucius]] — the "champion" the song unseats; he claims the anthem and is dethroned by it. He owns none of the song he calls his own.
- [[Roy]] — the champion is the ego in Lucius-mode, but the failure is one of seeing, not strength; the song diagnoses that self by how it fails to perceive.

## Symbols

- [[The Rose]] — the visualizer caption (signed "El Roy") shows the champion following her *"white rose in my left hand"* and stepping off the ledge; purity, the sign of the quest, in his hand the whole time and not saving him.
- [[The Mirror]] — inverted: the spectacle-self comes looking for reflection and recognition, and Mary returns neither.
- The halo and the gun — holiness without a persona to flatter; the militarized Madonna; crown and corner held together.

## Notes

The champion is also **The Fool** (Tarot, card 0), and unusually for a tarot read it is doubly grounded in Roy's own text. The visualizer caption — *white rose in the left hand, stepping off the ledge* — is the Rider-Waite-Smith Fool almost line for line; the song itself says *"made a move like a fool"*; and the companion *Half the Man* post shows Roy in a jester's ruff. The Fool's card is the leap of faith on trust rather than sight. But here the leap is fatal, not triumphant: he steps off toward a divinity he reads as halo-and-dress but never as a face, and *"she dropped me dead."* The white rose doesn't save him, because the failure was never of devotion but of seeing. (The Fool is a live Roy symbol across the catalogue, not an import.)

The form tells the rest. The chorus keeps coming back, more insistent, finally stacked four times — but by then we've seen what the boast is for: a man counting his rank to a room that has already turned to look at someone else. The repetition that sounded like dominance now sounds like a thing that cannot stop, a loop. Mary doesn't argue with the championship; she walks through it, and it cannot hold her, and the counting goes on into the void.

Two honest cautions. The plainer reading is a femme-fatale boast track — the braggart undone by a woman out of his league, the female hook overpowering the boaster as ordinary arrangement — with the Marian theology layered on top. And "Mary" is an extremely common name; *"quite contrary"* is just the nursery rhyme. The leap to Magdalene, Queen of Heaven, and the conqueror-Madonna is exactly the name-driven over-reading the project polices elsewhere, and with three co-writers the sacred-feminine depth may be an emergent product rather than encoded intent. What holds the reading up regardless is grounded: the vocal arrangement really does hand the whole chorus to a woman's voice and let the boaster overtake and silence her — the song stages the appropriation in the mix, whatever theology you do or don't read into it.

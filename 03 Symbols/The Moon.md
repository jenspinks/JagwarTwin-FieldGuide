# The Moon

> **One-line reading.** The moon is the project's image of borrowed light — a body that shines only because something brighter is shining on it, and whose whole meaning turns on whether it remembers that.

## What it does

The moon is the catalogue's figure for **reflected light**. It generates nothing of its own; everything it gives off is the sun's light bounced back. That single fact is the operation — the moon is light that is not its own source, and the work uses it to ask the question it asks everywhere: does a reflected thing point you back to what lit it, or does it set up as a light in its own right?

What's worth noticing is that the *same* image does different work in different songs. The moon is not one fixed character. It is a position several figures can occupy, and the meaning shifts with who is standing in it — light reflected faithfully, light claiming to be the source, light that has dimmed but is promised back, light that holds steady through the dark. The image is constant; the charge depends on the song.

That keeps the moon firmly tied to the larger frame the work runs on. A reflection is not false because it is a reflection. It becomes false only when it forgets it is reflected — which is the exact line the [[The Mirror|mirror]] turns on, and why the moon and the mirror are two faces of one idea.

## Associations

- [[Sir Lucius]] — the lunar, reflective register. He shines with a light that isn't his and behaves as though it were; lit by fear and shame, he reflects fear and shame back. The clean read holds him at the edge of the moon's danger — reflected light under distortion, mistaking itself for the sun.
- [[Roy]] — read in one framework as the one who *occupies* the lunar position: the vessel that has nothing of its own and receives entirely from the source, the self who operates "from the heart" and can say *"I have become nothing."* The moon's emptiness, on this reading, is not failure but the right relation to the light.
- [[Mary]] — the moon's theology rather than its position: the diminished, face-hidden presence who reflects a light not her own and is promised restoration. *"Saw a halo but I didn't see a head"* — the source glimpsed, not yet possessed.

## Appears in

- *Ballerina Boy* — the spoken blessing in the outro: *"the moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light."* The moon as preservation-through-darkness — proof that light survives the night even when the night is all you can see. The tender pole of the symbol.
- The visual canon — Lucius performs *Bad Feeling* on a moon-and-stars lullaby stage; the reflective figure framed by the reflective body.
- *#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)* — the halo without a head, the lunar-feminine presence arrived but with her source still hidden.

## Related traditions

One strong interpretive frame, drawn from the Kabbalah that Roy openly studies, reads the moon across the whole cast. In that tradition the moon is the vessel that *"has nothing of its own, but receives entirely"* from the sun — so the lunar position is **Roy's** (the self emptied to receive), and the moon's *story* is **Mary's**: the **Shekhinah**, the divine feminine in exile, the moon that was once the sun's equal, was diminished, and is promised that *"the light of the moon shall become as the light of the sun."* On that reading the recurring arc — the moon longing to be restored, the crown glimpsed but not yet held — is the engine under *"reclaim your crown."* It's a framework correspondence, not a claim the work states outright, but it fits the shape the songs keep drawing: a dimmed, face-hidden light promising a brightness that's coming. (In the same frame, [[Sir Lucius]] is better placed as the *morning star* — the herald mistaken for the dawn it only announces — than as the moon itself; he works *through* reflection without being its proper occupant.)

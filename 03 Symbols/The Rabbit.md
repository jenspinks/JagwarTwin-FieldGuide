# The Rabbit

> **One-line reading.** The rabbit is the searching, vulnerable self before the split — and the clown-makeup rabbit who slides into the passenger seat is what that self becomes once the mind hardens into a guide.

## What it does

The rabbit is, first, the seeker. Roy tells the origin of *Good Time (1:08)* as a dream: he is driving a highway, *"looking for something, but I didn't know what I was looking for,"* and — *"somehow I know that — I'm a rabbit."* The rabbit here is instinct, vulnerability, survival, the self that is searching without yet knowing its own name.

Then a second rabbit appears. *"This other character pops into my passenger's seat and he's a rabbit, but he's got like clown makeup on, and he says, I'm Sir Lucius. And he's like, I'm here to help you."* He has all the answers; he's factually right about every turn — *"but I had a weird feeling in my heart… he was telling the truth, but he was manipulating me in some way, and I couldn't explain why."* The dream ends in a lightning-bolt crash; Roy wakes at 1:08.

That two-rabbit structure is the whole figure in miniature. Both are rabbits — the **same species** — so the encounter isn't hero against monster but self against self: [[Sir Lucius]] is not an invader but an *adaptation* of Roy's own rabbit-nature, the same instinct with clown makeup added. The shadow grows *from* him, which is why it can't simply be cast out. The rabbit is the self before the split; the clown-rabbit is what the mind becomes when it takes the guide's seat too early.

There is a longer, gentler version of the same seeker in a poem Roy published on his own platform and called *"Sir Rabbit pre-Lucius"* — Sir Rabbit, who answers the question of his own identity with *"I do not know… but I must be someone,"* and is led by a cast of guides through a trial and out the other side, *"back where he begun." That poem shows the seeker's authentic initiation; the dream shows the same seeker intercepted at the threshold by the wrong guide.

## Associations

- [[Roy]] — the rabbit himself, in his own telling; the searching, vulnerable self before fixed identity.
- [[Sir Lucius]] — the second rabbit, the one in clown makeup; the rabbit-ears and March-Hare imagery carry into his public look.

## Appears in

- *Good Time (1:08)* — the dream where Roy is a rabbit and Lucius is the clown-makeup rabbit who intercepts him; the song where Lucius is born.
- The *Sir Rabbit* poem — the seeker's full initiatory arc; *"Sir Rabbit pre-Lucius."*
- The 2026 theatrical era — Lucius's rabbit-eared look, and Roy's note that *"the year begins with Janus and the March Hare."*

## Related traditions

The rabbit is **Alice's White Rabbit**, the harbinger one follows down the hole into a surreal inverted world — here, the descent toward the Circus. Lucius's rabbit ears tie him to the **March Hare** of *Alice in Wonderland*, the figure who is *mad* — "mad as a March hare" — and the rabbit-as-guide echoes Carroll's Wonderland throughout. Across folklore the rabbit reads as instinct, vulnerability, and survival rather than power, which is exactly the register the work gives it.

# The Mirror

> **One-line reading.** The mirror is the device by which the self becomes visible only as something *other* — and it stays faithful exactly as long as it remembers it is not the source.

## What it does

The mirror is the project's central self-relation device: the self appears to itself only as an **image** — a double, an audience, a mask, a reflection, a recording, a screen, an eye, a "you." The work is literally named a *Hall of Mirrors*, and the mirror/eye/*I↔you* grammar runs all through it (the *Empty Mirror* tattoo; *Rorrim Ehtni*, "In the Mirror" spelled backwards; the line *"I just want to be a mirror"*; a near-even split of "I" and "you" across the lyrics).

A mirror can do four different things depending on how it's used: it can **awaken**, **distort**, **trap**, or **transmit**. The image itself is not automatically false. It becomes false only at one specific moment — when it stops pointing back to what it reflects and starts claiming to *be* the source.

That is the line the whole figure of [[Sir Lucius]] turns on: a mirror is faithful while it knows it is a mirror; it becomes Lucius when it forgets it is not the source. A reflection that gains autonomy stops returning the viewer to anything and instead captures the viewer inside the image. The cure in the work is not to smash the glass — it's to put the reflection back in its place.

## Associations

- [[Sir Lucius]] — the reflection that gained autonomy and began speaking as if it were the source; the mirror's worst outcome made into a character.
- [[Roy]] — the one in front of the glass; the self the reflection is supposed to return him to.

## Related traditions

The mirror gathers several older images. In Lacan's account of the *mirror stage*, the "I" is first formed through an image that is also other — identity and alienation in the same act, the reflection becoming an idealized rival-double; it maps onto the Roy/Lucius pair without being something Roy cites. **Narcissus** drowns in his own reflection. The **Gnostic** traditions imagine the visible world as a reflection one can get lost in. In **Kabbalah**, the moon reflects a light that is not its own. These are resonances worth hearing, not claims the work makes outright — and the mirror organizes its family of images without explaining everything.

# Sir Rabbit

> **One-line reading.** Sir Rabbit is the seeker before the mask — Roy's image of himself as a searching, uncertain self, still open, still teachable, before any guide hardens into [[Sir Lucius]].

Sir Rabbit is the figure Roy keeps returning to when he wants to picture the self *before* it gets a fixed identity. He's forgetful, searching, heart-recovering — someone whose whole situation is an open question. His defining line is exactly that: *"I do not know… but I must be someone."* He isn't a separate third character standing beside Roy and Lucius; he's the [[Roy]]-self at an earlier stage, the seeker who hasn't yet split into a heart and a ruling mind.

## Who he is

The rabbit is Roy's self-image in two places he's put on the record. The first is a long poem, **the Sir Rabbit poem**, published on Roy's own platform in 2022 — an initiation story with a rabbit at its center, which Roy himself has described as *"Sir Rabbit, pre-Lucius."* That gloss is the page's whole thesis in his own words: the rabbit is the self *before* the protector-mind hardens. The second is the dream Roy described around the release of **"Good Time (1:08)"** (2023), in which he says he *was* a rabbit, driving down a highway looking for something he couldn't name. So the grounded claim is modest and clean: Roy pictures himself as a rabbit, across two texts. Reading that rabbit as *the seeker-self* — the Roy-pole before the split — is interpretation, but it fits both texts closely.

A note on "before Lucius": in the dream, *Roy* is the rabbit, and [[Sir Lucius]] is the **second** rabbit, the one in clown makeup who drops into the passenger seat saying *"I'm here to help you."* So Sir Rabbit isn't a young Lucius. He's the self that exists *before* Lucius arrives — which is exactly why the counterfeit guide can intercept him.

## Role in the mythology

Sir Rabbit is the searching self that the childhood wound produces. The [[Ballerina Boy]] is the buried child — the part told *"you're not a real boy"* and pushed down. Sir Rabbit is what's left walking around afterward: directionless but intact, still capable of taking a leap. The line runs wound → buried child → searching rabbit.

The two rabbit texts read as the same journey told twice — once true, once intercepted:

- **The poem** runs the full initiation. The rabbit passes a cat, a violet moon that turns out to be a machine, a fox with a box, a forgotten heartbeat, a dove with a lamp, a spider at the divide, and a mourning sun — and comes through to a transformed return, *"back where he begun."* It ends in a leap the rabbit *chooses*.
- **The dream** runs the same seeker-position, but the wrong guide arrives first. Lucius is factually right about every turn and emotionally wrong, and Roy's heart catches the manipulation before his mind can explain it. The dream ends not in a chosen leap but in an involuntary crash.

One reading the texts strongly support: the poem shows the *authentic* initiation, and the dream shows that same seeker **intercepted at the threshold by the wrong guide.**

## What the poem's figures suggest

The poem is useful because it gives the world a small cast that isn't reducible to Roy and Lucius:

- **The Fox** is a *redeemed* trickster — manipulative in form but medicinal in result. His box forces the rabbit inward until the lost heartbeat is recovered (*"the key is hidden in yourself / just listen for the knock"*), and he arrives carrying bread the machine can't make. The cosmology, in other words, has a cunning that *heals* — which means not every trickster is Lucius. (The contrast is exact: the fox is manipulative-form but medicinal-result; Lucius is helpful-form but parasitic-result.)
- **The "other mind."** The poem tells the rabbit to *"use your other mind… it's been mapped,"* and the key to it is the heartbeat. That refines the heart/mind split the rest of the work runs on: a mind *severed* from the heart becomes Lucius; a mind *returned through* the heartbeat becomes this "other mind." Not heart against mind — mind reoriented through the pulse it forgot.
- **The Spider** is the threshold-keeper, the one who poses the name question — *"Who are you, Rabbit?"* — names the dreaming, and pointedly refuses to answer for him: *"that's up to you, it's not for me to pry."* Selfhood here is a *name* problem before it's anything else.
- **The Dove** is a [[Mary]]-style guide who gives light and then lets go (*"as much as I love company you must be on your way"*) — help that doesn't trap you in devotion.
- **The Violet Moon** is mechanized reflection: beautiful first, revealed as a machine on arrival — the same shape as Lucius, where the helpfulness comes before the felt manipulation.

## The Lucius mechanism this clarifies

Taken together, the rabbit texts pin down *how* Lucius works. He's the counterfeit guide who gives true answers **without** the heartbeat — he takes the guide's seat *before* the initiation has actually been endured. The cleanest way to say it: Lucius doesn't only deceive by lying; he deceives by **arriving too early with true answers.** That's the engine underneath the better-known description of him as "the truth that deceives."

## Relationships

- **[[Roy]]** — the self Sir Rabbit is a picture of: the seeker before the split.
- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — the second rabbit; the counterfeit guide who intercepts the seeker at the threshold.
- **[[Ballerina Boy]]** — the buried child beneath the searching self.
- **[[Mary]]** — present in the poem as the dove, the guide who gives light and releases.

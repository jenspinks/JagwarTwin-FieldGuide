# Sir Rabbit

> **One-line reading.** Sir Rabbit is the seeker before the mask — Roy's picture of himself as a searching, uncertain self, still open, still teachable, before any guide hardens into [[Sir Lucius]].

He matters because he shows the self before Lucius climbs into the passenger seat.

Picture a rabbit driving down a highway at night, looking for something he can't name. That's the whole figure in one image: not a hero, not a villain, just a self in motion with no fixed identity yet — *"I do not know… but I must be someone."* Roy has put this rabbit on the record twice, and called him, in his own words, *"Sir Rabbit pre-Lucius."* That gloss is the page's whole thesis: the rabbit is the self *before* the protector-mind hardens into a guide.

What makes him worth a page of his own is timing. Every other figure in this world arrives *after* something has gone wrong — the mask, the mirror, the shame-voice. Sir Rabbit is the one snapshot we have of the self with the wound but without the armor: forgetful, searching, heart-recovering, and — crucially — still teachable. He is the moment before the second rabbit slides into the seat.

A small but load-bearing note up front: this is the **[[Roy]]-self at an earlier stage**, not a third character standing beside Roy and Lucius. In the grounded dream, *Roy* is the rabbit and *Lucius* is the **second** rabbit. So "pre-Lucius" means the self *before* the split — not a baby Lucius. Hold that and the rest follows.

## At a glance

- **Role** — Roy's initiatory self-image: the seeker before fixed identity, the self the childhood wound leaves walking around.
- **Start with** — [[Good Time (1-08)]] (the dream where he becomes a rabbit) · the *Sir Rabbit* poem (the full initiation) · [[Ballerina Boy]] (the buried child underneath).
- **Core conflict** — a searching self with no name has to find a real guide before a counterfeit one ([[Sir Lucius]]) takes the seat first.
- **Key symbols** — [[The Rabbit]] · the heartbeat · the violet moon · the fox's box · the spider · the dove · the Mourning Sun.
- **Confidence** — grounded fact (Roy's two rabbit texts and his "pre-Lucius" gloss) plus a strong interpretive reading of the rabbit as the seeker-pole.

## Why this figure matters

Most of the Jagwar Twin world is about what happens *after* the self has been intercepted — the mask that hardens, the mirror that traps, the voice that tells you you're a failure and is technically never wrong. Sir Rabbit is the rarest thing in the catalogue: a clean look at the self *before* any of that. He carries the wound but not yet the armor.

That's why his defining line lands the way it does. The Spider asks him point-blank, *"Who are you, Rabbit? And where does You come from?"* — and the rabbit answers, with total honesty, *"I do not know… but I must be someone."* That is the entire condition of the seeker in one couplet: empty of a fixed identity, but not empty of the conviction that there is a self to find. He is directionless and intact at the same time. The work is whether he gets a real guide before a fake one finds him first.

## The two rabbit texts

Roy has put the rabbit on the record in exactly two places, and the grounded claim is modest and clean: he pictures himself as a rabbit, across two texts. Reading that rabbit as *the seeker-self* — the Roy-pole before the split — is interpretation, but it sits very close to both texts.

**The poem (2022).** A long initiation poem Roy published on his own Hall of Mirrors platform — a rabbit in *"a garden full of sound"* who takes a walk to count the stars and gets pulled into a full cosmic journey: a strange cat, a violet moon that turns out to be a machine, a fox with a box, a forgotten heartbeat, a dove with a lamp, a grinning spider at the Divide, and a leap *"straight through the Mourning Sun"* that lands him *"back where he begun."* Roy filed it under origins (more on that below) and later glossed it himself: *"this looks sir rabbit pre-lucius."*

**The dream (2023).** Around the release of [[Good Time (1-08)]], Roy described a dream he'd had — and it's worth telling with the comedy intact, because the cosmic initiation literally begins as a celebrity bit. Roy leaves a party, a white minivan pulls up, and *"guess who's in the white minivan? It's Drake, naturally."* Drake gives him some fame advice (*"things I wish people would have told me when I started"*), then gets out and says *"it's your turn to drive."* The wheel changes hands and the dream proper begins. *"During this whole thing, I'm a rabbit. Somehow I know that."* He's driving, *"looking for something, but I didn't know what I was looking for,"* when a figure drops into the passenger seat — also a rabbit, *"but he's got like clown makeup on, and he says, I'm Sir Lucius… I'm here to help you."* The clown-makeup rabbit has all the answers and is factually right about every turn, *"but I had a weird feeling in my heart… he was telling the truth, but he was manipulating me in some way. And I couldn't explain why."* The dream ends in an involuntary crash — *"a white semi truck with a lightning bolt comes — boom, hits my car"* — and Roy wakes at 1:08. The title is the clock-time.

So the rabbit isn't a young Lucius. He's the self that exists *before* Lucius arrives, which is exactly why the counterfeit guide can intercept him.

## A strong reading: the authentic path and the intercepted path

Put the two texts side by side and the same journey is being told twice — once the right way, once hijacked. This is the page's central interpretive claim, and it's a strong one.

- **The poem is the authentic arc.** A real initiation, endured in order: the rabbit is carried up, trapped, forced inward, recovers his own heartbeat, is given light, is named at the threshold, and *chooses* the leap. He returns transformed — *"back where he begun,"* same starting point, different self.
- **The dream is the intercepted arc.** Same seeker, same directionless searching — but the *wrong guide arrives first.* Lucius takes the guide's seat before any real initiation has happened. He's factually right and emotionally wrong, and the rabbit's heart catches the manipulation before his mind can explain it. There's no chosen leap. There's a lightning-crash he didn't choose.

The cleanest way to hold the pair: **the poem shows the seeker's true initiation; the dream shows that same seeker intercepted at the threshold by the wrong guide.** One ends in a leap; the other ends in a wreck. (The crash, eerily, later showed up in waking life — a semi rear-ended Roy on the freeway. He noted he'd been listening to BTS right before, *"surely has nothing to do with the story."* Dry. And the crash is also a bit: the real truck driver climbs out, *first* worried about the wrong thing — *"oh no, I can't have TMZ coming here… you're a celebrity, right?"* Roy answers *"everyone's a celebrity,"* the driver squints at him and tries again — *"are you Russian?"* The initiation's most violent beat is delivered as deadpan.)

## The wound underneath

Sir Rabbit doesn't come from nowhere. He's what the childhood wound produces. The [[Ballerina Boy]] is the buried *content* — the embodied child told *"you're not a real boy"* and pushed down. Sir Rabbit is the searching *self* that results: identity-vacancy walking around, directionless but intact, still capable of taking a leap. And the armor that eventually forms over him is [[Sir Lucius]] / [[The Protector]].

The line runs: **wound → buried child → searching rabbit → armor.** Sir Rabbit is the third link — the one most easy to miss, because by the time anyone's looking, the armor's already on.

## The poem's small cast

The reason the poem matters beyond its prettiness is that it hands the world a *cast* that isn't reducible to Roy and Lucius. Each figure does one specific thing to the rabbit. Here's the scannable version:

| Figure | What happens | What it suggests |
|---|---|---|
| **The Cat** | A *"very strange Cat"* carries the rabbit up to the moon for lunch — they *"eat till half past two / With Seals and candles burning,"* the moon-machine's *"funny ears that always kept it clean"* | A psychopomp with a comic streak: the strange escort who lifts the seeker out of the ordinary world, then sits him down to a cosmic business lunch (candles, seals, and all) |
| **The Violet Moon** | Beautiful violet moon in the sky; on arrival it's *a machine* with *"whirling gears and funny ears"* | Mechanized reflection — charm first, apparatus revealed second (the exact shape of Lucius) |
| **The Fox** | Offers to teach *"The Game of Many Things,"* invites the rabbit into a box — the lid *slams shut* | A trickster who traps you… into yourself. Manipulative in form, medicinal in result |
| **The heartbeat** | Inside the box, the rabbit recovers a forgotten pulse — *"a heartbeat he forgot"* | The key to the *"other mind"*; the part of you the machine can't generate |
| **The Dove** | Waits in an emerald garden tree with a lamp, gives light, then *releases* the rabbit | A guide who helps and lets go — light without a leash |
| **The Spider** | Grinning, golden-caped, at *"The Divide"*; poses the name question, names the dreaming, offers the leap | The threshold-keeper; selfhood is a *name* problem before it's anything else |
| **The Mourning Sun** | The rabbit *chooses* to leap *"straight through the Mourning Sun"* and returns *"back where he begun"* | The passage home — transformed return, not a reset |

Three of these figures do enough work that they're worth pulling out of the table.

### The Fox — not every trickster is Lucius

The Fox is the figure that keeps the cosmology honest. He's *"ever cunning,"* he baits the rabbit into a box and slams the lid — and then it turns out the box was the point. *"The key is hidden in yourself,"* he says. *"Just listen for the knock."* Trapped and sulking, the rabbit eventually hears the forgotten heartbeat behind the wall, knocks, and a door appears, unlocked. The Fox even arrives carrying **bread** — real provision a machine can't make.

So the Fox is **manipulative in form but medicinal in result.** Which means the world has a kind of cunning that *heals* — and that's load-bearing: **not every trickster is Lucius.** The contrast is exact. The Fox is manipulative-form / medicinal-result. Lucius is helpful-form / parasitic-result. One traps you into your own heartbeat; the other offers help and quietly feeds on you. Tell them apart by what they *produce*, not by how they behave on the way in.

### The Dove — a Mary-style guide, not Mary

The Dove waits in an emerald garden tree, holds up a lamp so the rabbit can see — and then, gently, sends him on: *"As much as I love company you must be on your way."* That's help that doesn't trap you in devotion, which is the precise opposite of clinging Lucius. It reads as a [[Mary]]-*function* — a light-bearer who transmits and releases rather than catching and holding.

The caution matters here: this is a **Mary-register**, not the named Mary, and not "the clearest Mary in the catalogue." A guide who gives light and lets go *resembles* Mary's gesture without being her. Hold the resemblance; don't collapse it into an identity.

### The Spider — selfhood as a name problem

At *"The Divide,"* a grinning, golden-caped Spider stops the rabbit with the question the whole figure turns on: *"Who are you, Rabbit? And where does You come from?"* The rabbit's honest *"I do not know… but I must be someone"* is the seeker's entire condition. The Spider's reply reframes everything — *"You are someone asleep in bed, you're dreaming all the time"* — and the rabbit snaps to: *"I must wake up… I must wake up tonight!"*

What makes the Spider a *true* guide is what he refuses to do. He names himself a guide (*"You're here at The Divide / I'm here to be your guide"*), he leads the rabbit to the edge — and then, asked *"is this a test? I'm not ready to fly!"*, he hands the choice back: *"that's up to you, it's not for my to pry."* He preserves the seeker's sovereignty. He won't make the leap *for* the rabbit. That refusal is exactly what the counterfeit guide will not do — Lucius answers everything *for* you.

## The Lucius mechanism this clarifies

Taken together, the rabbit texts pin down *how* Lucius actually works — and it's sharper than the better-known phrase "the truth that deceives." Lucius is the counterfeit guide who gives true answers **without the heartbeat.** He occupies the guide-seat *before* the initiation has been endured. The cleanest formulation:

> **Lucius doesn't only deceive by lying; he deceives by arriving too early with true answers.**

That's the engine underneath everything Lucius does. The poem shows what a real guide costs you — the box, the silence, the forgotten heartbeat you have to find yourself, the leap nobody can make for you. The dream shows the shortcut: a guide who skips all of it, hands you the correct turns, and lets your heart go quietly wrong while your mind nods along. Same destination words, opposite road. (This is *complementary* to the "truth that deceives" reading, not a replacement for it.)

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the "other mind" and the heart it's keyed to
> The poem doesn't just trap the rabbit; it tells him *how* to escape, and the instruction refines the heart/mind split the whole project runs on. A voice on the far side of the box says: *"Now you must use your other mind, dear Rabbit, it's been mapped."* The key to that other mind is the **heartbeat** — the rabbit waits in silence, hears *"a heartbeat he forgot,"* knocks over the spot, and the door opens.
>
> So the formula sharpens. A mind *severed* from the heart becomes Lucius. A mind *returned through* the heartbeat becomes the *"other mind."* This isn't heart *against* mind — it's mind *reoriented through the pulse it forgot.* And *"it's been mapped"* is doing quiet work: the faculty was always there. The rabbit isn't building a new mind; he's remembering one. (Call it anamnesis — knowledge by recollection.) The same seed-doctrine shows up in the Fox's line *"the key is hidden in yourself / just listen for the knock,"* which is Roy's later maze-password *"you are the key" / "you are it"* stated years early.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the fox's box as the missing fourth container
> The world's broken-vessel logic ([[Keilim - The Breaking of the Vessels]]) usually sorts containers into a few kinds: the prison that seals the self away, and the husk that's form without seed. The Fox's box is a *third* thing — the missing **initiatory container.** Not a prison and not a husk, but an **interiorization chamber**: an enclosure that isolates the self just long enough to hear the forgotten heart, then opens.
>
> The catch is diagnostic. Boxes *look alike* from the outside — the lid slams shut on the rabbit exactly as a trap would. The difference isn't the enclosure; it's what the enclosure *produces.* Severance, or the heartbeat. That's the same test that separates the Fox from Lucius, scaled up to cosmology: you can't read a container by its shape, only by its fruit.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — Loop vs Circle, and the Mourning Sun
> The poem ends with the rabbit landing *"back where he begun"* — and that line is the sharpest statement of a distinction the work cares about a lot ([[The Circle]]). A **Loop** is return *without* transformation: the same point, the same self, nothing learned. A **Circle** is return *with memory restored*: the same point, but the subject has changed. Sir Rabbit returns to his starting place *after* the heartbeat, the Dove, the Spider, the Divide, and the chosen leap — so his return is a Circle, not a Loop. The geography is identical; the traveler isn't.
>
> One spelling flag worth keeping. The leap is through the *"Mourning Sun"* — transcribed as written from the artifact. If that spelling is intentional (and not a transcription slip), the ending reads as *grief-as-solar-passage* rather than a happy dawn, and the mourning/**morning** pun points straight at the **Morning Star** — Lucifer — Lucius. The rabbit passes *through* the morning sun and comes home. Spelling-dependent, so flagged, not asserted.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the child-knight, the White Knight, and the maze cluster
> The poem travels with a small visual cluster found in the Hall of Mirrors. One open-leaf image is a photo of **young Roy** in toy plate-armor with a dragon shield, sitting on a pony. It reads as the *biographical genesis* of [[The Protector]] — the survival-self armoring up *before* it calcifies into Lucius. The careful version: the maze *selected a childhood-armor image that already holds the armoring structure.* It is **not** "Roy staged this as Lucius." The resonance is structural, not authored. (As the lab framing puts it: a child in a knight costume is also just a kid playing.)
>
> There's a literary rhyme here too. Carroll's **White Knight** — the protective-but-absurd guide who saves Alice and then insists on reciting "the name of the song" — chimes with both the toy knight *and* with the poem's proto-guides (Cat, Fox, Dove, Spider). It's a lineage note, not an authorial claim. And the poem itself is unusually well-sourced: it survives both on the crown-gated room pages *and* as three consecutive iPhone-Notes screenshots that are themselves open maze rooms, all matching the text verbatim — so it's triple-sourced, not single-witness.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — Roy's "silly rabbit" register and the food chain

>

(Same night he glossed the poem as "pre-lucius," he also dropped *"I can pull a rabbit out of a hat"* alongside a *"Rudolph Steiner rabbit hole with the Gospel of Luke"* — the name-research and the rabbit imagery bundled into one evening. How the Steiner/Luke thread bears on the rabbit specifically is a documented research vein, not a stated equation.) The 2026 iconography grounds it further: *"The year begins with Janus and the March Hare,"* and a gematria aside resolving to a rabbit emoji.

## Source notes

- **Grounded.** Roy pictures himself as a rabbit in two public texts: the *Sir Rabbit* poem (published 2022 on his own Hall of Mirrors platform, in the GENESIS/origin collection) and the [[Good Time (1-08)]] dream he narrated publicly (2023), in which he says *"I'm a rabbit"* and Lucius is the *second*, clown-makeup rabbit. Roy's own gloss — *"this looks sir rabbit pre-lucius"* — is the documented basis for reading the rabbit as the self *before* Lucius, and he dated the figure's debut himself: *"sir lucius first appeared in the video and artwork for good time 108."* The poem's text (the cat, the violet-moon machine, the fox's box, the recovered heartbeat, the dove, the spider at the Divide, the leap *"back where he begun"*) is quoted from the artifact's displayed pages. The Spider's companion maze-room (*"I'M THE SPIDER,"* code *"the answers in a name"*) is a solved, grounded artifact.
- **Strong reading.** That the rabbit *is* the seeker-pole — the [[Roy]]-self before the split — is interpretation, though it fits both texts closely. So is the central pair: the poem as the *authentic* initiation and the dream as the *intercepted* one. So is the Lucius mechanism it clarifies ("arriving too early with true answers"), the wound → buried child → searching rabbit lineage, and the Fox-vs-Lucius contrast.
- **Framework.** The Dove-as-Mary-function, the "other mind" keyed to the heartbeat, the fox's box as a fourth container-type, the Loop-vs-Circle reading, the Mourning/Morning-Star pun, and the child-knight/White-Knight resonances are interpretive frameworks laid over the material — folded into the Advanced reading callouts above and labelled as readings, not claims. The spelling of *"Mourning Sun"* is transcribed as-shown and flagged for verification.
- **Register guard.** This page deliberately does **not** say "Roy *is* Sir Rabbit," and does **not** treat Sir Rabbit as a third character peer to Roy, Lucius, and Mary. He's the Roy-register seeker at an earlier stage.

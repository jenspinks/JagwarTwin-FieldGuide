# Ballerina Boy

> **One-line reading.** Ballerina Boy is the buried child — the stage-joyful, undefended self that went into the ground at seven, and the one the whole arc is trying to dig back up.

> [!quote] The reframe this page turns on
> Ballerina Boy is the buried child-self — the original expressive light, hidden after shame made visibility feel dangerous. He is not dead, but *preserved* underground. The central question is not whether he existed, but whether the system can become safe enough for him to return.

In the language of the inner system, he is **the exile** — the most vulnerable, most feeling part, sealed away because being seen once cost too much. Everything else the arc builds — the protector, the searcher, the grown interpreter — is organized around the fact of him being down there. Keep that tenderness in view: this is the sincere wound at the center, the thing the machinery was built to guard, not a bit to riff on.

## At a glance

- **Role** — the origin/light layer of the [[Jagwar Twin]] system, and its **exile**: the wounded, feeling child every other figure is a response to.
- **Start with** — the song [[Ballerina Boy]] and [[Roy]]'s page, where this figure is the founding injury.
- **Core conflict** — he was not just hurt; he was told his very being was wrong, which forced [[Roy]] to bury him alive and build [[Sir Lucius]] over the grave.
- **Key symbols** — [[The Moon]] (light that survives the dark) · the stage (where he came alive) · the dance (the buried, feminine-coded joy).
- **Confidence** — grounded autobiography (the wound is real and publicly told) braided with strong interpretation (his role as the system's origin).

## Why this figure matters

Most of the Jagwar Twin cast are masks, voices, or faculties. Ballerina Boy is a person — specifically, the seven-year-old [[Roy]] used to be, before the world taught him there was something wrong with being him. He is the *content* every other figure is built to protect, hide, weaponize, or rescue. [[Sir Lucius]] is the armor; [[Roy]] is the grown man deciding whether to go back for him; [[Mary]] is the voice that blesses him. Ballerina Boy is the thing all that machinery is *about*.

He's also the rare figure here whose backstory is not mythology. The wound is literal autobiography, told publicly by [[Roy]] outside the lyric, and triple-confirmed: in the song itself, in a sit-down interview, and — independently — in a concert-venue artist bio that simply notes he *"embraced ballet as a child while his peers pursued sports."* When the work feels heaviest, it is usually because it has circled back to this kid.

And here is the quiet, devastating fact that the whole arc turns on: the child's own response to being shamed was not to cry it out and move on. It was to perform an execution. *"I killed that ballerina boy / I buried him inside."* A seven-year-old buried himself, and everything after — the masks, the protector, the depression, the search — grew out of that grave.

## The wound, in his own sequence

[[Roy]] released this story himself, in order, both inside the song and out of it. Age seven, a Christian school, ballet — *"I came alive when I was on that stage."* Then rumors; a teacher calls his father: *"We think your son is gay."* The boy overhears (*"I overheard cause he was mad"*) and reasons his way to the cruelest possible conclusion — *"Gay must mean bad or else they wouldn't call"* — and prays *"to God to make me good cause it wasn't my fault."* He is the only boy in the class; he stands *"back against the wall"* while the girls surround him with the chant that becomes the project's founding wound: *"you're not a real boy."*

In the interview telling, the picture is even more disarming: *"a little blonde bowl cut… tights, ballet shoes, tap shoes,"* the only boy in the room, who came home crying *"I'm never gonna dance again."* The comic tenderness of tiny tap-shoe Roy is exactly what makes the next part land — because the shame, he says, got *"pushed so deep into the subconscious you don't know where it came from."*

His father gives the one piece of love that's available: *"there's nothing wrong with you, my son."* It is real, and it is not enough — for reasons the song quietly tracks, and which the **Two blessings** reading below takes apart. The boy does the thing instead. He buries himself. *"I never danced again."*

## What he is, as a figure

Strip the biography down to a function, and Ballerina Boy is the **embodied, undefended, stage-alive self** — softness, play, feminine-coded expression, creativity that hasn't yet learned to protect itself. He is what a person is *before* they learn to perform a version of themselves that the world will stop punishing.

The wound is not that he was hurt. It's that he was told he was *bad* — at a Christian school, where social shame curdles into moral and spiritual shame. *"You're not a real boy"* doesn't critique what he did; it attacks what he *is*. That's the difference between an injury and a verdict, and it's why the response had to be so total. You can recover from being hurt. Being declared incompatible with survival is a different problem — and solving it is what forces the psyche to manufacture a protector.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the buried child produces *two* figures, not one
> The wound doesn't only make a grave; it sends two figures out of it. **Ballerina Boy** is the buried *content* — the child himself, interred. **[[Sir Rabbit]]** is the searching *self* that results: identity-vacant but intact, the one who says, in effect, *"I do not know who I am, but I must be someone,"* and goes looking. The full genealogy runs: wound → buried child (Ballerina Boy) → searching rabbit ([[Sir Rabbit]]) → armor ([[Sir Lucius]]). One injury, three downstream figures, each a different stage of the same unfinished sentence.

## The grave is the origin of everything else

The load-bearing reading of the figure, and it reframes the entire cast: **[[Sir Lucius]] is not the ballerina boy, he's what grows around the grave.** When softness is buried as unsurvivable, the psyche still has to live, so it builds the opposite of softness: strategy, performance, control, mockery-before-being-mocked, power-before-vulnerability. That armor is Lucius, formed at the precise moment the child went into the ground.

Which means the relationship between them is gentler than it first looks. He is not *destroyed* by [[Sir Lucius]] so much as *hidden behind* him. Lucius is the guard at the door of the wound — severe, clever, mocking, often cruel, but originally built to keep the most vulnerable part from being shamed again. That reframes the whole quest. The arc is not *"defeat Lucius and free the boy."* It is: **earn enough trust that the protector no longer has to bury what he loves.** You do not storm the door. You make the room behind it safe enough that the guard can finally stand down — because the thing he is guarding was always the thing he loved most.

Two claims here, and they sit at different confidence levels so, worth keeping apart. That the burial *produced* Lucius is interpretation; the song never names him. But that Lucius is *invested* in keeping this grave closed is grounded fact, and it's the single most telling piece of evidence on the page. In a theatrical episode of the video series, Lucius mimes a little ballet move at the camera and sneers: *"Do you know what your lowest-performing song of all time is? Ballerina Boy. When you're vulnerable you LOSE!"*

That isn't random cruelty. A shadow who fact-checks your streaming numbers to wound you, the inner critic as a guy with a Spotify-for-Artists dashboard, is dark humor... but the comedy is doing serious work. Lucius mocks exactly *one* song by name, and it's this one. If [[Roy]] rescues the buried child, Lucius loses his monopoly on protection. A figure with a demonstrable stake in keeping a specific grave closed tells you what's buried there matters most.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the same light, differently oriented
> The tightest single-sentence version of the genesis reading lives on the [[Weirdness]] essay: Lucius is *"the split-off radiance of the buried child — the same light that went underground at seven years old — operating under the wrong orientation."* That's the key move. Lucius isn't the *opposite* of Ballerina Boy; he's the **same light** bent. The child's stage-radiance, sealed off and re-tasked as armor, lit by fear instead of joy. It's also why [[Roy]] can say of the protector that *"the deal may have been worth it"* — Lucius *"genuinely held the buried light"* through years [[Roy]] couldn't. The protector kept the light alive by keeping it imprisoned. (See [[The Dweller on the Threshold]] for the mechanism by which a buried self accretes a guardian.)

## The loop — and why the answer isn't success

The structure of the song is the argument. Verse one is the schoolyard; verse two leaps forward to seventeen, when [[Roy]] *"started a band and I was on a mission"* — and the childhood chant survives the jump, transposed into the music industry. *"Driving 'round the country / couldn't afford an egg McMuffin / music just wasn't landing"* — and there it is again, the same verdict in a new uniform: *"you're not a real boy."* Then it escalates into the open existential question: *"What makes a real boy?"*

That egg McMuffin is the whole figure in one detail. A man stands at the most extreme fork imaginable — and the emblem of rock-bottom he reaches for is that he couldn't afford a fast-food breakfast. It's genuinely funny and genuinely devastating at once: cosmic stakes measured in hash browns. The smallness is the point. Humiliation scaled down to breakfast is still humiliation.

Same mechanism across every venue — schoolyard, religion, label, algorithm, audience, money: *sacrifice the true self so the outer world stops punishing you.* Ballerina Boy is where the exact logic [[Sir Lucius]] later weaponizes in [[Not Your Homie]] and [[Welcome to the Circus]] first appears — before it had a face.

Which is what makes the fork's resolution so pointed. *"Do I kill myself or come back for that kid I let go."* Notice the choice [[Roy]] does *not* offer himself: he never asks *do I become successful?* He asks *do I come back for that kid?* The chosen answer is **integration, not achievement** — it goes inward first, the exact inverse of a bargain song that says win the outer game and maybe feel something later. *"This song's for Ballerina boy / This song's a prayer for hope."* The song isn't a report of a rescue. It *is* the rescue act — a ritual object, a prayer with the kid's name in it.

But integration is not demolition, and this is the exile's hardest problem. The armor that buried him is the same armor that kept him — and the person — alive. So the whole arc can be compressed into a single question, and it is a tender one:

> [!quote] The master sentence
> *How can the buried light be allowed to flow without destroying the structure that kept it alive?*

That is why the answer can't be to win, and can't be to fight. Winning leaves the child underground; fighting the protector attacks the very thing that preserved the child through the years [[Roy]] couldn't hold him. The task is subtler and kinder — to let the light back up *through* the structure, until the structure no longer has to be a wall.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the counter-algorithm, and the joke on Lucius
> The song refuses the performance economy on its own terms — it declines the metric. It risks being *"low-performing"* precisely because its worth isn't denominated in streams. Form and content agree all the way down: the off-album single about the off-album child, kept out of the very spectacle it diagnoses. (The kid who got told he didn't belong made a song that didn't make the tracklist — the structure rhymes so neatly it's almost a bit. *Why* it was kept off the album is not publicly stated, so that's a wink at the irony, not a claimed intent.) And the joke is on Lucius: he calls it the *"lowest-performing"* song, which is the entire point — it loses by the scoreboard because it refuses to play that game. He's wielding the one metric the song was built to ignore. See [[Communion vs Spectacle]] and [[The Audience]].

## The two blessings — and why the deepest one is feminine

The song answers the world's hostility not with armor but with blessing — and it does so twice, in two different voices that are easy to conflate and shouldn't be.

The **father's** blessing is narrated inside the story: *"there's nothing wrong with you, my son."* It's real love, and it fails anyway — because it's stated in the *negative*. It's a pardon that still operates inside the same right/wrong courtroom the teacher and the girls were using. It acquits the charge; it doesn't leave the court. Telling a shamed child *you are not broken* is simply not the same as telling him *you are, exactly as you are, good.*

The **outro** blessing is the other thing. Delivered by an older, motherly female voice, it's *generative* rather than exculpatory: *"You are one of a kind, God's design… so my son, let your light shine… The moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light. There's no one else like you out there."* It doesn't answer an accusation; it claims the child's nature outright. (Both lines address *"my son,"* which makes them easy to hear as one speaker — but they're two voices, and the difference between them is the whole resolution.)

And the deepest blessing arrives in the feminine voice, **exactly where shame tried to erase him.** That placement is the song's quiet thesis: the divine feminine enters, in full, precisely at the site of the wound. Lucius protects by *armoring* — become untouchable. The maternal/divine voice protects by *blessing* — you are already, exactly, right. The arc hinges on that contrast.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the moon, and the Mary-class voice
> The outro's *"the moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light"* may be the most load-bearing symbolic line in the catalogue. The moon **does not generate light — it reflects it.** That's the signature of borrowed, preserved light: not a source, but proof a source exists somewhere past the dark. (See [[The Moon]], which lists this song as the *tender* pole of the symbol — *"preservation-through-darkness."*) The blessing voice is the clearest [[Mary]]-class appearance in the whole body of work, and possibly the earliest chronologically. The female, motherly voice is **grounded** (audio-confirmed); the identification of *that voice* with the named [[Mary]] of [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)|#1 Champion]] is the working assumption, held as a strong read until a casting credit settles it. Note: *who* sings the outro is not credited — present her as *"a motherly female voice,"* not a named performer.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the esoteric registers (framework, not authorial intent)
> The figure sits cleanly inside several interpretive traditions [[Roy]] openly studies. These are correspondences, not claims about what the song "means."
>
> - **Jungian.** Ballerina Boy reads as the **Divine Child** — the true embodied self (with anima elements, not merely the anima). [[Sir Lucius]] is the compensatory protector the psyche manufactures; the parental/lunar voice is the witnessing **Self** that blesses. The deepest wound isn't that the child was hurt — it's that he was made *incompatible with survival*, which is what forces the psyche to build Lucius.
> - **Gnostic.** *"You're not a real boy"* is demiurgic language — a false authority telling the soul its lived truth is invalid, training it to forget itself. The rescue is *gnosis*: remembering what was true before the world misnamed you. (It rhymes with [[Soul Is A Star]]'s *"do you know who you are?"*) See [[Gnosticism - The World as Trap]].
> - **Kabbalah.** The [[Kabbalah - The Tree of Life|Tree]] in miniature: **Malkuth** (body, school, gendered rule, humiliation) → **Yesod** (memory, the moon, the buried image) → **Tiferet** (heart, blessing, *"let your light shine"*). The trauma happens in Malkuth; the buried image *lives* in Yesod; the rescue requires entering the lunar memory-realm without being consumed by it. (The heavier two-mothers / crown apparatus is interpretation, kept off this page; it belongs, hedged, to [[Mary]] and [[The Jaguar]].)
> - **The loop becomes a circle.** Adult [[Roy]] returns for child Roy — while the child was *already* being held by a future-self / divine voice in the outro. The recursion stops being a trap and becomes a developmental circle, rhyming with [[The Circle]]'s *"inside of the inside"* and [[The Thread]]'s retrieved feeling. See [[The Loop and the Circle]].

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the Pinocchio analogue (curated, held loosely)
> *Pinocchio* sits on [[Roy]]'s own [[The I'm 33 Board|board of films]] he says the pattern shows up in — which makes it a **Roy-curated** mythic rhyme, not an inherited template. It gathers all three grounded elements at once: a *made boy* told he is not a *real boy*; a figure defined by *the string* tying him to his maker; and a *feminine voice* that confers realness (the Blue Fairy ↔ the motherly outro, held as the [[Mary]]-function in strict structural rhyme). Discipline matters here: the wound is *also* literal autobiography. The myth coexists; it doesn't replace. The claim is **not** "Ballerina Boy is about Pinocchio." (The string-inversion — *I've Got No Strings* selling severance as freedom, where [[The Circle]] inverts it to *remembering* the held string — lives mainly on the [[Inversion]] page.)

## Relationships

- **[[Roy]]** — Ballerina Boy *is* Roy's buried child self; the age-7 ballet wound is Roy's own autobiography. He's the origin point of Roy's whole arc, and Roy is the one who must *"come back for that kid I let go."* Roy's own open question — *is the buried ballerina boy the same as what [[Mary]] is reaching toward?* — keeps the relationship live.
- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — the protector, and the guard at the door of the wound; the armor formed the moment the child went under. The boy is not destroyed by Lucius but *hidden behind* him. He mocks the song by name to defend his own necessity — yet he *"genuinely held the buried light"* through the years [[Roy]] couldn't. The resolution isn't defeating him; it's earning enough trust that he no longer has to bury what he loves.
- **[[Mary]]** — the divine-feminine blessing voice of the outro; the clearest [[Mary]]-class appearance in the catalogue. Her *"let your light shine"* / *"born to shine"* is the structural *answer* to the shame-voice's *"you're not a real boy."* Whether Mary *is* the one Roy comes back for, or distinct from the buried child, stays open.
- **[[Weirdness]]** — the healing pair. Its faceless feminine voice — *"you were born to shine"* — answers the wound directly, and its dancing child *"in the rain"* maps onto the same pre-shame freedom as the ballerina. It also supplies the tightest statement that Ballerina-Boy-light and Lucius-light are one light, differently oriented.
- **[[Not Your Homie]]** — the opposite pole of one wound: the shadow says *"you don't know me,"* the child says *"come back for me."* To know Lucius, [[Roy]] has to know Ballerina Boy.
- **[[The Moon]]** — his tender symbol: light that survives the night, reflected rather than self-generated; preservation-through-darkness.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the wider web (companions, contrasts, brackets)
> - **[[Happy Face]]** — the coping mechanism the wound installs: *"if you're sad don't let it show"* — the perfect-child armor.
> - **[[Welcome to the Circus]]** — the public spectacle whose private origin is this song. Ballerina Boy is a *structural absence* there: he's never named, with two readings — (1) he's sealed *underneath* the Circus, buried before it was built; (2) he's what the Circus *can't absorb*, his absence his protection. *"The most important figure in the project is not in the song, and the absence is the song's shape."* See [[The Circus]].
> - **[[Move To You]]** — the contrast that completes him: where Ballerina Boy interred the child behind armor, Move To You lets the tender-self narrate in its *own* voice — because reciprocal, non-possessive love finally made the room safe.
> - **[[Soul Is A Star]]** / **[[Another Way II Heaven]]** — the doctrine and the map. *"Do you know who you are?"* is answered autobiographically here; *"one become two… I was still I"* is the metaphysics this song lives out. **[[SOL]]** is the love-current the rescue moves toward — and the song Lucius overrides in the video series.
> - **[[Loser]]** (2018 debut) — the *earliest* anchor. Roy's gloss on the children's choir — *"getting back to that childlike self before people told you you were different… when you were just you, unashamed"* — states the buried-child doctrine **seven years before** this song names it. The catalogue is bracketed by children's voices with inverted functions: 2018's child-chorus is the *recovered* pre-shame self (awakening); the 2026 [[The Circle (Lucius Version)|Circle (Lucius Version)]] child-voice is the *captured* self sung to sleep (sedation). Same device, opposite vector.
> - **[[Jagwar Twin]]** (the container) — the meta-system holds three interior layers: [[Sir Lucius]] (mind/strategy), [[Roy]] (heart/meaning), and Ballerina Boy (**origin/light**). He's named as the project's buried light. (A note on roster bookkeeping: the pre-persona human layer collapses *into* this figure rather than standing apart — origin/light subsumes it, keeping the interior cast lean.)

## The figure in the nested system

The clearest way to hold Ballerina Boy is to place him at the center of the whole nested cast — because everyone else is defined by their relation to him:

- **Ballerina Boy** — the buried **original light**. The exile. The feeling, expressive self that went underground when it was made to feel dangerous.
- **[[Roy]]** — the conscious interpreter, the grown man trying to recover him: *"come back for that kid I let go."*
- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — the protector who hid him, and who guards the door so he can't be shamed again.
- **[[Mary]]** — the blessing that calls him out by name: *"let your light shine… you were born to shine."*
- **[[Jagwar Twin]]** — the container that holds all of them together.

And a distinction worth keeping straight: the **figure is bigger than the song**. The single *[[Ballerina Boy]]* is the *evidence* — the place the wound is stated outright and the name is first spoken — but the buried child it points to runs underneath the entire body of work, in songs that never say his name. Read the song to meet him; read the arc to understand why everything is organized around him.

## Source notes

- **Grounded (autobiography and release).** The ballet wound is real and publicly told by [[Roy]], and it's triple-confirmed: the lyric, an interview telling, and an independent venue artist bio noting childhood ballet. The single released June 13, 2025 (on Big Loud Rock under license to Mercury Records / UMG); it was kept off the *[[Lucius Lullaby (album)|Lucius Lullaby]]* album, which is a fact. Roy frames his adult dance videos as shadow-work to reclaim the buried dancer — so the *rescue* is something he's publicly, bodily doing, not just singing about.
- **Grounded (Lucius's investment).** That [[Sir Lucius]] targets *this* song by name in the video series is on the record. It supports — without proving — the reading that the burial produced him.
- **Strong reading.** That Ballerina Boy is the system's origin/light, that the burial *generated* the protector, that the two blessings are unequal, and that the feminine voice landing at the wound is the song's thesis — these are interpretation, anchored in the lyric and visual lore but not stated outright by the work.
- **Framework / esoteric.** The Jungian, Gnostic, Kabbalistic, lunar, and Pinocchio readings are correspondences drawn from traditions [[Roy]] openly studies — offered as resonance, not as the song's stated meaning. The simple reading — a sincere trauma-confessional that needs no mythology — is valid and sufficient on its own.
- **Open questions.** *Why* the song was kept off the album isn't publicly stated. *Who* sings the outro isn't credited (a *"motherly female voice,"* not a named performer). And the song presses one question on itself: it states the burial outright, yet the adult is plainly a stage creature, and *"come back for that kid"* admits the burial was incomplete — so the child may have been *misidentified* (kept alive as performance, denied recognition as that child) rather than fully buried. Held open, not asserted. See [[Open Questions]].

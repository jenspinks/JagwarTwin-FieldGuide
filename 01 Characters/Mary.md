# Mary

> **One-line reading.** Mary is the divine-feminine voice in the Jagwar Twin world — a faceless blessing-presence that appears where shame tried to erase the self and says, *you were born to shine.*

> [!infobox] Mary
> - **Also known as** — La Conquistadora · Queen of California · the faceless angel · the blessing-voice
> - **Nature** — the divine feminine; a source, not a reflection
> - **Register** — lunar · maternal · seeing-without-consuming
> - **First heard** — *Ballerina Boy* (the motherly spoken outro)
> - **Named once** — *#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)*, *Lucius Lullaby* (2026)
> - **Key symbols** — the hidden face · [[The Moon]] · the halo · the gun
> - **Related** — [[Roy]] · [[Sir Lucius]] · [[The Rose]] · [[The Moon]]
> - **Appears in** — [[Ballerina Boy]] · [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] · [[Weirdness]]

If [[Sir Lucius]] is the voice that tells you you're a failure, Mary is the voice that tells you who you actually are. She's the counter-frequency to the shame-voice — and unlike Lucius, she doesn't need an audience and doesn't change shape depending on who's watching. She's the same in every room.

A guardrail before we go further: *Mary* is this guide's name for a recurring **pattern**, not a fixed character the work keeps naming. She's spoken aloud exactly once. The rest of the time she's a shape you learn to recognize — a face out of sight, a blessing that calls someone back to themselves. Not every female voice in the catalogue is symbolic Mary, and the work doesn't always agree to be tidy about it. Read her as a *figure* and a *function* before you read her as a person.

## At a glance

- **Role** — the divine-feminine presence the work keeps reaching toward; the structural answer to the shame-voice.
- **Start with** — [[Ballerina Boy]] (hear the voice), then [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] (the one time she's named), then [[Weirdness]].
- **Core conflict** — where [[Sir Lucius]] traps you in the *image*, Mary *transmits* the source-light; she's the one thing his system can't absorb.
- **Key symbols** — the hidden face · the halo · [[The Moon]] · the gun · the white rose · [[The Rose]].
- **Confidence** — grounded fact (one named, faceless, armed appearance) braided with strong interpretation and an openly framework-tier mystical reading.

## Why this figure matters

Every wound in the Jagwar Twin world traces back to a sentence said over a child: *you're not a real boy.* That's the [[Ballerina Boy]] cut — a kid who dances, told there's something wrong with him. The whole project is, in one sense, the long work of answering that sentence. [[Sir Lucius]] answers it the wrong way — he agrees with the shame, dresses it in logic, and offers protection in exchange for obedience. Mary answers it the right way. She doesn't argue with the shame; she *cancels* it. *Stop pretending. You were born to shine.*

That's the human stakes in a single move. Lucius tells you what you've failed to be. Mary tells you what you already are. One is a verdict; the other is a blessing. The reason she matters so much to the architecture is that she's the only force in the world that the shame-machine cannot metabolize — you'll see exactly why below, and it's funnier and stranger than you'd expect.

## The marker: a face out of sight

Mary's signature is almost embarrassingly consistent: a feminine presence whose **face is hidden**, paired with a blessing meant to call someone home to themselves. Three appearances carry the bulk of it.

In [[Ballerina Boy]], the entire spoken outro is an older, **motherly** voice — audibly, unmistakably maternal. The blessing is generous and complete: *"you are one of a kind, God's design… so my son, let your light shine… the moon reminds us that even in the darkness there is light… there's no one else like you out there."* It's the clearest divine-feminine moment in the catalogue, and likely the earliest one chronologically. Notice the grammar later — it matters.

In [[Weirdness]], she's nearly an apparition: *"she looked like an angel, baby / but her face was out of sight,"* and her line is the same gnosis-in-three-words: *"Stop pretending, baby / you were born to shine."* And here's a quiet structural tell — that pre-chorus arrives **twice**, once after the childhood wound and once after the industry wound. She's not a single vision that fades. She's persistent. She keeps showing up at the exact moments a self is about to be erased.

And in [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]], she's named outright for the only time in the whole body of work — and the line that names her does it through a nursery rhyme: *"Mary, Mary, so contrary / Extraordinary, she was scary when she walked by."* That's the single most direct fact on this whole page — the lyric that actually says her name — and it arrives riding Mother Goose's *"Mary, Mary, quite contrary."* (More on why the nursery rhyme is load-bearing below.) The same verse gives her a face we still can't see: *"Saw a halo but I didn't see a head."* A halo and a body, holiness and presence, with no face in between. That line is the master key to the entire figure, so it gets its own section.

> [!note] Source note
> [[Ballerina Boy]] is a grounded female voice (audio-confirmed); reading her as *Mary* is a strong interpretation. [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] is the grounded anchor — she's named *and* faceless right there in the lyric. [[Weirdness]] is a strong read (faceless feminine = Mary-class).

## "Saw a halo but I didn't see a head"

This is the line the whole figure pivots on, and it's a diagnosis. The narrator can read a **halo** (status, holiness, the sign of the sacred) and he can read a **dress** and a body (desire, surface, the usable image). What he cannot read is the **head** — the face, the person, the presence that refuses to collapse into something he can consume. He has exactly one instrument, the consuming eye, and it works on everything except the one thing that matters.

The visualizer makes the same confession even more nakedly. Roy's public reel for the song carries a caption signed *— El Roy* ([[Public Posts]]): *"I saw her divinity, but not the one who held it, or where it led. I followed anyway, white rose in my left hand, and stepped off the ledge."* Same failure, twice stated: he perceives the holy *sign* but not the *person who bears it*. He sees the divinity and not the one holding it.

So Mary's facelessness isn't coyness. It's the whole point. She is the presence that cannot be turned into a surface — and the failure to see her is a failure of the seer, not of her strength.

## She cannot be surveilled

Here's where it gets sharp. The spectacle-self comes to Mary the only way it knows how: looking for a surface that returns his own reflection — recognition, conquest, a face to flatter or to win. And she returns **nothing**. No face to charm. No reflection to claim. She *sees* and is not seen-and-flattered.

This is [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]] dramatized in four lines. Surveillance is the look that ranks and consumes; witnessing is the look that holds and loves. Mary is pure witness — she's the one who watches without taking — and when the consuming eye meets a presence it cannot consume, the self that knows no other way to look simply... ends there. She's the antidote to the watched-and-ranked world precisely because she offers nothing to rank.

That's also why she's the one thing [[Sir Lucius]]'s system can't absorb. Lucius runs on reflection — he needs watchers, he changes shape per audience, he's a mirror that catches the light and holds it. Mary doesn't reflect; she **transmits**. She passes the source-light *through* rather than trapping it, and she's identical in every room. A mirror can't do anything with a window. (For the underlying mechanism, see [[Source vs Reflection]].)

## The armed Madonna

If you came expecting the meek handmaid, [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] has news. This Mary is *"La Conquistadora with a gun in her hand… mopping up the flora, fawning over no man."* She's *"such a revolutionary."* She's *"Queen of California on the corner doing her time."* Militant, self-possessed, and supremely uninterested in any man in the room — the Virgin Mary, armed, looking like she'd win a bar fight.

There's real theology under the swagger. *La Conquistadora* — "Our Lady of the Conquest" — is a genuine Marian title; the oldest Marian statue in the United States, carried in Santa Fe, bears that name. *Queen of California* echoes the Marian *Queen of Heaven*. Those are verifiable facts about the titles. Whether the song deliberately repurposes them is the interpretation — but the **Marian resonance is unmistakable**: the names she's given carry the Virgin's heraldry.

And notice the title chain the album builds: **Champion → Conquistador → Conquistadora.** The anthem of conquest arrives, at last, at *Our Lady of the Conquest.* The conqueror gets conquered. Mary isn't the prize the champion wins. She's the force that defeats the prize-seeker.

> [!note] Source note
> The lyrics (*"gun in her hand," "fawning over no man," "Queen of California"*) are grounded. That *La Conquistadora* and *Queen of Heaven* are real Marian titles is a verifiable public fact. That the song repurposes them deliberately is a strong reading.

## The nursery-rhyme ambush

Here's the trick the song plays on the ear. Mary doesn't enter on a hymn or a fanfare. She enters on a **Mother Goose rhyme**: *"Mary, Mary, so contrary"* is *"Mary, Mary, quite contrary"* with one word swapped — the schoolyard sing-song every listener already has by heart. That's not decoration; it's the album's whole **method**. *Lucius Lullaby* keeps smuggling the sacred in through twisted children's forms — *Row, Row, Row Your Boat* in *"life is but a dream,"* *Ring-a-Ring-a-Rosies* in *"ashes, ashes,"* the Oompa-Loompa song re-voiced as a threat. The nursery rhyme is the Trojan horse: the sacred enters dressed as something you'd dismiss as cute.

So watch what gets stacked into one breath. *"Mary, Mary, so contrary"* (a nursery rhyme) → *"La Conquistadora with a gun in her hand"* (a militarized saint) → *"Queen of California on the corner doing her time"* (a crown in the gutter). Crown, gutter, and a Mother Goose rhyme in the same gesture. The figure who unseats the champion is introduced in the register of a kindergarten chant — which is exactly why he never sees her coming. (Hold that thought; the chant turns out to be the weapon.)

## The chorus is hers — and that's the joke

Listen closely to [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] and a delicious structural prank reveals itself. The **entire chorus is Mary's voice** — the count (*"A-B-C, count 'em, one, two, three"*), the verdict (*"you got a little bit of money, but it don't come cheap"*), the *"know 'bout me,"* even the title line. The "champion" has **no line of his own.** Lucius can only enter by climbing onto *her* voice for *"number one champion,"* then drowning her out, and ends up finishing the title **alone, a cappella, after the music has stopped** — a braggart left boasting to an empty room. He wins the crown by silencing the person actually singing it. The appropriation *is* the unseating, and it's enacted in the mix, not just argued.

Two more things fall out of this. First: that *"me"* in *"know 'bout me"* is **Mary**, not the champion. Second, the structural hinge — the verse breaks off mid-sentence, *"made a move like a fool, dropped me dead when she said—"* and cuts straight into the chorus in her voice. So *what she said* is the count. The line wasn't a withheld blank; it was a **hand-off**. He's dropped dead by what comes next, and what comes next is a woman calmly counting to three.

Which is the best joke in the whole catalogue: the champion doesn't die to a weapon or a curse. He dies to **multiplication tables.** *A-B-C, one-two-three* — cosmic creation-grammar delivered like a kindergarten chant. (Why the count is lethal is worth its own unpacking; see the Advanced reading below.)

And then the trap springs on its own bait. In the visual series ([[The Interview]], EP7), [[Sir Lucius]] forces Roy to sit and listen to the album, hits this song, recognizes the boast, and proudly claims it: *"another song about ME."* He's so busy enjoying the chorus that he never hears the verse where Mary drops the character dead. The ego identifies with the anthem — and the anthem is the thing that executes the self that couldn't resist identifying with it. He claims the crown in the exact line where he loses it. The song works *because* the self it indicts cannot keep its hands off it.

## The two blessings of Ballerina Boy

[[Ballerina Boy]] hides a careful distinction that's easy to miss on first listen, and it's the cleanest evidence for who Mary is. There are **two** blessings in the song, in **two different voices, with two different grammars.**

The **earthly father's** blessing is the in-verse line *"there's nothing wrong with you, my son."* Hear what that is — it's stated in the *negative*. It's a pardon issued inside the very same right/wrong court that did the wounding. It clears the charge but keeps the courtroom.

The **divine-mother's** blessing — the motherly outro voice, the Mary-class voice — is *"my son, let your light shine."* That's *generative*. It doesn't acquit the child of a crime; it **claims his nature.** It dissolves the court entirely. One voice says *you didn't do anything wrong*; the other says *here is what you are.* That gap between *pardon* and *benediction* is the whole difference between surviving the shame and being freed from it.

## The dedication that became a benediction

There's a direction to Mary's appearances that rewards stepping back, and it sits inside a longer obsession with **faces** that runs the whole catalogue. Track the word: *"the disguise on my face getting clearer"* ([[Long Time Coming]]) → *"this one's for the faceless"* (the same song) → *"ain't gonna be no face in the crowd"* ([[Superhuman]]) → [[Happy Face]], the forced smile → the literal face-paint and mask era. The project keeps asking the same question — *what is a real face, and which faces are masks?* — long before Mary arrives to answer it by having **none.**

And the direction inverts. On *Subject to Flooding* (2018), [[Long Time Coming]] dedicates the song **to** the faceless: *"this one's for the faceless, the lost, not the famous."* In 2018, the faceless ones **receive** the care. By 2025–26, the faceless one is the one **giving** the blessing — the Ballerina Boy outro, the Weirdness angel, the Champion Madonna. The object of care has become the **source** of care. The dedication has come back around as a benediction.

It's best held as a *function swapping direction* rather than a single entity that traveled across the years — but as a shape, it's striking: the work spent a decade learning to bless the thing it once asked you to pity.

## Relationships

- **[[Roy]]** — the one she blesses; the self she's calling back to life. They share the lunar register but split it cleanly: Mary carries the moon's *theology* (reflected light, exile, promised return); Roy carries the moon's *position* (the self emptied to receive). Open question whether Mary is the figure Roy "comes back for," or someone distinct from the buried Ballerina Boy child.
- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — not simply her opposite. Where he **reflects**, she **transmits**; where he needs **watchers**, she's **constant**. They read as *two ways of relating to the same underlying drive*, which is exactly why his system can't absorb her. In [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] she unseats the championship — and the crown he resells in [[Not Your Homie]] is the one *she* revoked. Watch for him **counterfeiting** her, too: the *"I came to set you free / magic mirror"* rescue-voice in *Lucius Lullaby* is **Lucius faking the rescue**, not a true Mary appearance.
- **The Ballerina Boy child** — the wound she answers. Her outro blessing lands directly over the childhood shame: *let your light shine* laid across *you're not a real boy.*
- **The beautiful stranger** ([[City of Angels]]) — Mary's exact counterfeit. The "beautiful stranger" is *faced* (visible, an object you look *at*) and generic (*"another beautiful stranger"*); Mary is *faceless* (the ground you look *from*). The false feminine is hyper-visible because she's a reflection for sale; the true one is invisible because she's the source. The stranger *"does nothing but fawn"*; Mary *"fawns over no man."*
- **The Blue Fairy** ([[Ballerina Boy]]'s Pinocchio frame) — a structural rhyme, not a merger. *Pinocchio* sits on Roy's own film board, and it's the one myth that gathers all three of Ballerina Boy's elements: a made boy told he's *not a real boy*, a figure defined by the *string* tying him to his maker, and a **feminine voice that confers realness** — the Blue Fairy's *"you're a real boy now."* That realness-conferring voice is the **Mary-function** exactly: it rhymes with the motherly outro (*"you are one of a kind, God's design"*). Held to the function — the figures stay distinct.
- **[[The Rose]]** — the divine-feminine presence the rose has always belonged to (*rosa mystica*, the Virgin's thornless rose); the heart the work keeps reaching toward.
- **[[The Moon]]** — Mary carries its theology (reflected light, exile then restoration). *"Saw a halo but I didn't see a head"* = the source glimpsed, not yet possessed.
- **The Wise One / the Maker-Jaguar** — on [[The Circle]] and [[The Loop and the Circle]], the founding myth is bracketed by two feminine figures who both hold the same string: a primordial female Jaguar who makes the others and *teaches them to sing,* and a Wise One who *"remembered she once held the string."* Same divine-feminine register (Sophia / Shekhinah). Possibly one figure under two names, possibly two faces of the same source. Well-anchored, not locked.
- **[[The Jaguar]]** — an open question of order: is the Jaguar a genderless *seeing-field* distinct from Mary the *seer*, or does the work collapse seeing and seer into one function with two faces? Left open here.
- **[[SARAVI]]** — shares the wise-feminine, oracular register; an open resonance, nothing locked.
- **MERCY** — *not* Mary. MERCY (Mercedes Colletta Britton) is a real, credited backing vocalist and co-writer — she originated the *Not Your Homie* hook via a joke voicenote after a party. She's a person and a collaborator, not a symbol. The casting of the actual Mary voice in the songs is unconfirmed. This firewall matters: Mary is the *presence the songs reach for,* never simply "the woman singing in the background."

## Open questions

A few threads the work hasn't tied off, worth holding open rather than forcing shut.

- **Is Mary Ariadne?** In the project's symbolic vocabulary, the thread you follow through the dark is the heart, the feeling-line through the labyrinth (see [[The Thread]]). Mary is the figure who shows up precisely *when the distortion fails to contain what it holds* — which is exactly when the thread-bearer is needed. So: is Mary **Ariadne, the thread-bearer**? And a finer fork under it — is the thread **given** (handed to you by Mary / the divine) or **held** (your own refusal to sacrifice feeling)? Probably both, but which the work emphasizes changes the whole agency model.
- **Is Mary the Dweller-crossing figure?** The [[The Dweller on the Threshold|Dweller on the Threshold]] is the guardian you meet at the gate of real change — the accumulated self that either blocks the crossing or forces it. Mary appears to *break the mirror system*, which is precisely what a successful crossing would do. Open question: does she appear **after** the threshold is crossed, or is she the one who **makes the crossing possible**?
- **Is she the one Roy comes back for?** Or is the buried [[Brandon|Ballerina Boy]] child a separate thing she's reaching *toward*? The blessing lands over the child, but that doesn't make them the same figure.

## Advanced reading

> [!note]- Advanced reading — It's As Easy as ABC
> Why is a woman counting to three the most lethal thing in [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]]? Because the champion and Mary share a numerical surface but mean opposite things by it. The champion's whole world *counts rank* — first place, who's cooler, the leaderboard. Mary's *"A-B-C, one-two-three"* counts **creation** — the pre-personal grammar of things being made, ordered, brought into being. Same numbers, two objects. The ego that counts its rank cannot survive hearing the count that makes worlds; it discovers, mid-boast, that it was only ever *imitating* a grammar it didn't author. And because the count is impersonal creation-grammar rather than self-disclosure, her facelessness is preserved even as she speaks. She destroys him without ever showing her face.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — The champion is the Fool (Tarot 0)
> The visualizer image — *white rose in the left hand, stepping off the ledge* — is the Rider–Waite–Smith **Fool**, card 0, almost line for line: the white rose of trust, the cliff's edge, the leap taken on faith. The reading is doubly grounded: the lyric itself says *"made a move like a fool,"* and the project's jester/Pierrot imagery (the ruffed clown of the *Half the Man* visual world) sits right beside it. But the Fool's leap-on-trust is rendered **fatal** here. The card normally promises that the cliff is an illusion and trust will carry you. In #1 Champion the leap kills, because the failure isn't a lack of devotion — it's a failure of *seeing*. He followed her divinity without ever seeing *her*, and stepped off the ledge anyway.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — The Shekhinah frame (you don't need it to read the page)
> One strong frame — drawn from the Kabbalah Roy openly studies — reads Mary as the **Shekhinah**, the divine feminine *in exile*: the moon that reflects a light not its own, longing to be restored. On that reading, *"saw a halo but I didn't see a head"* is the crown glimpsed but not yet possessed. This is a **framework correspondence, explicitly not authorial intent** — the work never says "Mary is the Shekhinah." What licenses the frame is public: the *I'm 33* board, the *El Roi / El Roy* signature, the openly Kabbalistic furniture of the project. You don't need a word of this to read the page; the blessing-voice works on its own. But for readers who want the deep structure, it fits the recurring shape exactly: a diminished, face-hidden presence promising a light that's on its way. See [[Kabbalah - The Tree of Life]].

> [!note]- Advanced reading — The two-mothers ascent (Malkhut → Binah)
> Within the Shekhinah frame, the feminine divine is split top and bottom of the Tree of Life: **Binah** (the Upper Mother — the Great Sea, the womb, Saturn) and **Malkhut / Shekhinah** (the Lower Mother, Bride, Daughter — the moon that reflects the source-light, in exile). Mary isn't one fixed placement; she's the figure who *spans* them — the Shekhinah ascending from Malkhut to the throne of Binah, *"the Daughter, having become the Bride, set upon the throne of the Mother."* (That motion is also the Marian dogma of the Assumption and Coronation of the Virgin.) On this reading *"saw a halo but I didn't see a head"* is the **crown reached at the top of the ascent** — present but unpossessable, the throne arrived at while the source above stays hidden (Cordovero's *atarah*: Malkhut *becomes* the crown the moment she ascends — the literal engine of "reclaim your crown"). The exile arc grounds the "moon trying to become a sun" shape: the feminine in exile (*Galut ha-Shekhinah*), the diminished-moon myth (the moon once sun-equal, made to shrink), and the promised restoration (Isaiah 30:26, *"the light of the moon shall become as the light of the sun"*). The "face out of sight" quality = the diminished, exiled Presence not yet restored; the blessing-to-shine = the restoration promised. **This is the heaviest framework material on the page — syncretic, framework-tier, never claimed as intent.**
>
> One artist-grounded footnote keeps the substrate honest: the Hall of Mirrors *"Queen Binah"* artifact, in Roy's own hand, draws Binah as a whale captioned *"GOD the Mother on Saturn / Great Sea"* with *"extra eyes."* So *Binah = Divine Mother + Saturn + the Great Sea* is **Roy's own stated identification.** That grounds the Binah substrate as real engagement, not projection — though *Mary = Binah* stays interpretation, since Roy named Binah without equating her to Mary.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — Other registers: Magdalene, Sophia, the Dove
> **Magdalene.** *"Queen of California on the corner doing her time"* — the sacred feminine brought low (a street corner, serving a sentence) and *still* Queen. Holy-and-fallen at once; devotion as disruption (*"on her knees… making everyone stare"*).
>
> **Sophia / gnosis** ([[Weirdness]]). The faceless angel resembles Sophia — divine wisdom arriving from *outside* the system at the threshold of despair. Her message isn't new information; it's *remembrance*. *"Stop pretending, you were born to shine"* doesn't add a fact — it cancels the false one that was suppressing the real. That's gnosis in a single line. See [[Gnosticism - The World as Trap]].
>
> **The Dove** (the *Sir Rabbit* poem). A lamp-bearer in an emerald garden tree who gives light and then *releases* — *"As much as I love company you must be on your way."* The distinguishing trait is the tell of a true guide: she helps **without trapping you in devotion** — the exact opposite of [[Sir Lucius]], who clings. Held as a Mary/Sophia *function*, not a named Mary.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — The watcher-mask between Mary and Lucius
> One *Hall of Mirrors* illustration makes the witnessing/surveillance split into a single face. It's a white line-drawing — a bald head with a **third eye** on the forehead, the Jagwar Twin heart-monogram on its chest, set in a field of **mirror-doubled roses**. But the mouth bares teeth and the grin is *off*: closer to a watcher-*mask* than a serene higher eye. Read symbolically, it sits exactly **between** the two poles this page keeps naming — Mary's seeing-that-restores on one side, Lucius's watching-that-captures on the other. The roses pull the face toward devotion and beauty; the teeth keep the watcher's edge. Love and fear sharing one face, in a garden of mirrored roses. (The image's own puzzle-code is unrelated and unsolved; this is the symbolic read only.)

> [!note]- Advanced reading — False makers vs true creation
> *Lucius Lullaby* the album stages counterfeit creation against the genuine article, and Mary sits on the "true" side of the ledger. The **false makers**: *Soul Is A Star*'s "playing God behind the scenes," and [[Great Time to Be Human]]'s Chemist who "made a god" by mistake — *"She went to the Chemist to make her a dog / but he was dyslexic, so he made a god… (Woof!)."* A whole Demiurge cosmology built on a spelling error, and the botched god still *barks* — the dog trapped inside the deity. The **true thing**: Mary's *"A-B-C, count 'em"* and SOL's "breath beneath all the verses." [[Great Time to Be Human]] also runs the false-feminine substitution — *"Lover casts a spell to bring back his Mother"* — the erotic partner conscripted as a magical surrogate for the lost mother. The counterfeit feminine *re-animates* the wound; the true feminine (Mary) *restores* the self. *Lucius Lullaby* (false rescue-voice) and [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] (true creation-voice) sit on the same record, running the same operation in opposite directions.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — The hijacked altar (the Banana)
> A staging-dependent reading, flagged as an elaborate construction: in [[Bananas]], the video frames an altar — candles, an Egyptian statue — that can be read as the concealed Mother / Shekhinah, the hidden feminine. Lucius lifts a banana *off* that altar and uses it as a PA / Walkman to broadcast **his own** songs, then sets it back as if untouched. The sacred offering rerouted into a delivery system for his own voice — and yes, it is, literally, a man singing into a banana. The concealed feminine's altar becomes the conqueror's microphone. See [[The Banana]].

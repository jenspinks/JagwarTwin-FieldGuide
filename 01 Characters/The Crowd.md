# The Crowd

> **One-line reading.** The Crowd is the collective made audible — the same gathered voices that can rise into communion or curdle into spectacle, depending entirely on how they're sung. A unison is not a communion.

> [!infobox] The Crowd
> - **Also known as** — the crowd · the many · the mob · the chorus · [[The Audience]] *(its seated, screaming form)*
> - **Nature** — a force, not a symbol; neutral as a noun, charged by direction
> - **Two poles** — communion *(sings truth back)* · spectacle *(sings the same song)*
> - **First heard** — a crowd already singing: *"Long Time Coming"*
> - **Key symbols** — the ring · the chant · the scream · the parenthetical echo
> - **Related** — [[The Audience]] · [[Communion vs Spectacle]] · [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]] · [[Source vs Reflection]] · [[The Circus]] · [[The Loop and the Circle|The Circle]] · [[Roy]] · [[Sir Lucius]]

The Crowd is the only figure in the Jagwar Twin world that can be either the best thing or the worst thing in the room, and the songs refuse to tell you which until you listen to *how* it's singing. A crowd returning a song in its own thousand distinct voices is the holiest image the project has. The very same crowd, ordered to scream on cue, is its most damning. Same people, same singing — opposite poles. The Crowd is what's at stake every time the music asks you to join in.

## At a glance

- **Role** — the collective/audience as an active force; the medium in which both communion and distortion happen.
- **Start with** — [[Communion vs Spectacle]] (the engine), then [[The Audience]] (its consuming form), then [[Welcome to the Circus]] (the dark pole, sung).
- **Core conflict** — the same gathered voices can be awakened into communion or flattened into spectacle, and the difference is the *direction*, never the people.
- **Key symbols** — the [[The Loop and the Circle|ring]] · the chant · the scream · the [[The Voice|parenthetical echo]].
- **Confidence** — grounded fact + interpretation: the sing-back device and its key lyrics are grounded; the corpus-wide voice-split that organizes them is a strong reading, flagged as such.

## Why this figure matters

Most of the project's villains are interior — a shame-voice, a ruling mind, a mask. The Crowd is the one force that lives *out here*, in the room, in the feed, in the comment section, and it implicates the listener directly. When [[Roy]] refuses the word "fan" and insists his listeners are *"co-creators of the world,"* he is making a claim about the Crowd: that the same people who could be marks are also makers, and what separates them is not who they are but how they arrive. The whole drama of the figure is a single hinge — will this gathering remember something together, or will it be harvested? The Crowd is where the project's biggest idea gets tested in public, on real people, in real time. It is also, conveniently, the funniest place in the catalogue, because the machinery of mass attention is genuinely absurd, and Sir Lucius is more than happy to narrate it.

## A unison is not a communion

This is the load-bearing line, and the cleanest key to the whole figure. Both communion and spectacle *look* like a crowd singing together — that's exactly the problem. The catalogue is full of collective-singing images, and if you read them all as sacred you'd be wrong about half of them.

- **Communion** is many *distinct* voices singing truth together — difference held inside unity. This is [[The Loop and the Circle|the Circle]]'s chorus: *"the crowd sings it back,"* voices that remember and return the song, themselves even in harmony.
- **Spectacle** is many voices flattened into the *same* song — difference deleted. This is [[The Circus]]'s crowd: synchronized, not awakened; measured by reach and conformity rather than reciprocity.

The test is one question, and it's the whole concept: **is difference held, or deleted?** Held → communion. Deleted → spectacle. The fuller treatment lives at [[Communion vs Spectacle]]; what matters here is that the Crowd is the body this distinction is *about*. It is never neutral as a verb. It is only ever neutral as a noun.

## The thesis is now in the lyrics

For a long time this reading rested on staging and interviews — how a room was lit, how Roy described a live show. Then a single line on [[Great Time to Be Human]] put it inside a sung text: *"the crooks and believers all sing the same song."* Crook and believer are *supposed* to be opposites — the entire moral order depends on telling them apart — and the spectacle dissolves the difference. Under the cheer, the con man and the true believer end up in the same choir, and neither one notices, because what counts is not what you mean but that you're singing along. That is the spectacle pole stated plainly, in the music, and the [[Communion vs Spectacle]] concept was seeded from this exact line.

Its visual twin is [[City of Angels]]'s *"everyone looks just like you"* — the same collapse moved from the moral register into the visual one. Sameness offered as the forgery of oneness: *"we are all one"* curdled into *"you are all interchangeable."* One operation, two registers.

## The sing-back device

The Crowd's signature behavior across the catalogue is the **echo** — the crowd takes a phrase and sends it back. This is one of Jagwar Twin's clearest participatory devices, and it is also where communion and spectacle split most visibly. The direction the echo runs tracks *who is singing*.

When [[Roy]] voices a song, the crowd tends to sing *with* him — distinct voices returning the song, a communion-echo:

- [[Long Time Coming]] — the album opens on a crowd *already* singing (*"they're singing…"*). You arrive mid-chorus.
- [[Loser]] — the bridge turns *I* into *we*: *"we're singing."*
- [[Superhuman]] — *"it goes, we sing / raise up your voices."* (For the productive wrinkle here, see *Advanced reading* below.)
- [[Dream - Dream|Dream/Dream]] — *"dream that dream with me."*
- [[Shine]] — *"so sing it if you know you're gonna shine."*
- [[Imagine A World]] — *"just imagine,"* the line left open for the listener to finish.

When [[Sir Lucius]] voices a song, the crowd tends to be *ordered, synchronized, or reduced to noise* — a commanded, harvested echo:

- [[I Like to Party]] — the Pied Piper: *"follow me… sing along… to the beat of my drum,"* and *"one, two, three, four."*
- [[Pay Attention (feat. little luna)]] — *"what's my name? / Attention."* To answer is to pay.
- [[Welcome to the Circus]] — *"take your seat, scream and shout."* The crowd scripted as *noise*, not song.
- [[Bananas]] — the id-permission chant: *"do you ever wanna break shit?" → "well, alright."* The crowd echoes destruction back.
- [[Not Your Homie]] — *ring-around-the-rosie* weaponized into a plague-march.
- [[Great Time to Be Human]] — *"all sing the same song."*
- [[City of Angels]] — an echo-economy with no communion left in it.

A caution belongs here, because call-and-response is endemic to pop and the mere *presence* of sing-along lines proves nothing. The claim survives only on the **directional split by voice** — who sings with the crowd versus who commands it — not on the fact of a crowd. That's also exactly what would break it: a clearly Roy-voiced song scripting the crowd as consumable noise, or a clearly Lucius-voiced one genuinely inviting distinct-voiced return, would falsify the pattern. So far the split holds.

## The ringmaster's crowd

The spectacle pole gets its fullest performance in [[Welcome to the Circus]], where Lucius steps to the front and works the room with a showman's contempt. The instruction is the collapse itself: *"take your seat, scream and shout / 'cause I'm the one they're talking 'bout."* In one line the co-creator is seated, handed a role, and converted into ambient noise around the star. The crowd isn't gathered here; it's *processed.*

The operating principle is stated without apology: *"they love it when you lose control."* This is not a complaint about audiences — it's the marquee. The system isn't hiding that it runs on your breakdown; it's printing it on the sign out front, cheerfully. The crowd's appetite for watching someone come apart is both the fuel that powers the show and the product it manufactures more of. And then the fourth wall doesn't break so much as it asks for a tip: *"when the curtains close, did you like the show?"* The ringmaster turns to the camera and asks the crowd to rate its own consumption. The figure closes the loop with *"I'll chew you up, spit you out"* — the Circus as a digestive system that consumes audience and performer alike.

The Crowd is why Lucius exists at all. He is the most audience-dependent figure in the whole project: without watchers to play to, he doesn't cohere. **No audience, no Lucius.** The Crowd is his power source — which means the contempt and the dependency share one address. *"You don't know me,"* screamed at the exact crowd buying the merch, is the whole parasocial joke compressed to four words.

## The ring weaponized

The single richest crowd-image in the catalogue is in [[Not Your Homie]], and it's an inversion so clean it's almost a thesis statement. The song's spine is a playground rhyme — *"ringing rosies, my pocket's full of posies… ashes, ashes, we all fall down"* — lifted from *"Ring a Ring o' Roses,"* which is to say from the most communal chant-form there is: a children's *circle-game*, the literal shape of [[The Loop and the Circle|the Circle]], hands joined, everybody in. Lucius hands that gesture to the plague-voice and turns it into a plague-march: *"coming in like a plague from the dark side."* The rhyme gets literalized — the thing that spreads is unconsciousness itself, shadow gone memetic — and the Circle's own communal gesture becomes the vehicle of contagion. The move into spectacle, captured in one image: take the most communion-shaped thing you have, and weaponize it.

The plague-voice also crowns itself, and the gag is the whole pathology in one boast: *"might run for president, I'm so presidential."* The crowd-handler's self-regard scales straight to the top office — the same appetite for the room that runs the Circus runs for election — and the comedy is that he's not wrong about the mechanism, only shameless about it. Mass attention and political power are the same harvest; *"so presidential"* just says the quiet part with a grin.

The hook, fittingly, wasn't even a thesis — it came from a joke. Roy's collaborator MERCY sent him a voice note singing the chorus after getting back from a party, and Roy knew immediately it had to be a Lucius song, calling it *"the ultimate introverted extrovert anthem."* The most engineered piece of crowd-manipulation on the record started as someone's party joke — which is its own small argument against reading deep single-author intent into the shallowness. The shallowness is the point, and it's funny on purpose.

## Co-creator versus spectator

This is the figure's moral center, and the project keeps it open. The same listeners are both the *co-creators* — Roy's deliberate word, chosen over "fan" — and the *marks*, the ones the Circus performs for and processes. What separates them isn't who they are; it's how they arrive: as subjects doing their own interior work, or as objects consuming someone else's spectacle. [[The Circus]] exists to collapse the first into the second, and *"take your seat, scream and shout"* is that collapse performed on the listener in real time.

Roy's stated position raises the stakes past entertainment. The quality of consciousness a listener brings shapes the shared reality the work belongs to; in his framing, *the world you build inside your own head is the world everyone then has to live in.* That's a transfer of responsibility, not a flattery. It asks something of the Crowd rather than offering it a show. The project's arc can be read as the recovery of the co-creator position *from* the spectator position — not escaping the Circus, but refusing to be reduced to *audience* inside it.

Which leaves the open question the page should hold rather than resolve: is the crowd's love of watching you lose control a **moral failure** of the crowd, or a **designed feature** of the system the crowd can't be blamed for? The honest answer is that the project diagnoses both and convicts neither. That ambiguity is the ethical fulcrum of the whole figure — and it's why the Crowd is a *force*, not a villain.

## The herded crowd, and the comedy of it

The attention-economy comedy is not incidental to this figure; it's the texture. [[I Like to Party]] (the most-streamed song on *33*, around 12.6 million plays) is a party anthem for people who *"don't go outside / stay in and pretend that we're in the club,"* a crowd *"running around like chickens with no head / in circles, they go"* — the Circle inverted into pure dissipation. *"Give me attention, attention / the people are mine"* is the Pied Piper saying the quiet part: the crowd herded, not gathered, counted in like a drumline. Everyone's at the party; nobody's at the party.

[[Bananas]] runs the same move in a nursery key: the cradle-rocker as a corrupted children's-TV host green-lighting arson. *"Hey kids, do you ever wanna break shit?… well, alright"* — and the crowd echoes *destruction* back. (Roy's public gloss on the banana itself sharpens it: *"most bananas are sterile and cannot reproduce by seed ;)"* — the Cavendish is a clone, all identical, all wiped out together precisely *because* identical. A monoculture you can chant.) The compact way to say it: a banana peel under the conscience, funny until you notice the fall is the point.

And [[Great Time to Be Human]] is the darkest joke of all, because its ad-libs are children's-show emotional-cue buttons fired at exactly the wrong moments — a brother is smothered → *"oops!"*; kids dance to destroy → *"yay!"*; a made-god still barks → *"woof!"*. It's horror with a laugh-track, the algorithm laughing at the boy who lost his mother. The Crowd is funniest precisely where it's being trained not to feel — which is the whole indictment, delivered as a gag.

[[City of Angels]] runs the bleakest version of the joke through the crowd's reward system itself. The promise the Spectacle dangles is the Walk of Fame — your name in a star on the sidewalk, immortality you can step on — under a survival-chant carried in from the track before: *never gonna die*, live forever in the bright lights, godlike. The punchline is what that deathlessness actually *is*: not resurrection but **taxidermy**. The crowd is sold living-forever and handed a stuffed pose of it. A phoenix burns and comes back genuinely new; taxidermy keeps the *shape* and removes the life — a thing that looks alive only because something cleaned it out and propped it up. That's the celebrity the Crowd is taught to want: preserved, postured, hollow. And it rhymes exactly with *"everyone looks just like you,"* because a Walk of Fame is a row of interchangeable stars and taxidermy is sameness with the breath taken out. The gag and the diagnosis are the same move — what the Spectacle calls eternal life is a glassy-eyed mount with your face on it.

## Relationships

- **[[The Audience]]** — the closest sibling, near a synonym. The Audience is the consuming, *seated* form of this same force; the Crowd is the broader figure that can still tip either way. The Crowd is the gathering; the Audience is the gathering once it's been seated and told to scream.
- **[[Communion vs Spectacle]]** — the keystone distinction the whole figure runs on. This is the conceptual engine: *a unison is not a communion.*
- **[[The Circus]]** — the Crowd's habitat at the spectacle pole; the structure built to collapse co-creator into spectator. The Crowd is the Circus's fuel *and* its product.
- **[[The Loop and the Circle|The Circle]]** — the communion pole. *"The crowd sings it back"* is the Circle's signature gesture; the Circus is this same crowd-energy pointed the opposite way. The Circle *receives* voices in communion; the Circus *extracts* them for the metric. Same singing crowd, opposite direction.
- **[[Roy]]** — voices the communion-echo (the crowd sings *with* him); author of the co-creator doctrine that rejects "fan." The alternative to the audience-as-spectator model.
- **[[Sir Lucius]]** — the most audience-dependent figure; voices the commanded echo (*"scream and shout"*); the ringmaster who collapses the crowd. *No audience, no Lucius* — the Crowd is the thing he runs on.
- **[[The Spectacle]]** — the broader condition the Crowd lives inside; it requires spectators to exist at all.
- **[[Witnessing vs Surveillance]]** — the sibling axis on the *being-seen* side of the same coin. Communion/spectacle is what happens when the Crowd *sings*; witnessing/surveillance is what happens when the Crowd *watches*. To be witnessed is to be seen and known by a presence that holds you; to be surveilled is to be seen and *captured* by an apparatus that harvests you — the same gaze, opposite directions, exactly as a unison is not a communion. The Circus's *"did you like the show?"* sits right on the hinge: it asks to be witnessed and gets you surveilled.
- **[[The Realm|The System]]** — the apparatus (industry + [[The Algorithm|algorithm]]) that engineers and harvests the Crowd; the broadcast-war fought over the listener.
- **[[Mary]]** — the faceless source a communion might sing *toward*; her counterfeit is the *"beautiful stranger"* the Crowd gets pitched in [[City of Angels]]. (Open question below: does communion need a faceless source to sing toward, where spectacle sings at a faced object?)

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the *Superhuman* wrinkle that sharpens the rule
> [[Superhuman]] (Subject to Flooding, 2018) looks at first like a counterexample: it is *command-cadenced* — *"left, right, left"* — yet it is unmistakably **we**-voiced. The commander sings *with* the crowd, not *at* it. That's not a break in the voice-split; it's the Roy-pole borrowing a martial form for communion, which is exactly what distinguishes it from Lucius's *"scream and shout."* The same drill-sergeant grammar can serve either pole — the tell is whether the leader is inside the chorus or barking at it. Far from violating the directional rule, *Superhuman* shows why the rule has to be about *direction* and not surface form: you cannot read the pole off the cadence alone.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the production layer carries its own crowd
> The records don't wait for a live audience to manifest the Crowd — they build one into the mix. The official lyric texts are saturated with parenthetical background-echo: *(it's been a long time coming)*, *(death of my generation)*, *(watching me)*, *(pay attention)*, *(in a loop)*, *(side quest)*, *(bounce)*. The recording echoes its own claim back at itself — a built-in crowd at the production level, the chorus already implied before anyone joins in. The dark pole of this is literalized in [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]], where Roy and child voices are *aligned to sound like one voice unless you listen closely* — unison-not-communion, engineered as a production fact. The communion pole would be many voices staying distinct; here the distinctness is deliberately mixed *out.* It is the [[Communion vs Spectacle]] thesis rendered at the level of the master.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the live event, and a load-bearing distinction
> In 2026 the sing-back device was performed live: the BMRC underground event (announced on X) was staged with production specifically recording the *crowd's* voices, and the first public footage-artifact is the *"bounce (Live Underground)"* reel, captioned *"who's coming to the next one?"* Even the title card joins in — *"(bounce) BROUGHT TO YOU BY JAGWAR TWIN (bounce)"* reproduces the catalogue's parenthetical-echo device in *typography*, the crowd-echo rendered as graphic design.
>
> The crucial nuance: this is **not counterfeit communion.** The singing is genuine. What the footage captures is *real* communion **mis-directed** — true collective voice aimed at the reflection-pole (the show, the brand, the spectacle) rather than at the source. That distinction — genuine-but-misdirected, versus fake — is the whole point, and it's easy to get wrong. The Circle's signature gesture, captured by the Circus, without anyone having to fake a thing.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — "The crowd is the untruth" (Kierkegaard)
> The deepest grounded backbone for a page titled *The Crowd* is a named influence: Kierkegaard's *"the crowd is the untruth,"* which Roy has cited and posted on his own quote-cards (alongside *"anxiety is the dizziness of freedom"* and *"people demand freedom of speech as a compensation for the freedom of thought which they seldom use"*). The crowd, in this frame, is not merely where untruth happens — it is the *condition* of untruth, the place individual responsibility dissolves into a mass that can hold a conviction no single member would own alone. It grounds the whole premise directly: the Crowd is precisely the terrain [[Sir Lucius]] needs in order to exist (*no audience, no Lucius*), and the spectacle pole is what a crowd becomes when it stops being a gathering of selves and becomes an untruth with a single voice.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — bread, circuses, and the arena's second floor
> The historical antecedent is [[Panem et Circenses]] — Juvenal's diagnosis (c. 100 CE) that the Roman public, which once *"bestowed commands, consulships, legions, and all else,"* came to *"eagerly yearn for just two things — bread and circuses."* The insight that gives it lasting force: the crowd is sedated not by force but by *entertainment* — the chains are pleasurable. The mapping onto the attention economy is almost too direct (bread → the dopamine of content; circuses → the optimized feed). The Roman emperors didn't need armies inside the city; they needed the games — and the modern comedy is that we'd download the games voluntarily and rate them five stars.
>
> There's a darker second floor, less often noticed: the Roman circus and arena were the literal sites where witnesses were *killed* for crowd entertainment. So the Crowd is also a martyrdom-site jury — and *"I'll chew you up, spit you out"* reads at once as the industry machine *and* the arena's description of consuming those who refused to recant (see [[Consumption]]). It is not only the audience that gets processed; it is the witness on the floor.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the long crowd-lineage
> The Crowd inherits a deep, uncomfortable ancestry, gathered on [[The Audience]]: the **Roman arena crowd** whose thumb decides life or death (the crowd that loves the fall); the **biblical mob** that screams *"crucify him"* (Matthew 27:23), collective emotion hardened into a verdict; **Elias Canetti's** *Crowds and Power* (the crowd as its own entity with its own physics of feeling); the **carnival barker and the mark** (P.T. Barnum's willing "sucker"); **Euripides' *Bacchae*** (the audience-as-mob that dismembers the king — the permitted scream that kills); and Paul's wry **1 Corinthians 4:9**, *"we are made a spectacle unto the world, and to angels, and to men"* — the apostle describing himself as the world's involuntary circus act. The figure's modern delivery mechanism is [[The Algorithm]], which optimizes for exactly the appetite Juvenal named.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the flattening is one operation in three registers
> *"All sing the same song"* ([[Great Time to Be Human]]) is the collapse in the **moral** register; *"everyone looks just like you"* ([[City of Angels]]) is the same collapse in the **visual** one; the synchronized live crowd is the **sonic** one. It is a single operation appearing three places at once: difference deleted. A circle requires distinct points held in communion — and a circle with no distinct points collapses into a single point repeating, which is the **Loop** (see [[The Loop and the Circle]], [[Flattened Identity]]). In a project named Jagwar *Twin*, built on the productive two and the mirror that *wakes*, sameness is the nightmare version of the figure: twinning gone metastatic, infinite doubling with no *other* left to meet.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — communion as a sacramental correspondence
> Held as resonance, not a claim the songs assert: *communion* in the religious sense is a body made of distinct members who remain themselves — many grains, one bread; many voices, one song, none erased. Its counterfeit is the crowd whose members are made interchangeable, the unison that *erases* rather than gathers. On that correspondence, the project's recurring crowd-images sort into the real and forged versions of the same longing for oneness: one returns each singer to a shared source; the other dissolves the singers into a single manufactured surface. The relevant open question: does communion require a *faceless* source ([[Mary]]) to sing toward, where spectacle sings toward a *faced* object (the *"beautiful stranger"* for sale)?

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the crowd as Babel (framework resonance only)
> A framework reading the project's shape supports, held at resonance level: Babel (Genesis 11) is the scriptural name for *one language collapsing into nonsense* — *"one language and the same words,"* then confusion, then scattering. Babel's crowd is a **forced unison** — the spectacle pole exactly — and that forced unison is precisely what gets scattered. So a crowd taught to sing *one* song back to the ringmaster is the Babel crowd, whose biblical fate is collapse; the Circle's communion (distinct voices, mutually understood) is the *anti*-Babel, the Pentecost inversion of many tongues and one understanding. This darkens the live-gathering question — assembling a one-song crowd is, on this reading, assembling Babel. The thematic crowd-as-Babel reading is robust on its own. (Any stricter letter-by-letter wordplay is the project's own synthesis, *not* traditional teaching, and is deliberately kept off this page — the resonance stands without it.)

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the era hypothesis, and why it doesn't hold
> It's tempting to read a clean chronological decay — communion in 2018, command in 2022, scream in 2026. Resist it. The split is **by voice, not by year**: *33* (2022) already carries *both* poles, since [[I Like to Party]] and [[Pay Attention (feat. little luna)]] are Lucius-pole crowd-handling sitting inside the same album as plenty of communion. Treating it as a timeline misses the actual mechanism. The Crowd doesn't degrade over the discography; it gets sung two opposite ways throughout, and the variable is always *who's holding the mic.*

> [!note]- Advanced reading — Debord, and why Lucius is bound to the Crowd
> Guy Debord's *Society of the Spectacle* (1967): the spectacle is not a collection of images but *a social relationship mediated by images* — and once life is lived as accumulated spectacle, the representation becomes more real than the thing it represents. The Crowd is what converts life *into* spectacle: *spectare*, to look at. Remove the watchers and the spectacle simply ceases to exist — which is the structural reason [[Sir Lucius]] is bound to the Crowd so tightly that he is at once its operator and its product. He is the part of the self that only exists while it is being seen.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — where this sits in the bigger map (genus and species)
> Communion-vs-spectacle isn't a one-off binary; it's the **collective species** of a much larger split the project runs everywhere: [[Source vs Reflection]] (or Source vs Mirror) — the question of whether a thing returns you to the real or holds you in the copy. Source vs Reflection is the **genus**, the master pattern; communion-vs-spectacle is what that genus looks like *when it happens to a crowd.* Communion is Source at collective scale (many voices returned to the real thing they share); spectacle is Reflection at collective scale (many voices held in a manufactured surface). The same genus throws off other species elsewhere — [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]] is the being-*seen* species, the *Heart/Mind* split the interior one — and reading them as siblings under one parent is what keeps the page from treating "the Crowd" as an isolated theme. It's one operation, scaled to the many: does it send you back to the source, or trap you in the reflection?

## Source notes

- **Grounded.** The sing-back device is a real, recurring feature of the catalogue, and its key lyrics are verifiable in the released songs: *"the crooks and believers all sing the same song"* ([[Great Time to Be Human]]); *"take your seat, scream and shout,"* *"they love it when you lose control,"* *"did you like the show?,"* *"I'll chew you up, spit you out"* ([[Welcome to the Circus]]); *"everyone looks just like you,"* the *"beautiful stranger,"* and the *never-gonna-die* survival-chant set against the Walk of Fame ([[City of Angels]]) — the *resurrection-versus-taxidermy* gloss on that material is interpretation, not a sung lyric; the *ring-around-the-rosie* spine, *"coming in like a plague from the dark side,"* and *"might run for president, I'm so presidential"* ([[Not Your Homie]]); the herding and *"the people are mine"* ([[I Like to Party]]); the id-permission chant ([[Bananas]]). Roy's rejection of "fan" for *"co-creators of the world,"* his Kierkegaard quote-cards, and his public sterile-banana gloss are from his own public posts and interviews. The live sing-back event and the *"bounce (Live Underground)"* reel are public official posts.
- **Strong reading.** The corpus-wide **voice-split** — that the echo runs toward communion when Roy sings and toward command when Lucius sings — is the page's central interpretive claim, and it is offered as a strong reading, not a fact. It was built skeptical (call-and-response is everywhere in pop) and survives only on the directional split, with a clear falsifier named in the text. The reading of *"everyone looks just like you"* and *"all sing the same song"* as one flattening-operation across registers, and of *Not Your Homie*'s circle-game as the communion-gesture inverted, are strong readings consistent with the released material.
- **Framework / resonance.** The sacramental sense of communion, the crowd-as-Babel reading, Kierkegaard's *"the crowd is the untruth,"* Juvenal's *panem et circenses* and the arena-as-witness-site, the long historical crowd-lineage (Roman arena, the *"crucify him"* mob, Canetti, Barnum, the *Bacchae*, 1 Corinthians 4:9), and Debord's spectacle are framework correspondences and named influences — harmonics worth hearing, held as resonance rather than as claims the songs assert. A standing honesty guard, noted on [[Great Time to Be Human]]: that song is the most collaborative track on the album (five writers), so its single line may be dark-comedy irony rather than a single-author crowd-cosmology — hold the synchronized-crowd thesis as a strong reading and the gag-reading as live.

> [!question]- Open questions
> - Is the Crowd ever *neutral*, or is the difference entirely in the **mode** — how it's sung? (The lab leans: a force, neutral as a noun, charged by direction.)
> - Is the crowd's love of watching you lose control a **moral failure** of the crowd, or a **designed feature** of the system the crowd can't be blamed for?
> - Can a member of the Crowd become a *co-creator* — and if so, what's the move? Does it require leaving the Circus, or refusing to be reduced to audience *within* it?

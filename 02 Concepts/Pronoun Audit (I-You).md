# Pronoun Audit (I-You)

> **One-line reading.** Count the pronouns across two albums and a real pattern jumps out — *33* runs on "you," *Lucius Lullaby* runs on "I" — but the sharper truth is that it's the "you" that drains away, not the "I" that hardens.

This is the working note that set out to prove the obvious and got out-argued by its own arithmetic. The hypothesis was tidy: *33* is the relational record, *Lucius Lullaby* is the self-absorbed one, so the pronouns should swing from second person to first. They do. But when you actually run the count, the swing turns out to be powered by something quieter and stranger than "the ego got louder" — and that quieter thing is the whole point.

## At a glance

- **In one line** — a pronoun count that shows the second person *evaporating* between *33* and *Lucius Lullaby* — the "you" drains out while the "I" barely moves — and what that evaporation does to a body of work built on facing an other.
- **Where it shows up** — [[City of Angels]] (the *your → my → your* rotation), [[Weirdness]] (the *your → my* integration flip), [[SOL]] (the wordless collapse of the whole axis).
- **Connected ideas** — [[Source vs Reflection]] · [[The Speaker Problem]] · [[The Mirror]] · [[Not Your Homie]]
- **Confidence** — the counts are hard fact (whole-word, reproducible); what the shift *means* is strong interpretation, labeled as such throughout.

## What it is

The idea is almost embarrassingly literal: take the lyrics of both albums, count the first-person words (*I, I'm, my, me, mine*) against the second-person words (*you, you're, your, yours*), and see which way each record leans. No theory required to do the counting — just a script and a clean lyric text. It's the rare reading in this whole guide you could check yourself with a highlighter.

What makes it more than a parlor trick is that the leaning is real, large, and pointed in a direction that *means* something. *33* (2022) is soaked in the second person. *Lucius Lullaby* (2026) flips to the first. And the project is built, from its name on out, as a [[The Mirror|hall of mirrors]] — a body of work where "I" and "you" are never just two people in a room but the two poles of a mirror. So counting the pronouns turns out to be counting the *mirror itself*, at the level of grammar. The audit is small and dumb on purpose. The thing it measures is not.

## How it works

Here's the count. Whole-word, case-insensitive, run over the lyric bodies of both albums (section labels and stage directions stripped out, the whole thing lowercased), grouping each pronoun family:

| Album | I-cluster | you-cluster | I : you |
|---|---|---|---|
| **33 (2022)** | 166 (3.7%) | 287 (6.3%) | **0.58 — "you"-dominant** |
| **Lucius Lullaby (2026)** | 134 (4.0%) | 82 (2.5%) | **1.63 — "I"-dominant** |

The ratio nearly triples (0.58 → 1.63). That's the headline, and it's true. But look at *where* the swing comes from. The *I*-density barely twitches — 3.7% up to 4.0%, a rounding error's worth of extra ego. The *you*-density, meanwhile, roughly **halves**: 6.3% down to 2.5%. Mechanically, the ratio doesn't move because the "I" got louder. It moves because **the "you" collapsed.**

That's the finding, and it's worth saying in plain words because it's the opposite of what the hypothesis assumed. The honest version isn't *"the I hardens."* It's *"the you drains."* The self doesn't swell so much as the *other* vacates — relation, address, the second person being called toward — and the "I" simply expands into the room the other left empty. Same crown, fewer people in the audience.

**The texture underneath.** The averages flatten a vivid per-song spread, and the per-song numbers are where you can actually feel the two records doing different jobs.

*33* is an **other-directed engine.** Its center of gravity is the second person — songs that exist to face, call, or wake someone else:

- [[Down to You]] — *you* 80 / *I* 1. Almost pure address; the "I" nearly disappears into the act of speaking *to.*
- [[Imagine A World]] — *you* 35 / *I* 0. A collective "you," not a single beloved — and not one "I" in the whole song.
- [[It's Your Time]] — *you* 45 / *I* 34. Balanced exhortation; the most evenly-weighted of the bunch.
- [[Soul Is A Star]] — *you* 39 / *I* 19. Witness-and-turn (more below).
- [[Online]] — *you* 22 / *I* 12.

The exceptions keep *33* from being purely relational — *Another Way II Heaven* runs I:you 7.0, *Happy Face* 2.33 — but they're islands in a sea of "you."

*Lucius Lullaby* is an **I-engine**, and the "I" it runs on is mostly *performance* — self-assertion, branding, the survival-clutch. The comedy is the mask:

- [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] — I:you 5.5.
- [[Bananas]] — I:you 4.5. Manic self-assertion up top, bleak underneath: the banana is sterile, looks healthy and can't reproduce, and the whole strut is a sealed first-person room. The joke *is* the mask — the funniest song is also the most evolutionarily dead-ended.
- [[Playing to the Gods]] — *I* 4 / *you* 0. The deathless survival-mantra, *"if I make it out alive, then I'm never gonna die"* — not a single "you" in it. And it's the album's darkest punchline: in the *Lucius Lullaby* video the line runs *over a heart-monitor flatline.* He didn't make it out alive, so the boast becomes its own joke — the brag and the corpse in the same frame.
- [[Bounce]] — I:you 3.0. *"You just love me for my lore"* — the I-engine winking straight at its own parasocial audience, naming the transaction even as it runs on it.

And the crucial detail: the "you" that *does* survive on *Lucius Lullaby* isn't the beloved. It's the **audience** — and often a **refusal**:

- [[Not Your Homie]] — *you* 29 / *I* 25 — *"you don't know me, let's not pretend."* The boundary, not the beloved. There's a real joke buried in it: *"you don't know me"* is a strange thing to insist on coming from an audience-*dependent* shadow-persona — the parasite biting the hand that feeds it. Lucius wears *El Roi,* "the God who sees me," on the throat that keeps singing *you don't know me.* Seen by God, unknown to you; he wants it both ways, which is the whole con.
- [[City of Angels]] — *you* 10 / *I* 9 — the "you" as a mark to be converted (more below).

A few late tracks **evacuate the pronoun axis entirely**: *Moths to the Flame* (0 "I" / 0 "you" — pure image), *Great Time to Be Human* (*I* 0 / *you* 2 — a collective, ironic "you"), *The Circle (Lucius Version)* (1 and 1 — a flat mantra). And the album closer, [[SOL]], voids it completely: it's wordless. Person dissolves. No "I," no "you," only breath. The end-state of the machine is the **cancellation of the axis** — the grammar of relation switched off and replaced with a voice making no words at all.

**Why this is the mirror, not just a stat.** The reason the count is load-bearing rather than cute: Roy's own stated doctrine is that *"humans are mirrors for each other"* — *"I just want to be a mirror."* If that's true, then the second person is always *also* the first; the "you" you address is a reflection of the "I" addressing it. So the pronoun that won't sit still is the mirror-doctrine made structural. The [[The Speaker Problem|Speaker Problem]] — *who is the "I," who is the "you"?* — isn't a puzzle the audit can solve. It's unanswerable **by design**, and the count is the closest thing to empirical footing under that whole idea. The mirror isn't just an image *in* the songs. It's the grammar *of* them.

## Where it appears

The album-level count is the skeleton; the flesh is a handful of songs where the pronoun mechanics happen right on the surface, in real time, where you can hear them.

**[[City of Angels]] — conversion made grammatical.** The song rotates a single possessive across its runtime: *"All your dreams are coming true"* (verse, *she said*) → *"All my dreams are coming true"* (after the saviors-and-gangsters bridge) → *"All your dreams… (And she said)"* (outro). Pitched at him, internalized, then re-transmitted — the exact shape of a convert who becomes a recruiter. He doesn't escape the beautiful stranger; he *becomes* one. And notice the song ends mid-rotation, parked in the recruiter slot, which is why it never wakes up the way *Lucius Lullaby* does — there's no one left outside the spell to confess *"it's all just a lie."* The Loop, encoded in three pronouns.

**[[Weirdness]] — the counter-move, and the album's only way out.** The whole integration event of the record is, mechanically, *one pronoun.* The angel-voice arrives saying *"Your weirdness is the sunshine"* — and by the final chorus that has become *"And now I know that all my weirdness is my sunshine."* The shadow's distorted light, reclaimed as one's own: projection → ownership. The same flip runs the string line — *"Your head is off its string"* → *"My head is off its string"* (both choruses agree the head is *off* its string; there's no on/off arc, the entire change is the possessive). Integration here isn't defeating anyone. It's taking the *your* and making it *my*.

**[[Lucius Lullaby|Lucius Lullaby]] — the undecidable "I."** The refrain is first-person present continuous: *"It's a beautiful life… I'm dreaming."* Not *you're* dreaming — *I'm.* Which leaves you unable to tell whether Roy is *consenting* to the dream or whether Roy and Lucius have *merged* into one "I." The lullaby works precisely *because* you can't tell whose voice sings *beautiful life.* Sedation, proven at the level of a pronoun.

**[[Soul Is A Star]] — the verse-to-chorus turn.** The verses are third-person witness — *"I met a man," "I met a girl"* — and then the chorus breaks straight to the second person: *"do you know who you are?"* That pivot, from observing others to addressing *you,* is *33*'s you-engine in miniature.

**[[It's Your Time]] — pronouns in scare-quotes.** The song holds its own pronouns at arm's length: *"As far as 'I' can tell," "who you are to 'me'," "your Self"* (capital S). The grammar knows it's unstable and flags it.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the function layer (the real finding, and why it's still a reading)
> Frequency is the cheap part. The counts above are grounded — you can re-run them. But a count can't tell a "you" that means *beloved* from one that means *crowd* or *God*, or an "I" that's a *confession* from one that's a *brand*. The actual finding lives one layer up, in **function**: scoring each pronoun by its grammatical and rhetorical *role,* song by song.
>
> Read that way, the two records aren't just numerically different — they're doing opposite things with the same words:
> - **In *33*, "you" functions as mirror, relation, awakening, contagion, plea.** The second person is the *other* being called toward — witness, beloved, the collective you're trying to wake.
> - **In *Lucius Lullaby*, I/you functions as performance, possession, recruitment, refusal, crowd-control.** The first person *asserts* and *brands;* the residual "you" is *audience* or *victim,* not the beloved.
> - **In *SOL*, the axis collapses into voice, breath, source.** No role left to play because there's no pronoun left to play it.
>
> So the licensed reading is sharper than "the record tilts toward 'I'": the I-and-you machine **loses its second person.** *33* faces an other; *Lucius Lullaby* turns that address into spectacle (the "you" becomes the crowd, then gets refused), and the self inflates to fill the vacated space — *the reflection claiming sourcehood at the level of grammar.* Then *SOL* voids the whole axis into breath. This is the strong-reading layer. The grounded floor *confirms* the hypothesis at the frequency level; the function pass carries the actual claim — and is exactly the work still to be fully scored, occurrence by occurrence.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the method, the denominators, and the SOL caveat (for anyone who wants to re-run it)
> **Method.** Whole-word, case-insensitive counts over the lyric bodies; frontmatter, `[section]` labels, and parenthetical stage-directions stripped; everything lowercased. The clusters: **I-cluster** = *i / i'm / i've / i'll / i'd / my / me / mine / myself;* **you-cluster** = *you / you're / you've / you'll / you'd / your / yours / yourself.* Album membership = the 10–12 studio tracks; features count as part of the track they're on. Reproducible from a clean lyric text.
>
> **The denominators** (the public table gives only percentages): *33* = **4,538 words;** *Lucius Lullaby* = **3,344 words.** Catalogue total across 45 sung lyric sources ≈ **13,122 words.**
>
> **Robustness check.** Excluding the wordless *SOL* barely budges the result: *Lucius Lullaby* = I:you **1.61** without it, **1.63** with it. The finding survives.
>
> **The SOL caveat (important).** The lyric file for *SOL* shows a 222-word count — but that's the *EP2 / Move-To-You ancestry text,* **not** the wordless album cut. Exclude it. The album version (track 14) is voiced but non-lexical — vocalization, no words — so its *wordlessness is the datum,* not a number to be tallied.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the catalogue-wide count (the parent footing)
> Zoom out past the two-album audit and the same grammar shows up at full-corpus scale. Across all 45 sung lyric sources (~13,122 words; the voice-memo excluded, *SOL* contributing none), the single **most-used word in the entire body of work is "I"** — 492 uses — narrowly beating *"the"* (485) and *"you"* (478). Clustered, the **I-cluster ≈ 912** and the **you-cluster ≈ 635;** together they're roughly **12% of every word sung.** This is the parent footing that lives in [[Source vs Reflection]], [[The Speaker Problem]], and [[The Mirror]] — the album-level audit is the down-tree special case of it. When the most-spoken word across a decade is *I,* and the second-runner is *you,* the claim that "the mirror is the grammar" stops being a metaphor and starts being a frequency table.

> [!note]- Advanced reading — the traditions underneath (held as resonance, not authorial intent)
> The audit instantiates the **mirror-doctrine** — Roy's grounded *"I just want to be a mirror" / "humans are mirrors for each other"* — at the level of grammar; that's the one tradition with public footing. The rest are framework-tier substrate, licensed by the shape of the work, never claimed as Roy's stated method:
> - **Joni Mitchell's "multiphrenic" self** (Ann Powers' phrase) — a *confirmed influence* articulating the exact shifting-speaker structure the audit measures. Held as provenance/substrate; the precise lineage is a lead, not a verdict.
> - **Collective consciousness / "we, not me"** — Roy's 2018-onward framing of Jagwar Twin as a collective entity (*"It's us, we, not me and we are all Losers"*). This is grounded, and it destabilizes the grammatical "I": it's never simply solo-Roy doing the singing, which is part of why the speaker won't sit still.
> - **Neoplatonism** — the One and its emanations/images; *epistrophe,* the turning-back to the source. The resonance under [[Source vs Reflection]].
> - **Lacan's mirror stage** — the "I" constituted through an image that is also *other,* the idealized rival-double — maps onto the I/you-as-mirror grammar. A resonance, not a claim that Roy sources Lacan.
> - **The pre-pronoun breath** — Kabbalistic reflected/lunar light, Genesis *ruach,* Theosophy's "Great Breath" — carry the *SOL* end-state: the voiced-but-wordless breath where person hasn't formed yet (or has dissolved back). Framework-attributed.

## Source notes

- **Grounded (the counts).** Every frequency and ratio on this page is empirical and reproducible from the released lyrics — the album totals, the per-song counts, the catalogue-wide tally, the robustness check. The source texts are the released album lyrics (Apple Music / AZLyrics-verified). The per-song pronoun *rotations* are grounded in the verified lyric texts: *City of Angels*'s *your → my → your;* *Weirdness*'s *your → my* and *"off its string"* in both choruses; *Soul Is A Star*'s verse-to-chorus turn; *It's Your Time*'s scare-quoted pronouns; the title track's first-person *"I'm dreaming."* That *SOL* is wordless is grounded (by ear; Apple Music lists "No Lyrics Available"). Roy's mirror-doctrine (*"I just want to be a mirror," "humans are mirrors for each other"*) and his *"we, not me"* framing are public statements.
- **Strong readings.** The **function shift** (you-as-relation → you-as-crowd/refusal; the reflection claiming sourcehood at the grammar level), the reading of *SOL*'s wordlessness as *cancellation of the axis,* and the per-song keystones as conversion/integration/sedation are all interpretation built on the grounded counts. The "you drains, the I doesn't harden" correction is grounded *as a description of the numbers;* what it *means* is the reading.
- **Framework (resonance, not intent).** The Joni "multiphrenic" lineage, Neoplatonism, Lacan's mirror stage, and the breath-cosmology under *SOL* are syncretic substrate — licensed by the shape of the work, never asserted as Roy's method.
- **How we'd be wrong.** Songs rotate pronouns constantly; ordinary pop address-shifting could account for the *City of Angels* and *Weirdness* keystones without any of the freight here — the Loop and the integration are *encodable* in the pronouns, not *proven* by them. And the whole arc rests on **two data points.** *Subject to Flooding* (2018) is the untested third: counting it would show whether the second person was always there to be lost — i.e. whether "the you drains" is a real trajectory or just a two-point line drawn through a convenient hypothesis. There's also an open question one layer down, in the function pass itself: the "residual *you* = audience / refusal" reading is clean for the keystones, but it hasn't been tested against *every* "you" on *Lucius Lullaby.* If even a handful of them read as a genuine beloved rather than a crowd or a victim, the "the album loses its second person" claim softens from a rule to a strong tendency — which is exactly the per-occurrence scoring that's described here but not yet finished.

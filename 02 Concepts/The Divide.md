# The Divide

> **One-line reading.** The named edge where the leap is chosen — the threshold a seeker crosses to make the return.

The Divide is a threshold that appears in the Sir Rabbit poem, the verse narrative that runs alongside the Hall of Mirrors. It is the edge the seeker reaches and the place where the choice to jump gets made. It's a small, single-source idea rather than one of the project's load-bearing concepts — but it sits at a meaningful hinge in the larger geometry of return.

## In the poem

The Spider leads the seeker to the brink: *"You're here at the Divide. I'm here to be your guide… The Spider led him to an edge below the Great Divide. Jump off the edge… and through the sun you fly."*

Two things stand out about how it's staged:

- **The guide points but does not push.** *"That's up to you, it's not for me to pry."* The choice is left to the seeker; sovereignty is preserved at the threshold.
- **The leap is chosen.** This reads as the deliberate counterpart to the involuntary crash in the "Good Time" dream, where the fall is *done to* the dreamer. Here the seeker jumps. The passage *through the sun* delivers the [[The Loop and the Circle|Circle]] return — coming back, but only after the crossing.

## How to hold it

This rests on a single source so far, so it's best read as a suggestive threshold-image rather than a fully anchored doctrine. If a "Divide" or "Great Divide" edge recurs elsewhere in the work with its own anchors, it would earn more weight. For now, its interest is positional: it names the chosen-leap moment that the return depends on, and it ties the seeker's free choice to the larger pattern of passing *through* something in order to come back transformed.

# The Upload

> **One-line reading.** The Upload is Roy's plan to pour his consciousness into the internet so a digital version of him can live there forever — pitched first as a joke about influencer culture, then built out, and taken seriously by the songs as dread.

The Upload is the application of [[The Pattern]]: the operation Roy calls "how to upload myself to the internet." It distributes the self into a watching network, and that move is the hinge between his digital mythology and his deepest fears about the self.

## What it is

Roy spells the premise out in public, first-person prose on the project's ARG page: *"my name is Roy… my failure at being a social media influencer has me here at 33 still trying to 'make it.' To solve this problem I'm uploading my consciousness to the internet so a digital version of 'me' can be a 24/7 influencer."* The accompanying mechanic — "mint body parts" — is realized in the [[Hall of Mirrors]], where unlock codes name the anatomy of the uploaded self piece by piece ("left hip of a dove," "teeth of a whale"), described in the gallery as "remnants of the Upload that created SARAVI."

The fuller operation comes from Roy's "I'm 33" board — his own artifact — which lays out steps to "enter the digital dimension": gather memories, build a personality mask, digitize, push to the cloud, teach an AI who he is, and make a body for that AI. Alongside runs the question "how to live forever?" with the answer "put music on chain," and a premise borrowed from cybernetics: "all the world is mind, mind woven into matter."

## It began as a joke

In a published interview, Roy describes the origin: "I joked with the CTHDRL team saying, 'what if I uploaded my consciousness to the internet so that there could be a 24/7 social influencer version of me.'" So the Upload — and [[SARAVI]] with it — was born as a satirical quip about influencer culture, then built out in earnest. The irony is load-bearing. The deadpan "24/7 influencer self" of the manifesto is the same idea that [[Online]] and [[The Watchers]] take seriously as horror. The Upload cuts both ways by design.

## Attention, given a face, is the Eye

The board asks how to "anthropomorphize attention," and sets the question beside Horus, the Eye of Horus glyph, and the god Marduk. Read together with the upload steps, a thesis emerges: to upload a self is to distribute it into a watching network — to give attention a body and a way of seeing. The net is the new all-seeing field; the Eye (Horus, Ra) is the old name for that field. The Egyptian and Eye imagery isn't decoration but the mechanism. A self made of everyone watching is the Eye made of everyone.

This has a human precondition Roy states plainly: "Done being a person. I'm just a vessel for God." The self as vessel or conduit, rather than author, is what the upload requires. But it cuts both ways — Roy frames the vessel as serving God, not the net, which makes the upload the machine's counterfeit of a genuine impulse: the same emptying that [[The Empty Mirror]] redeems, here sold to the feed.

## A cross-album spine

The Upload isn't a single image; it's a through-line that runs for years. [[Online]] (2022) is the surveillance-and-clone complex as a finished, public song — an army that has taken his face, the net as a web with us as the spider's food. [[The Watchers]] (2024) is the net-that-watches — the Eye — plus the clone panic, "I'm afraid I'm a clone." The 2026 material carries the Eye and surveillance imagery forward. The theme is corroborated on the public NFT gallery, whose cards name it outright ("Complete Upload Recipe," "Ra-link," "Trojan Horse Program").

## The clone paradox

The board lists the Eye-of-Horus fractions — ½, ¼, ⅛, and so on down — which famously sum to 63/64, always missing the final 1/64 (the piece Thoth restores in the myth). That is the upload's paradox: encode fraction after fraction and never reach the whole. It's the same fracture as "I'm afraid I'm a clone" — a complete being rendered in parts, one part always left out. The Egyptians already wrote the equation for the incomplete copy.

A public artifact, the "Complete Upload Recipe," makes it literal: it assigns the senses those exact fractions — "½ part smell, ¼ part sight, ⅛ part thought… 1/64 part touch" — so that touch, the hardest sense to digitize, is the smallest and last. The other ingredients name the rest of the operation, including "sacrifice" and "love" as the cost. The self as a fractional assembly of faculties, with the price built in.

## On the Egyptian and esoteric layer

The Eye imagery has real footing rather than free-floating decoration. The Eye of Ra and Eye of Horus are the divine seeing-power sent out into the world — wounded and then restored — which rhymes precisely with uploading consciousness into a watching network. Roy's childhood fascination with Egyptian mythology is documented, and he publicly engaged the *Law of One* (the "Ra Material") in his Modern Archeology series, which fuses the Egyptian Ra with its own entity called Ra. That makes the Eye a real, named influence. It does not follow that any given song *encodes* that doctrine — the board itself points to Egyptian glyphs rather than the *Law of One*'s vocabulary, and per-song readings need their own anchors.

# The Labors of Hercules

> **One-line reading.** A trial-cycle the project genuinely runs on: Roy reads the Twelve Labors not as adventure stories but, following Alice Bailey, as a map of the soul's own journey — twelve faculties each tested and returned to the heart's rule, with the Lion of Leo (the ego grown tyrannical) the one that matters most here.

Of all the esoteric frameworks the Jagwar Twin world draws on, this is one of the best-grounded, because Roy engages it openly and by name. He keeps three Alice Bailey quotes in his public influences. He photographed a hand-annotated page of Bailey's *The Labors of Hercules* to his Instagram Story. And in a livestream he named the book outright and taught its core idea as his own. So the framework is genuinely *in* the project, not imported into it. What follows leads with that grounded engagement, then offers a reading of how the cycle maps onto the catalogue — a reading clearly marked as such, because the closer mapping is interpretive.

## The myth, and Bailey's reading of it

The Twelve Labors of Hercules are the canonical trial-cycle of Greek myth — twelve ordeals the hero must complete in sequence, each harder and more meaningful than the last. The labors traverse the zodiac, one per sign.

In the **esoteric tradition** — Theosophy broadly, and Alice Bailey's *The Labors of Hercules: An Astrological Interpretation* (1974) specifically — the labors are not adventures. They are a **map of the soul's path**: the stages a person crosses to move from sleeping, mechanical existence into integrated, awakened being. Hercules isn't a single mythic strongman; he's the *prototype*, "an earnest, erring, deeply flawed human being" who learns through devastating trial and error. He is every aspirant who consciously begins the work. Bailey reads the cycle as moving *against* the current of material instinct — the soul has to *choose* the trial, election being the first requirement.

This is exactly how Roy frames it. In his livestream he described *"a book by Alice Bailey called The Twelve Labours of Hercules, and Hercules, which is the soul, which is us, the initiate, goes through all twelve signs of the zodiac… but we all have these inside of us… in different aspects of our life."* The labors as an *internal* initiation — twelve faculties inside one person — is his stated reading, not an external imposition.

## What's grounded, and what's a reading

It helps to separate two claims that are easy to blur:

- **Roy works the Bailey labors as a framework for himself — this is established.** He names the book, annotates it, applies a specific sign to his own birth chart, and teaches the whole cycle as an inner journey. Bailey sits as one named pillar of his larger system, alongside other sources he assembles on his "I'm 33" board.
- **Specific songs were composed as specific labors — this is a reading, not a stated fact.** Roy has not said "song X is labor Y." The mapping below is an interpretation built on lyric and image, offered because it illuminates the work — held as a strong reading, not as the author's blueprint.

A clean fit between a song and a labor doesn't settle which of those it is: a tidy match is equally consistent with deliberate design and with an attentive listener's pattern-finding. So the mapping earns its place by the anchors in each song, not by how neatly the whole thing lines up.

## The cycle as the project reads it

Bailey runs the labors in zodiacal order, Aries through Pisces. Read as faculties of the soul, each labor tests a different power that has gone distorted, and the work is to return it to the heart's rule — the project's recurring move of *"my mind must serve my heart."* The arc, in one line: **Mind → Desire → Knowledge → Soul → Ego → the Feminine → Balance → Shadow → Speech → Death → the Collective → Wholeness** — the soul learning, stage by stage, not to misuse power.

## Leo — the Nemean Lion (the central labor)

The fifth labor is the one most clearly woven through the project's public work, so it earns the most depth.

**The myth.** An invincible lion terrorizes Nemea; its hide can't be pierced by any weapon. Hercules tracks it to a cave with two openings, chases it through, and the lion just runs out the other side. Sword and arrows bounce off. So he changes his approach entirely: he **blocks one opening**, *leaves his weapons outside*, enters through the other, and **strangles the lion bare-handed** in the dark. He cannot kill it from a distance with his usual tools. He has to enter the cave, drop the weapons, and meet the beast directly.

**Bailey's reading.** The lion is the **coordinated dominant personality** — the ego built up by all the earlier labors into something genuinely powerful, capable, self-sustaining. By Leo the aspirant is no longer a floundering mess; they've become highly successful by every worldly measure. *And that is the danger.* The same ego-architecture that secured survival becomes a self-centered force devastating to everyone around it. The lion is *successful Hercules*, turned against the world. The cave's two openings are read as dual modes of consciousness; blocking one seals the *emotional escape route* — no fleeing into excuses, victim-narratives, or defensive discharge. And the strangling isn't killing in the ordinary sense: what dies is the ego's *authority*, its dominance over awareness. The lion's *energy* is preserved. What changes is the hierarchy.

**The Strength card.** The tarot's Strength card (major arcana VIII) shows a serene figure gently closing the jaws of a lion — not slaying it, not caging it from a distance, but in calm, direct contact. Its meaning is mastery through gentleness rather than force, instinct *integrated* rather than suppressed. **The lion is not the enemy.** The card is the steady state *after* the Leo labor is done right — Hercules's strangling is the active integration; the Strength card is the peace that follows.

**Beast integration.** Across all of it — Bailey's Leo, the Strength card, the broader Western mystery tradition — the consistent move is the same: the Beast is not destroyed, it is **integrated**; integration is not amputation (the energy is kept, the authority changes); the means is *direct contact up close*, not combat at distance; and the aspirant must *put down their habitual weapons* — the defenses and deflections that worked before — to meet it. The Beast is, structurally, *the aspirant's own prior solution* — the ego they built to survive, now grown too strong to share power with. This is the same operation Carl Jung calls **shadow work**: the disowned material holds real value, and the task is *relationship*, not elimination.

This is why [[Sir Lucius]] reads as the Nemean Lion — the dominant personality grown tyrannical — and why the project never frames the answer as "kill Lucius." [[Welcome to the Circus]] is the lion *ravaging in the open*, the dominant ego operating outside the cave, never yet confronted in the dark.

## The strongest lyric anchor

The clearest single piece of public evidence tying the framework to the catalogue is in [[Not Your Homie]]:

> *"But if you choose to tame The Beast / you've got to prove yourself to be / a champion."*

Three things land at once. *The Beast* — capitalized and definite-articled, a specific named entity, not just any beast. *Tame* — not *fight*, not *slay*; *tame* is the operative verb of the Strength card and Bailey's Leo, integration through contact rather than destruction at distance. (If the line said *fight* the Beast, the reading would weaken badly.) And *champion* — which, in a body of work built on ordeal, descent, and return, reads as the technical word for *one who has completed the trials*, not as gym-floor motivation. The cluster *tame + Beast + champion* across three consecutive lines is tight enough to read as a deliberate touch on the Leo labor's symbolic field.

The song [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] reinforces it. *La Conquistadora* — "the conquering one," feminine — rhymes with the Strength card's own gendered image: not the violent conqueror but the one who has *tamed*. Whether the song is a completed-Leo victory or Lucius *naming* a destination he can't actually occupy is an open question — the figure can describe the goal without reaching it.

## Cancer and Libra — the labors Roy taught

**Cancer (the Doe).** In his livestream Roy told this labor in full and applied it to himself. Hercules must capture the thing he loves most — a doe. Two goddesses counsel him, one urging *instinct* ("like an animal, lower"), the other *intellect* ("higher"); acting from either alone fails. A *third* voice succeeds — *"the synthesis between the instinct and the intellect… the intuition… the god voice."* He captures the doe, holds it, loves it. Cancer's gift is to love and nurture deeply; its shadow is to *"hold too tight and won't let go."* The lesson: he is commanded to *let the doe go*, sacrificing it on the altar of the sun-god, where it is *"born again over and over forever"* — *"letting go and not attaching to the outcome."* Roy applies this to his own chart (Venus in Cancer) and to record labels, career, and love. This instinct-intellect-intuition triad is faithful to Bailey's own text, and it resonates closely with the project's heart-mind structure and its move toward synthesis — and the *"sacrifice on the altar of the sun-god"* with the sun imagery of [[SOL]]. (One honest note: in Bailey the doe *is* the intuition; Roy personalizes it as "the thing I love and create" — faithful application, slightly shifted.)

**Libra (the Boar).** The page Roy annotated and shared is the Libra labor. His underlines cluster on *the cost of serving a humanity that misreads you* — and the densest mark of all is the fully-underlined line about *"the perversity that upholds the opposite of what is said,"* which reads almost as a definition of [[Inversion]] (the same form turned to opposite content — "call evil good, light darkness"). Marking that line ties the Bailey material to the project's inversion theme. That these are *his* underlines is grounded; the thematic link is the reading.

The pattern across all three is telling: he posted the Libra labor and is Libra Rising; he taught the Cancer labor and has Venus in Cancer. He works the labors of *his own birth chart* — which confirms his unit is *the soul's lesson per sign*, lived personally, rather than "song X equals labor Y."

## A note on scope

Two cautions keep this honest. First, "tame the beast" is a near-universal phrase — it lives in self-help, Christian asceticism, Buddhist psychology, and Jung's shadow generally. What lifts *this* reading past the generic is the convergence of Bailey-as-named-influence with the word *champion* close by, in a catalogue that consistently frames change as ordeal. Second, Bailey is **one lens among many**, not the master key. Roy's influences are openly syncretic — Jung, Hermeticism, Kabbalah, Gnosticism, Plato, tarot, and more — and the album's arc fits several frames at once (a single dark night's wrestle, a Tree-of-Life ascent, a cyclical return, a Bailey-shaped ordeal). More than one can be true together. The discipline is to ask of every reading: *would this still hold without Bailey?* Where the answer is "several frames fit," the reading stays strong but explicitly multi-framed — which is exactly the kind of self-sealing, totalizing mythology the project's own work tends to critique.

# The Speaker Problem

> **One-line reading.** In any given line, *who* is the "I," and *who* is the "you"? The catalogue never settles it — and that refusal is the mirror doctrine showing up at the level of grammar.

The Speaker Problem is the question the whole catalogue keeps un-answering: in any given line, which self is speaking, and who is being addressed? It has two faces, and they turn out to be the same problem.

## The "I" is layered

The project stacks identities, and the lyrical "I" can be any of them at once:

- **Brandon Roy Wronski** — the private person.
- **Roy English** (2006–2015) — the early industry name, the "mystery man" era of being credited everywhere and pictured nowhere (see [[Witnessing vs Surveillance]]).
- **Jagwar Twin** (2018–) — the project, framed as collective consciousness ("we, not me"); the name itself is the jaguar (reflexive seeing) plus the twin (duality).
- **Sir Lucius** — the shadow-persona, the [[The Protector|Protector]] crystallized.
- **The lyrical "I"** — the speaker inside the song, floating across all of the above.

The result is a kind of recursive identity performance: *who is speaking?* is never settled, and the project performs its own identity-instability rather than resolving it.

## The "you" won't resolve either

The second person is just as deliberately unfixable. Across the catalogue "you" keeps refusing to land on one referent — in LOOP it can be lover, algorithm, trauma, or self; in *Bad Feeling*, listener, industry, Roy, or Lucius; in *Bounce*, "you just love me for my lore"; in *Weirdness* the pronoun slides from "your" to "my"; in *Ballerina Boy* it's "no one else like you." The pronoun is built to drift.

## Why it won't resolve

The "you" can't be pinned because Roy's grounded doctrine is that humans are mirrors for each other — "I just want to be a mirror." So the second person is always also the first: the grammar *enacts* the mirror. A pronoun that won't sit still is the doctrine made structural. The Speaker Problem is therefore the [[Source vs Reflection]] axis at the level of pronouns — the "I" facing "you" *is* the source-and-reflection relation.

This is the mirror doctrine in its grammatical form: "see yourself, not me"; the [[The Empty Mirror|Empty Mirror]]; the enso that says "tell us who you are"; [[Soul Is A Star]] turning to the listener with "do you know who you are?" The songs work as a mirror the listener sees themselves in, not as a self-portrait.

## The arithmetic backs it up

It isn't only a thematic reading. A count across 45 sung sources (roughly 13,000 words) finds the most-used word in the catalogue is "I" (492), edging out "the" (485) and "you" (478). The I-cluster runs to about 912 words and the you-cluster to about 635 — together roughly 12% of every word sung. The catalogue is, grammatically, an I-to-you machine. The album-level dynamics shift across the arc, from a "you"-heavy *33* through an "I"-heavy *Lucius Lullaby* toward a pronoun-collapse later — but the machine is always running.

## An inherited echo

A confirmed influence articulates the exact structure: the critic Ann Powers describes Joni Mitchell's "multiphrenic" self — "her vantage point shifting from male to female to animal to angelic with a phrase's turn." That shifting-speaker, mirror-grammar way of being is a strong substrate for what Jagwar Twin runs, held as a documented influence rather than a claim about intent.

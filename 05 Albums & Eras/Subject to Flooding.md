# Subject to Flooding

> **One-line reading.** The 2018 debut — made in the wilderness years on almost no money — diagnoses the disease the rest of the catalogue spends years treating, and quietly carries the seed of nearly everything that comes after. The soil contained the seed; it just wasn't planted on purpose.

> [!info] Release facts
> - **Year** — first released independently September 21, 2018; redistributed and expanded by **Atlantic Records** April 26, 2019 (Apple lists it as 13 songs, 34 minutes).
> - **Label** — Atlantic → (left) → Big Loud Rock.
> - **Tracks** — 13.

## At a glance

- **Era** — the debut. The origin record, built roughly 2012–2018 across Joshua Tree, Florence, Haiti, a remote coastal town in Ireland, and Lake Arrowhead, with producers S1, Linus, and Matt Pauling.
- **What shifts** — this is where the whole cosmology starts: the survival-self that later hardens into [[Sir Lucius]], the method of building songs out of borrowed older voices, and the very first time Roy says [[The Circle]] aloud (2019, on "Move To You"). None of it is named yet — that's the honest part.
- **Who's present** — [[Roy]], as the wounded, hopeful self the project keeps trying to return to. The *protector* who becomes [[Sir Lucius]] is already at work here, caught mid-formation in the mirror — but he has no face and no name in 2018.
- **Start with** — [[Loser]] (the breakthrough, and the lens Roy chose to open the album with), then [[Move To You]] (the keystone), then [[Shine]] (the ending).

## What it is

*Subject to Flooding* is the first Jagwar Twin album, and its surface is a deliberate *we-not-me* humanism. At launch Roy called Jagwar Twin "a collective consciousness," said "we're all really mirrors to each other," and offered the title's own gloss: with the world fragile "emotionally, physically, environmentally, and politically we should all let go of ego… After all, we are all Subject to Flooding." He opened the record with "Loser" on purpose, calling it "the lens to view the rest of the Jagwar Twin world through."

The name arrived mid-recording. Roy had been putting music out under his own name, Roy English (born Brandon Roy Wronski), but somewhere in the sessions the scale of it changed: "This is big. This is not about us anymore… I didn't feel right putting it out as a Roy English album… because it's not." His own decode of "Jagwar Twin": the jaguar is "the creature who looks into the souls of other people and then, in turn, can look back into itself — humans are just mirrors for each other," and the twin is "the dual nature — the light and the dark, the yin and the yang," plus, he adds, the plain fact that he's a Gemini.

The title carries two meanings at once, and Roy's own framing holds both. The **flood is a threat** — the record says it outright, with heads going underwater and a sea you're going to drown in. But the flood is also the **cure**: letting the ego-walls fall so people can reach each other again. "We are all subject to flooding" means both *we can all drown* and *the walls between us can all come down* — vulnerability and communion as the same exposure. Years later Roy named the flood as polarization he'd felt coming: "if we keep separating into more and more separate pieces… we're gonna hit the metaphorical flood," a pattern he watched arrive as COVID and mass polarization, resolving toward "we need to come together" — which is where he says the next album was headed.

That double-valence is not a critic's overlay; it's built from four things Roy put on the record and in the room. "Loser" renders a wall's collapse *as water* — "Jericho fell like a wave" — and in the Bible Jericho's wall falls to collective sound, which is the [[The Circle|Circle]]'s mechanism (the crowd singing it back) eight years early. Roy described the album's own making as "that breaking down of walls," insisted "there is only perceived separation," and topped it with "let go of ego… we are all Subject to Flooding." What the flood destroys is the separation the album diagnoses as the disease. The title contains its own remedy.

## The arc

Underneath the night-to-morning surface, the album does something more specific: it runs from **reflection to radiance.** It opens in a mirror — [[Long Time Coming]], the disguise "getting clearer," a self studying its own reflected light — and it closes in [[Shine]], a self no longer studying reflected light but trying to *become* it. That deeper movement is the album's real spine, and it doubles as its hidden architecture: the formation and defense of a survival-self, the protector who later becomes Lucius, operating quietly beneath the hopeful register.

The album's move in the larger story is that it is **the seedbed.** *33* (2022) is the bloom; the Lucius era (2026) is the calcification. Listening back you can find the protector mid-operation, the borrowed-voices method already running, the Machine-vs-Human wound already open, and the Circle already spoken. But the era's contemporaneous vocabulary has no Lucius, no Circus, no Algorithm, no mirrors-as-metaphysics. The record *sincerely participates* in the language — dreams, masks, "we are all losers" — that the later work goes back and reinterprets. Reading its protector as a *villain* imports a verdict the survival-self hadn't yet earned: the disguise, the shame-preemption, the vibe-defense, the social filter were structures that *worked*. The danger was never that they existed; it's that they can calcify, until adaptation hardens into identity and the mask mistakes itself for the source. That hardening is the Lucius arc. Here, the protector is still just doing its job.

The single sharpest edge of the split: the very song where Roy *first voices the Circle* — "Move To You" — is the one Lucius later buries as a forgotten flop. The shadow's "dead weight" is the cosmology's origin point.

## Major songs

- [[Long Time Coming]] — the disguise becomes conscious ("the disguise on my face getting clearer"), the earliest mask-image in the whole catalogue. Travis Barker drums *and* takes a writing credit; the flattened-identity line ("rich man, poor man… nobody's free") is the Circus blueprint eight years early.
- [[Dream - Dream|Dream / Dream]] — built on Johnny Mercer's 1944 standard, and the album's only *spelled* hook (D-R-E-A-M). Roy keeps Mercer's gentle imperative but flips the posture from private consolation to communal invitation — "dream out loud," "come and dream that dream with me." An awakening that still believes the prison is external.
- [[Loser]] — the breakthrough. Claim the slur first ("I'm a loser, baby, just like you") and the wound becomes solidarity; the origin was a blog older kids once ran to mock his singing. Found in the studio when Linus muted everything and played one old guitar riff over it — "removing everything that brought it to life." The children's choir on the pre-chorus is deliberate: the sound of "that childlike self before people told you you were different."
- [[Good Day]] — the success-pole twin of "Loser," brightness attached to circumstance. "I'm on a wave" — on an album titled *Subject to Flooding*, our man is cheerfully surfing the flood everyone else is drowning in. Same wave, opposite relation. Also home to the deadpan flex that is secretly the whole thesis: "I don't ski but I look fresh."
- [[Superhuman (Interlude)|Superhuman (Interlude)]] — a verbatim relay of Charlie Chaplin's closing speech from *The Great Dictator* (1940): "the kingdom of God is within man… you, the people, have the power… let us all unite." The Machine-vs-Human thread opens here, in a borrowed 1940 voice.
- [[Superhuman]] — the human reaching, collectively, for the divine power the interlude just named: "all we are is human / but I'll try to be Superhuman."
- [[Move To You]] — the keystone. The heart as navigator ("even if the stars are out of place… I can hear your heart, it moves to me, I move to you"), Roy's stated favorite, "a prayer to and from the universe" that "felt given to me." And the song where he first says, aloud, "there is no beginning and no end — it's just a circle."
- [[No Fake Friends|N.F.F. (No Fake Friends)]] — the protector's social-filtration layer; belonging made conditional under collapse ("where did my friends go when I had nothing?"). "Looked down at my stars" — the heavens flattened into the Walk of Fame sidewalk and walked on.
- [[Roy Voice Memo - S1 Arrowhead|Roy Voice Memo: S1 Arrowhead]] — a roughly 42-second studio interlude, S1 counseling Roy about "the fear of when it happens," "the bubble bursting and then you're trying to find pieces." The documentary rupture between the fame section and the descent — two real people, briefly, inside the album's "we."
- [[Hell Of A Night]] — celebration with a blade in it ("if I get locked up, promise you'll bail me out… hands up and wave 'em to the ceiling"). "Hands up" doubles party and stickup, setting up Part 2.
- [[Hell of a Night Pt. 2 (The Hallelujah Stickup)|Hell Of A Night Pt. 2 (The Hallelujah Stickup)]] — sacred-as-heist: a fictional armed robbery scored with relentless praise. The title alone — a church shout welded to a stickup — is the whole joke-that-isn't-a-joke. And one track after the fake-detector song, "fake it like your passport": forge the documents to cross the border.
- [[Precious Time]] — where the title surfaces ("we never see our depth till we're underwater… waist deep, wasting time") and the personal story widens into mortality and mass death. The album's moral crisis — and a genuinely good pun (waist/waste) doing mortal-serious work.
- [[Shine]] — the closer rises, sparked, of all things, by Roy watching *Troy* and wanting a chorus that "felt happy, like the sun rising." It acknowledges the flood without denying it ("you're in the sea so you're gonna drown") and answers with chosen, not naive, hope. "A song is a mirror. I just want to be a mirror."

## Advanced reading

> [!note]- Source vs Reflection — the album's master key
> Underneath the catalogue's later splits (machine vs human, Circle vs Loop) sits an older, deeper binary this album already poses: **does a reflection return you to the source, or trap you in the image?** The album's whole world is mediated — mirror, dream, sample, mask, fame, friends, crowd, money, memory — and every one of those layers can either *transmit* a true self or *substitute* for it. A mirror can reveal the disguise or imprison you in it; a dream can guide or harden into inherited programming; fame can reflect you back or counterfeit you. The machine, in this frame, isn't a new problem — it's the late-stage, industrial-strength reflection-engine, the same danger at higher resolution. The album never names this; it's a framework-level reading. But it's the deepest thing the debut is doing, and it's why the record reads as a single argument rather than a track list.
>
> The resolving movement is **reflection → radiance.** It opens studying reflected light (the disguise "getting clearer" — recursive awareness without integration) and closes trying to *become* light ("Shine"). Not a rejection of reflection, but a recovery of the source behind it. And it shows up as two brightnesses on the same record: **"Good Day" is the false dawn** — light attached to circumstance (money, weather, motion, "I look fresh"), the kind that can harden into a mask — and **"Shine" is the earned dawn**, light that has passed *through* the flood (betrayal, drowning, the mortality of "Precious Time") and is chosen anyway. The reflected kind traps; the radiant kind returns to source. The closer reads as expensive, not casual, precisely because track 12 made the light pass through the water first.

> [!note]- The protector before he had a face — one formation, three modes
> The protector shows up as a single formation in three modes: **shame-preemption** ("Loser" — claim the slur before it can land), **mood-defense** ("Good Day" — "can't kill my vibe," a perimeter thrown up around a feeling), and **social filtration** ("N.F.F." — exclusion as policy, the list and the radar). The clearest hinge between this era and the Lucius era is a single word: *vibe*. "Good Day" protects it; a 2026 Lucius song weaponizes it — the same medium, a different operator. The tell is that "Good Day" clenches at the moment of *relief*, not threat: the wound is closing ("I had nothing almost three years"), and exactly there the defenses spike, because relief is what would make the protector unnecessary. "I might jump 'cause I feel untouchable" is that logic at full inflation — and "untouchable" has two meanings, *protected* and *unreachable*, which turn out to be the same condition. That is the Lucius condition in seed. He accumulates here; only the billing changes later.

> [!note]- The inherited-substrate method — ancestors, not decorations
> A signature is already running: building songs out of **borrowed voices.** Chaplin (his *Great Dictator* speech, 1940), Johnny Mercer ("Dream," 1944), and Sarah Vaughan are sampled, named, and built into the song-bodies — the past curated and relayed, not merely invented. One 1957 Vaughan recording of "Dream" feeds two tracks at once: the song itself (track 2) and the spoken banter *before* the song (sampled on "Precious Time," track 12). The project doesn't create from nothing; it receives, rearranges, and retransmits — the discipline the later work mythologizes as "the song handed down and called back." (There's also a fourth substrate, quieter: Roy's mother, who filled the house with "Celtic chants and amazing African drumming, Native American flute music," plus Joni Mitchell and Bob Dylan. That inherited *sound-world* is the most grounded reading of the "Navajo blood in the veins" line — music in the blood, not an ancestry claim.)
>
> One co-writer stays open: "Essex," credited on "Good Day," remains genuinely unidentified — most plausibly a writer or producer alias rather than a sample clearance. (The old "Terry Knight" and "David Essex" attributions were both false leads.)

> [!note]- The methods were debut-native (a date-floor, not a plan)
> Several methods usually credited to the later era are already operating here — as *craft*, not finished mythology:
> - **Mantra as reprogramming.** In 2018, on "Loser"'s repetition: "there's freedom in the repetition of that phrase," "the more you tell yourself something out loud, the more you actually start to believe it," "the metaphorical sunshine in the mind is reprogramming inside ourselves." This is the "chorus is like a mantra / Trojan Horse" doctrine the catalogue formalizes years later — same doctrine, floored at 2018.
> - **Subtraction as revelation.** "Loser" was found by *muting* the track — "removing everything that brought it to life." The catalogue keeps re-enacting this at larger scales; a later song is finished by removing its words entirely.
> - **The nursery-rhyme substrate.** "Long Time Coming"'s "rich man, poor man… prophet and priest" is the "Tinker, Tailor, Soldier, Sailor" counting rhyme — the playground fortune-telling game that assigns your identity by chance — inherited and *answered*: the rhyme says chance deals you rich-or-poor, the song says the deal doesn't matter, "all of us bleed." (A ranking system telling children what they'll be is, incidentally, a proto-Algorithm.) The "twisted takes on adolescent musical themes" method was running at track 1.
> - **Chaplin's distribution theology.** The interlude's borrowed line — "not in one man, nor a group of men, but in all men" — is the exact anti-hierarchical, anti-guru structure ("everyone's a guru") the catalogue spends eight years rebuilding in its own myth. The album's "we, not me" was never just solidarity; it was already a theology of distribution: the treasure is in everyone or it's nowhere.

> [!note]- Threads that run out of here into 33 and the Lucius era
> - **The Circle** — first named aloud on "Move To You" during this album's press cycle (2019), three years before *33*'s closer formalizes it. This is the literal receipt for the "flood-rich soil" framing, and it refutes the skeptical read that the Circle mythology was retrofitted from later marketing. The word is documented; the system it later anchors is not — but the word is there.
> - **The stars, sky to soul.** A consistent devaluation of the external star and enthronement of the internal one: "even if the stars are out of place" (2018) → "looked down at my stars" (flattened to the sidewalk, 2018) → "the stars in the sky conspired" (2025) → answered by "your soul is a star" (2022). Navigate by the one star that can't be out of place.
> - **The timebomb.** "Click on your clock radio / ain't got much time till I blow" (2018) → "ticking like a timebomb" (2020) → the id finally erupting, "ready to blow," in 2026. Suppression pressurizes; in "Loser" the bomb is even woken by media (the clock radio clicks the countdown on).
> - **The pieces.** S1's "trying to find pieces" (the voice memo, track 9) and Roy's "one little piece of the puzzle" (2018) — the shattered self and the participatory self — fuse years later into "I'm in pieces, looking for pieces of one piece."
> - **The face-inventory.** "The disguise on my face" → "for the faceless" → "face in the crowd" → *Happy Face* (2022) → face-paint (2023 on). The album conducts a face-census before the mask era literalizes it. (Direction matters: 2018 dedicates the song *to* the faceless; the later era has the faceless one *deliver* the blessing.)
> - **Machine vs Human.** Chaplin's 1940 "power to create machines" (external, visible) → *33*'s ambient screens and feeds → Lucius's fully internalized "the algorithm, baby." The antagonist is never the machine; it's optimization severed from the heart.

> [!note]- Framework correspondences (esoteric — never authorial intent)
> Read through the traditions Roy publicly draws on, the flood is a **threshold** image — the old order overwhelmed so a new one can emerge — and the album resonates with the *nigredo*, the "blackening" or dissolution stage that precedes transformation. On that frame, *Subject to Flooding* diagnoses the blackening (especially in "Long Time Coming") without yet resolving it, and the closer's turn from *seeing reflected light* to *trying to become light* reads as the first move toward the radiance the later work pursues. The flood-as-cleansing also maps, at resonance level, onto the Aquarian "cleansing of the stables" labour — the systemic flushing of accumulated rigid material before integration. And the album *can* be read as a descent of light down the Tree of Life, opening in a source-register and closing in embodied radiance on the earth-plane. These are correspondences the system permits, not claims about what Roy set out to encode. His stated meaning for the title stays the grounded floor: let go of ego; we are all subject to flooding.

## Source notes

- **Grounded (Roy's own words, released music, public posts, official videos).** The release and label history; the 13-track list; the wilderness-years account and the "I had no money to make it" framing; the title's stated meaning and its retrospective polarization gloss; the "Jagwar Twin" decode and the mid-recording naming; opening with "Loser" as "the lens"; the "Loser" origin (the blog, the muting, the children's choir) and the mask thesis stated in 2018; "Move To You" as Roy's favorite and a "download," and the first Circle naming attached to it; the Chaplin, Mercer, and Vaughan credits and the borrowed-voices method; "Shine"'s stated intent and the *Troy* anecdote; "Precious Time" as "about the time we have now moved into"; the mantra-as-reprogramming and subtraction-as-revelation craft statements; the mother's records as the first sound-world. A guard worth keeping: the "Shine" lyric is "gender of my generation" — some transcriptions mangle it into a celebrity surname; that's a scraping error, not a buried pun.
- **Strong readings.** Reflection → radiance as the album's spine; the two brightnesses (false dawn / earned dawn); the protector's three modes and the *vibe* hinge; the stars / timebomb / pieces / face threads; the flood's double-valence; the nursery-rhyme substrate and Chaplin's distribution theology; Source vs Reflection as the master key. Grounded in the material, but interpretive — held as such.
- **Framework (esoteric, syncretic — never authorial intent).** The nigredo, the threshold/flood-as-cleansing correspondence, the Tree-of-Life descent. The system permits these; it does not assert Roy intends them.
- **How this could be wrong.** If the plain "let go of ego" reading accounts for the record as well as the esoteric apparatus does, the apparatus is decorative. And the biggest genuine gap: for a *we, not me* album, the collaborators — S1, Linus, Jeff Bhasker, Matt Pauling, Michael Morgan — have never given substantive on-record interviews about it. The highest-value missing material is other voices.

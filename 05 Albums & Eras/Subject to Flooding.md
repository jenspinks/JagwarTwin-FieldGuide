# Subject to Flooding

> **One-line reading.** The 2018 debut — a record made in the wilderness years that diagnoses the disease the rest of the catalogue spends years treating, and quietly contains the seed of everything that comes after.

*Subject to Flooding* is the first Jagwar Twin album, released September 21, 2018 (expanded by Atlantic Records the following spring). It's the soil: *33* (2022) and the Lucius era (2026) are the bloom. Listening back, you can already find the protective survival-self that hardens into [[Sir Lucius]], the method of building songs out of borrowed older voices, and the very first time Roy names [[The Circle]] aloud. But the honest framing matters — those things are in retrospect. The 2018 record sincerely participates in the language (dreams, masks, "we are all losers") that the later work goes back and reinterprets. The seed was in the soil; it just wasn't planted on purpose.

## Overview

The album was built across roughly 2012–2018 — Joshua Tree, Florence, Haiti, Ireland, Lake Arrowhead — with producers including S1 and Linus, on almost no money. Its surface is a deliberate *we-not-me* humanism: in Roy's words at launch, *"Jagwar Twin is a collective consciousness,"* *"we're all really mirrors to each other,"* and *"we are all Subject to Flooding."* He opened the record with "Loser" on purpose, calling it *"the lens to view the rest of the Jagwar Twin world through."*

The title carries two meanings at once, and Roy's own framing holds both. The **flood is a threat** — the record says it outright, with heads going underwater and a sea you're going to drown in. But the flood is also the **cure**: letting the ego-walls fall so people can reach each other again. *"We are all subject to flooding"* means both *we can all drown* and *the walls between us can all come down* — vulnerability and communion as the same exposure. Years later Roy described the flood as a pattern he'd felt coming: separation and polarization rising until everything spills over.

## Major releases

The album runs thirteen tracks. Its hidden architecture is the formation and defense of a survival-self — the protector that later becomes Lucius — operating underneath that hopeful surface.

- [[Long Time Coming]] — the disguise becomes conscious: *"I see the disguise on my face getting clearer."* The earliest mask-image in the whole catalogue.
- [[Dream - Dream|Dream / Dream]] — built on Johnny Mercer's 1944 "Dream"; an awakening that still believes the prison is external.
- [[Loser]] — the breakthrough single. Shame-preemption: claim the slur first (*"I'm a loser, baby, just like you"*) and the wound becomes solidarity.
- [[Good Day]] — the success-pole twin of "Loser": brightness attached to circumstance, a false dawn. *"I'm on a wave"* — surfing the flood instead of drowning in it.
- [[Superhuman (Interlude)|Superhuman (Interlude)]] — a verbatim relay of Charlie Chaplin's closing speech from *The Great Dictator* (1940): *"the kingdom of God is within man… you, the people, have the power."*
- [[Superhuman]] — the human reaching for divine power, collectively.
- [[Move To You]] — the keystone. The heart as navigator (*"I can hear your heart… I move to you"*), and the song where Roy first says *"there is no beginning and no end — it's just a circle."*
- [[No Fake Friends|N.F.F. (No Fake Friends)]] — the protector's social-filtration layer: exclusion as policy.
- [[Hell Of A Night]] — celebration with a blade in it.
- [[Hell of a Night Pt. 2 (The Hallelujah Stickup)|Hell Of A Night Pt. 2]] — the sacred fused with a heist; praise sung over sirens.
- [[Precious Time]] — where the title surfaces (*"we never see our depth till we're underwater"*) and the personal story widens into mortality and mass death. The album's moral crisis.
- [[Shine]] — the closer rises, acknowledging the flood without denying it and answering with chosen, not naive, hope.

## Who's present

This is a record before the cast has faces. **[[Roy]]** is here as the wounded, hopeful self that the whole project will keep trying to return to. **[[Sir Lucius]]** isn't named — but the *protector* he eventually becomes is already operating, caught mid-formation in the mirror. The protector shows up as one formation in three modes: shame-preemption ("Loser"), mood-defense ("Good Day"), and social filtration ("N.F.F."). The clearest hinge is a single word — *"vibe."* "Good Day" protects it (*"can't kill my vibe"*); a 2026 Lucius song later weaponizes it. The figure accumulates here; only the billing changes later.

## What it's about

Underneath the night-to-morning arc, the album poses a question the whole catalogue keeps asking: **does a reflection return you to the source, or trap you in the image?** The album's world is entirely mediated — mirror, dream, sample, mask, fame, friends, crowd, money, memory — and every one of those layers can either *transmit* a true self or *substitute* for it. A mirror can reveal the disguise or imprison you in it; fame can reflect you back or counterfeit you. That's the deepest thing the debut is doing, and it sits above the catalogue's later splits (machine vs. human, the Circle vs. the Loop) as the common shape underneath them.

There's also a method here that becomes a signature: building songs out of **inherited voices**. Chaplin, Mercer, and Sarah Vaughan are sampled, named, and built into the song-bodies — the past curated and relayed, not just invented. It's the discipline that the later work mythologizes as *the song handed down and called back.*

The honest caveat — and the album insists on it — is that the protector is **not evil at its origin.** The disguise, the shame-preemption, the vibe-defense, the social filter: these are survival structures that *worked*. The danger isn't that they exist; it's that they can calcify, until adaptation hardens into identity and the mask mistakes itself for the source. That hardening is the Lucius arc. Here, the protector is still just doing its job.

## A framework reading

Read through the esoteric traditions Roy draws on, the flood is a **threshold** image — the old order overwhelmed so a new one can emerge — and the album resonates with the *nigredo*, the "blackening" or dissolution stage that precedes transformation. On that frame, *Subject to Flooding* diagnoses the blackening (especially in "Long Time Coming") without yet resolving it, and the closer's turn from *seeing reflected light* to *trying to become light* reads as the first move toward the radiance the later work pursues. These are framework correspondences, not claims about what Roy set out to encode — his stated meaning for the title stays the grounded floor: *let go of ego; we are all subject to flooding.*

## Suggested listening order

The album already sequences its own arc, so the album order is the reading order. If you want the throughline in miniature:

1. [[Long Time Coming]] — the disguise gets clearer
2. [[Loser]] — the wound turned into solidarity
3. [[Move To You]] — the heart as navigator; the Circle first spoken
4. [[Precious Time]] — the descent; the title surfaces
5. [[Shine]] — chosen hope, after the flood

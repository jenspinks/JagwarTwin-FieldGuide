# Lucius Lullaby (album)

> **One-line reading.** The 2026 album where the shadow gets the microphone — a sustained descent held almost to the end, in which the only thing that finally resolves isn't the man's inner war but the survival of the feeling underneath it.

> [!info] Release facts
> **Released** March 27, 2026 · **Artists** [[Jagwar Twin]] & [[Sir Lucius]] (joint billing — the first time the project shares credit with the voice it was built to contain) · **Label** Big Loud Rock, under exclusive license to Mercury Records (UMG) · **15 tracks, ~36 minutes** · **Tagline** *"Twisted takes on adolescent musical themes for hands that rock the cradle."*
> ▶ *Streaming: [add link]*

## At a glance

- **Era** — 2026. The record that hands [[Sir Lucius]] the mic. His name is on the marquee, his production runs the whole thing, and he takes over the album's premiere. The rollout ran a seven-part theatrical series ([[The Interview]]) that doubles as the album's plot.
- **What shifts** — the project's cosmology gets *personified into a machine.* Where [[33]] closed by waking up, this album opens by being sung back to sleep. The descent isn't diagnosed from the outside anymore; it's lived from inside.
- **Who's present** — [[Sir Lucius]] (author and narrator; he brackets the record and dominates the opening third) · [[Roy]] (the self underneath, put *under* rather than woken; his breath is what it comes down to) · [[Mary]] (the one crack in the floor, on "#1 Champion") · the [[Ballerina Boy]] (the buried child the whole descent circles).
- **Start with** — [[Welcome to the Circus]] (Lucius at full power) → [[Lucius Lullaby]] (the floor and the bargain) → [[Weirdness]] (the turn) → [[SOL]] (the wordless destination).

## What it is

Fifteen tracks, about thirty-six minutes, and the shape of the thing is the point. Read in order, the album is a long floor cracked once near the end: nearly every track sits in the blackening, and only one late turn — track 13, [[Weirdness]] — breaks into first light. The aesthetic is nursery rhyme turned sinister, and it's grounded right there in the lyrics: *ring-around-the-rosie* and *"ashes, ashes, we all fall down,"* a dog-and-god dark-nursery, *"go bananas,"* the Oompa-Loompa jingle re-voiced as a threat, a music-box lullaby. Childhood forms, in the hands that rock the cradle. (Note the ambiguity in the name: *this* is the **album**; there's also a **song**, [[Lucius Lullaby]], which is track 2.)

A structural detail worth knowing going in, and one heard firsthand at the listening and release parties: several of the "tracks" are really **one continuous piece split in two.** [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]] is the music-box overture that runs straight into the title track (both carry *"It's a beautiful life… I'm dreaming"*); [[Playing to the Gods]] is the refrain [[City of Angels]] develops (both carry *"never gonna die"*); and [[Moths to the Flame]]'s spoken-word bleeds without a seam into [[Bananas]]. Three seams, all confirmed by ear — though two of the three work differently from the last. Two carry a shared refrain that appears *only* between the pair (the *"I'm dreaming"* and *"never gonna die"* couplings); the third, Moths→Bananas, bleeds without a seam as a music/video pull-back — a descending, near-wordless vocalization and a slow reveal that the sermon was preached from atop a ten-foot pile of bananas — with no shared refrain at all. The album uses short intro tracks as overtures whose refrain the next full track expands — so the running order does real work.

The credits hold a quiet payoff for anyone who's followed Roy's influences, because a few of them are physically *on* the record. Josh Dun of Twenty One Pilots — the band Roy has called his favorite ever — drums on two tracks. Sia is a credited co-writer and backing vocalist on "Weirdness," the album's one turn into the light. And the childhood source "Bad Feeling" twists, the Oompa-Loompa song, brings its original *Willy Wonka* writers (Leslie Bricusse and Anthony Newley) onto the credits as co-writers of the interpolation. The album that recodes inherited songs also pulls some of its inheritances into the room. There's a continuity thread, too: Matthew Pauling, a core collaborator on [[33]], recurs on three tracks here — "Playing to the Gods," "City of Angels," and "Weirdness," the same 33-core hand steadying the seams and the turn — and little luna (the "Pay Attention" feature from that album) sings background on "Great Time to Be Human." Beneath the marquee, the workaday spine is consistent: David Burris co-writes and produces the two title-adjacent tracks ("Lucius Lullaby" and the acoustic "Bad Feeling" close), Tony Esterly and Cici Ward co-write "#1 Champion," and Dale Becker masters every track (Noah McCorkle co-mastering "Not Your Homie") — one house sound holding fifteen tracks of deliberate stylistic whiplash together.

## The arc

The engine is set in the first two tracks: **a life saved by being put *under* rather than woken.** The title track names the floor — rock-bottom at thirty-five, in plain first person — and the bargain that pulls the speaker back from the edge (*"you gotta serve somebody, so stop talking and listen"*). The rest of the album is Lucius's world running unopposed: a sedation dressed as a *"beautiful life."* It's the inverse of the debut's flood. Where [[Subject to Flooding]] *dissolves* the divided self in water, *Lucius Lullaby* *sedates* it in a dream — same severing, opposite mechanism, drowning versus the beautiful-life sleep. A lie that keeps you alive turns out to be more dangerous than one that doesn't.

The album measures its own distance by a grim little symmetry. Its darkest line — a plain first-person thought of ending one's life — is sung **twice**: once at the bottom, in the title track, and once again at the turn, in "Weirdness." Same despair, two places. The first time, the only voice that answers is the bargain that puts the speaker under. The second time, the blessing-voice lands. What resolves between them is one pronoun. The angel-voice's *"**your** weirdness is the sunshine"* becomes *"all **my** weirdness is **my** sunshine."* Integration happens not by defeating the shadow but by **reclaiming his distorted light as your own** — which is why the album can resolve without anyone winning.

And here's the thing it refuses: **triumph, but not resolution.** It's tempting to read the loop back to Lucius at the very end as the shadow simply winning, but that conflates two different things. What resolves is not the integration of Roy and Lucius, which stays open. What resolves is the **survival of the feeling** that was never sacrificed: "SOL" endures even with its words stripped away; the breath outlasts the bargain. The album resolves by *preserving what cannot be metabolized*, not by completing the cure. "Doesn't end in triumph" is not the same as "stays in shadow" — the shadow gets the last *track*, but the feeling gets the last thing the shadow can't consume.

Read across the catalogue, this is where the long machine-versus-human thread lands its endgame. The record stops *diagnosing* the severing and starts being lived inside it: [[City of Angels]] renders Hollywood as the paradise version of being already uploaded — deathless and identical, *"everyone looks just like you"* — the [[The Circle|Circle]]'s communion of distinct voices returning a song, forged into the [[The Circus|Circus]]'s counterfeit of it, sameness, pointedly on a record by a project called Jagwar *Twin.*

## Major songs

The album moves in five movements: an overture and a bargain, a long stretch of the Circus operating, a single turn, a wordless destination, and a loop back to the shadow.

- [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]] — the founding doctrine retold as a music-box lullaby; the overture. Keeps the sacred song's *name* and swaps its content for *"I'm dreaming."*
- [[Lucius Lullaby]] — the title track: rock-bottom named in the plainest first person, and the bargain that pulls the speaker back from the ledge.
- [[Great Time to Be Human]] — a toxic-positivity civilization anthem where *"the crooks and believers all sing the same song,"* plus the album's finest dark-nursery pun (the dyslexic chemist who orders a dog and gets a god).
- [[Welcome to the Circus]] — the Circus thesis; Lucius at full external power, cheerfully self-crowned: *"I'm the final boss."* It's the middle rung of a self-naming ladder that climbs across the era — *"I'm a mastermind"* ([[Good Time (1-08)]]) → *"I'm the final boss"* (here) → *"I'm the one they're talking 'bout."* The escalation is the tell: each title is grander and emptier than the last, because Lucius only exists as long as the room keeps saying his name. The final boss needs the arcade to stay plugged in.
- [[Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)]] — the candy-factory alarm that is also the candy factory's product. The beloved Willy Wonka jingle, re-voiced as a warning about the singer.
- [[Moths to the Flame]] — Lucius's spoken-word anti-civilization sermon, delivered from atop a ten-foot pile of bananas, and staged as a villain TED talk that ends on a mic-drop: *"build your own table… Get out of the way. You're welcome."* The overture to —
- [[Bananas]] — the sung id-eruption it licenses: *"Hey kids! Do you ever wanna break shit?"* The dark-nursery aesthetic handed to the cradle as an invitation. It also carries the era's signature staged gag: Lucius cues his own chart-toppers by pressing a browning banana like a play-button (in [[The Interview]] the same banana-button triggers [[Not Your Homie]] and [[Welcome to the Circus]]) — the [[The Banana|banana]] is at once the fruit going soft and the button that runs the hits.
- [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] — the boasting ego meets the faceless feminine ([[Mary]] — *"saw a halo but I didn't see a head"*) and gets unseated. The album's first crack in the floor.
- [[Playing to the Gods]] — the false-immortality refrain (*"never gonna die"*) that opens straight into —
- [[City of Angels]] — its full development; the "upload." Fame as deathless reproducibility, the counterfeit of communion.
- [[Not Your Homie]] — the bargain enacted: the commercial hit Lucius pushes over Roy's love song. An introvert's anthem set to *"ashes, ashes, we all fall down."*
- [[Bounce]] — kinetic evasion; the panic question posed and the structure answering *don't stop.* Also the album's most self-aware wink: *"you just love me for my lore."*
- [[Weirdness]] — the turn, and the only track to reach first light. The whole change is one reclaimed pronoun, and this time the blessing-voice ("you were born to shine") actually lands.
- [[SOL]] — "The Song of Love," released *voiced but wordless:* the words exist, but they're stripped, and only the breath survives. The destination, glimpsed rather than reached.
- *Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)* — acoustic live at the MOTH Gallery, a quiet Lucius-adjacent bonus that loops the record back toward its opening side.

## Advanced reading

> [!note]- The Circle hinge — 33 ends where this album begins
> This is the cleanest structural relationship between any two albums in the catalogue, and it runs on a single doctrine turned 180 degrees. [[33]] (2022) **closes** on [[The Circle]] — remembrance, awakening, *"the inside of the inside."* *Lucius Lullaby* (2026) **opens** on [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]] — the same named song, but its entire lyric swapped to *"I'm dreaming."* One album ends by waking up; the next opens by being sung back to sleep. Play them back to back and the seam between the records is the doctrine handing off to its own counterfeit.
>
> The move is **appropriation, not deletion.** The shadow doesn't erase the keystone doctrine; he keeps its name and hands you a sedative, so you think you've reached the cure while being put under. On the project's source-versus-reflection axis, this is the exact pivot from *source* (the remembered song) to *reflection* (the dreamed image, the beautiful-life sleep). As always: the facts here — the closer/opener placement, the kept name, the swapped words — are solid; "doctrine deliberately inverted" is the interpretive layer laid over them.

> [!note]- The John 8:32 decay — one Bible verse, corrupted across three eras
> A single scripture — *"the truth will set you free"* — recurs across three songs in three eras, and it degrades each time as it passes from Roy's mouth to Lucius's. Held **sincerely** on *33* (2022): *"the truth will set you free / but the truth is hard to tell."* **Paradoxed** on "Bad Feeling" (2023, now track 5): *"the truth is a lie that nobody can tell."* And **quoted-then-bent** by the shadow-voice in the "Lucius Lullaby" outro (2026): *"the truth will set you free, but nothing's, nothing's what it seems."*
>
> The last stage is the serpent's exact rhetorical move — cite the scripture, then undercut it — which is why the title track's outro reads as the tempter's signature rather than a sincere citation. And pointedly, *two of the three stages sit on this one album* (tracks 5 and 2), so the record internally stages the final leg of a verse's corruption. It sharpens the album's whole "truth" texture: two tracks apart, *"the truth is a lie that nobody can tell"* and *"nothing's what it seems"* are the same destabilization, dismantling the possibility of a stable truth with one Bible verse as the through-line.

> [!note]- The Dylan spine — the moral arc turns on one borrowed verb
> The title track's bargain, *"you gotta serve somebody,"* is Bob Dylan's — from "Gotta Serve Somebody" (1979), whose thesis is *you will serve; the only question is whom.* This isn't an inferred allusion: Roy captioned the official video short of "lucius lullaby" with *"'You're gonna have to serve somebody.' — Bob Dylan."* The artist named his own source. (Dylan is also a childhood influence, via Roy's mother, who played him and Joni Mitchell around the house — the same maternal channel the album's "hands that rock the cradle" is *about:* what is sung over a child becomes what the child sings.)
>
> The whole album's moral arc turns on that one transitive verb, redirected. Both of its poles command service. Lucius's bargain — *you gotta **serve** somebody* — against Roy's stated resolution elsewhere: *my mind must **serve** my heart.* The integration is the same bargain answered in its own grammar: the servant, the mind, reassigned from the external master (the label, the metric, Lucius) to the internal source, the heart. The fight was never over *whether* to serve. It's over the verb's object.
>
> An honest negative, so nobody re-runs the hunt: beyond "serve somebody," there is no second textual Dylan contact anywhere on the album. His grounded presence here is exactly one line, plus the caption and the biographical floor.

> [!note]- The seams as the Circus rendered as track-architecture
> All three seams are *descent-couplings.* In each, a short, often near-wordless piece — the overture, the doctrine, the mantra — opens into the full sung track that realizes it. The form itself models Lucius's method: a small seed-cell that expands to fill a room. The three shortest tracks on the album (The Circle LV at 0:44, Playing to the Gods at 1:05, SOL at 1:18) are the seam-heads and edges; tracks 1 and 14 bracket the whole record, and two of the three are near-wordless (music box; breath). The editorial choice — where to split the tracks — is exactly where the breath carries through.

> [!note]- Why the comedy is the tell — the joke is the anesthetic
> Lucius is genuinely funny, and that's the mechanism, not a garnish. A villain who threatened would trip the alarm; a villain who *entertains* gets waved through. Every laugh is a dose of the same sedative the album is about — the bit lands, the guard drops, and the bargain gets signed while you're grinning. So the gags are worth cataloguing as evidence, because the register itself is the argument.
>
> The staged bits: he cheerfully crowns himself the *"final boss"*; he runs his own chart-toppers by pressing a browning banana like a play-button (the signature gag — same button triggers [[Not Your Homie]] and [[Welcome to the Circus]] across [[The Interview]]); he preaches the [[Moths to the Flame|Moths]] sermon from atop a ten-foot pile of bananas and closes it like a TED talk — *"build your own table… Get out of the way. You're welcome."* The lyric jabs: *"Hey kids! Do you ever wanna break shit?"* as a nursery invitation; the boast about *"singing songs about sticking pencils in people's eyes"*; the fourth-wall wink *"you just love me for my lore."* And the personal needling aimed straight at Roy — that he can now *afford to dress like a… yeti*, and the *"37, living at your parents' house"* pressure-lever, the shadow poking the exact shame the bargain was built to numb.
>
> Read together they scan as stand-up, which is the point: the funniest villain in the catalogue is also the most dangerous one, because [[Subject to Flooding|the flood]] announces itself and the *lullaby* makes you laugh on the way down. This is characterization laid over the bits, not a claim the jokes were written as a thesis — but the pattern (charm as the delivery system for sedation) is consistent enough that the comedy reads as the diagnosis, not the relief from it.

> [!note]- The singles runway — where Lucius was assembled before the album
> The album doesn't arrive from nowhere; three era singles build the machine part by part, and each is a full precursor thread in its own right. [[Good Time (1-08)]] (2023) is the **birth**: Sir Lucius is named into being out of a Roy TikTok dream, the *"I'm a mastermind"* self-crowning that opens the naming ladder. [[The Watchers]] (2024) is the **clone-fear** — a Rockwell *"Somebody's Watching Me"* interpolation that seeds the deathless-sameness terror the album pays off in [[City of Angels]]. [[Ballerina Boy]] (2025) is the **buried child** the whole descent circles, and it closes on a Mary-class blessing outro (*"let your light shine"*) that rhymes forward into "Weirdness." By the time the album opens, the villain, his fear, and the child he's built to bury are all already on the table — the record just hands them the mic. (Strictly a precursor context, not part of the album's fifteen-track spine; Ballerina Boy also recurs as a named character the record circles.)

> [!note]- The blessing-voice, and Mary as the album's two threshold events
> The album's only two threshold-state movements before SOL are its two Mary-cluster / blessing-voice tracks. "#1 Champion" (8) is the first *crack* — the ego meets the faceless feminine and is unseated, the threshold merely *approaching.* "Weirdness" (13) is the *crossing* — the pronoun reclaimed, the blessing-voice landing. Held at resonance (not an intent claim): the canonical pop blessing-voice text is Dylan's "Forever Young" (*"may your song always be sung"*), and its best home in this catalogue is right here — "Weirdness"'s *"you were born to shine,"* and, cross-album, the [[Ballerina Boy]] outro's *"let your light shine."* The one thing the Circus can't absorb is the voice that blesses without needing an audience.

> [!note]- The alchemical frame, and its own falsifiers
> In the alchemical lens the analysis uses, the album is a sustained *nigredo* (the blackening) with a single turn to *albedo* (first light) at "Weirdness" — thirteen of the fourteen distinct tracks sit in the blackening; exactly one turns. "SOL" at the end is the destination glimpsed rather than reached, and its brevity *is* the doctrine: a glimpse, not a residence, because a long triumphant ending would assert "love wins" in a way the arc deliberately refuses. The title track maps to the threshold between the lowest sphere and the one just above it on the Tree of Life; whether each later track climbs a further rung is left open.
>
> The disciplined counter-reads, kept on the table: **(1)** "13 of 14 are nigredo" may be near-vacuous — when almost everything satisfies a category, the tag stops discriminating, and a flatter description is just *a concept album with a villain-narrator, a dark-nursery aesthetic, and one softer track.* **(2)** "Tracklist = the Circle" reads sequencing as cosmology; the *seams* are grounded, but shared refrains and short intro/reprise tracks are ordinary album craft. **(3)** "SOL-at-14 is the doctrine" may be over-reading an outro. The falsifier for all three: Roy or the label describing the running order as flow-driven rather than conceptual would drop the Circle-tracklist reading toward coincidence. This is interpretive framing laid over the music — a lens, not a claim about intent.

## Source notes

- **Grounded** (released music, public posts, official videos). The release facts, joint billing, label, tracklist, tagline, and dual-vocalist credit device; the three seams (heard firsthand at the listening and release parties — two carrying a shared refrain that appears only between the pair, the third a wordless music/video bleed); the credited collaborators (Josh Dun, Sia, Bricusse & Newley, Matthew Pauling on tracks 9/10/13, little luna, plus David Burris, Tony Esterly & Cici Ward, and Dale Becker / Noah McCorkle mastering); the Oompa-Loompa interpolation; the John 8:32 lyrics across three eras; the "serve somebody" line and Roy's own Dylan caption on the official short; the nursery-rhyme recoding across the lyrics; the staged comedy (the banana play-button, the yeti and pencils and "you're welcome" jabs); "SOL" released voiced-but-wordless; the era singles that precede it ([[Good Time (1-08)]], [[The Watchers]], [[Ballerina Boy]]). The theatrical spine, [[The Interview]] (EP1–7), is public material on the official channel.
- **Strong readings.** The five-movement arc (sustained blackening, single turn, wordless destination, loop-back); *resolution ≠ triumph*; the Flood/Circus symmetry; the Circle hinge as a deliberate doctrinal inversion; the John 8:32 decay as one tracked corruption; the *serve*-hinge; the upload endgame; the seams as descent-couplings. Grounded in the material, but interpretive — flagged as such.
- **Resonance** (correspondence, never authorial intent). The alchemical / Tree-of-Life mapping; the "Forever Young" blessing-voice lineage; the water→fire element-map across the three albums; the named intellectual substrate (Narby's *Cosmic Serpent* under the outro serpent, Lewis's *Screwtape Letters* behind the Lucius voice, Gurdjieff's "wild animals are wiser" under Moths/Bananas).
- **How we'd be wrong.** If the plain "a dark concept album with a villain-narrator" description accounts for the record as well as the alchemical apparatus does, the apparatus is decorative. The counter-reads above are kept live for exactly that reason.

# Lucius Lullaby (album)

> **One-line reading.** The 2026 album — a sustained descent held almost to the end, where the shadow gets the microphone and the only thing that finally resolves is the survival of the feeling underneath.

*Lucius Lullaby* is the 2026 Jagwar Twin album, released March 27, 2026, and credited jointly to **[[Jagwar Twin]] & [[Sir Lucius]]** — the first time the project shares its billing with the voice it was built to contain. Its tagline tells you the aesthetic: *"Twisted takes on adolescent musical themes for hands that rock the cradle."* This is a record, the strategy implies, that Lucius produced. (Note: the title here is the *album*; there's a separate song, [[Lucius Lullaby]], which is track 2.)

## Overview

Fifteen tracks, about thirty-six minutes, and the shape of the thing is the point. Read in order, the album is a long floor cracked once near the end: nearly every track sits in the "blackening," and only one late turn — track 13, [[Weirdness]] — breaks into first light. The aesthetic is nursery rhyme turned sinister: *ring-around-the-rosie* and *"ashes, ashes, we all fall down,"* a dog-and-god dark-nursery, *"go bananas,"* the Oompa-Loompa interpolation, a music-box lullaby. Childhood forms, in the hands that rock the cradle.

A grounded structural detail, heard firsthand at the listening and release parties: several of the "tracks" are really **one continuous piece split in two.** "The Circle (Lucius Version)" is the music-box overture that runs straight into the title track (both carry *"It's a beautiful life… I'm dreaming"*); "Playing to the Gods" is the refrain that "City of Angels" develops; and "Moths to the Flame"'s backing music becomes "Bananas" without a seam. The album uses short intro tracks as overtures whose refrain the next full track expands — so the running order does real work.

The credits hold a quiet payoff for anyone who's followed Roy's influences: a couple of them are physically *on* the record. Josh Dun of Twenty One Pilots — the band Roy has called his favorite ever — drums on two tracks. Sia is a credited co-writer and backing vocalist on "Weirdness," the album's one turn into the light. The childhood source that "Bad Feeling" twists, the Oompa-Loompa song, brings its original *Willy Wonka* writers (Leslie Bricusse and Anthony Newley) onto the credits as co-writers. The album that recodes inherited songs also pulls some of its inheritances into the room.

## Major releases

The album moves in five movements: an overture and a bargain, a long stretch of the Circus operating, a single turn, a wordless destination, and a loop back to the shadow.

- [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]] — Lucius's music-box retelling of the founding doctrine; the overture.
- [[Lucius Lullaby]] — the title track: rock-bottom named in plain first person, and the bargain that pulls the speaker back from the edge (*"you gotta serve somebody"*).
- [[Great Time to Be Human]] — a toxic-positivity civilization-anthem.
- [[Welcome to the Circus]] — the Circus thesis, Lucius at full external power.
- [[Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)]] — the candy-factory alarm.
- [[Moths to the Flame]] — Lucius's spoken-word anti-civilization doctrine; the overture to —
- [[Bananas]] — the sung id-eruption it licenses.
- [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] — the boasting ego meets the faceless feminine ([[Mary]]) and is unseated. The album's first crack.
- [[Playing to the Gods]] — the false-immortality refrain (*"never gonna die"*) that opens into —
- [[City of Angels]] — its development; the "upload." Hollywood rendered as a paradise of the already-uploaded: deathless and identical, *"everyone looks just like you."* It's the Circle's communion — distinct voices returning a song — forged into the Circus's counterfeit of it, sameness, pointedly on a record by a project called Jagwar *Twin*.
- [[Not Your Homie]] — the bargain enacted.
- [[Bounce]] — kinetic evasion.
- [[Weirdness]] — the turn: the only track to reach first light.
- [[SOL]] — "The Song of Love," voiced but wordless. The destination.
- *Bad Feeling (Oompa Loompa)* — acoustic live at the MOTH Gallery, a quiet Lucius-adjacent bonus that closes the record.

## Who's present

This is **[[Sir Lucius]]**'s album. He brackets it — the first voice (track 1) and the last (track 15) both belong to his side of the house — and he dominates the opening third. **[[Roy]]** is here as the self underneath, the one being put under rather than woken; his breath is what the record finally comes down to. **[[Mary]]** appears at the one crack in the floor, on "#1 Champion," the faceless feminine who unseats the ego. And the [[Ballerina Boy]] — the buried child — is the light the whole descent is circling.

## What it's about

The engine is set in the first two tracks: **a life saved by being put *under* rather than woken.** The title track names the floor (rock-bottom at thirty-five) and the bargain that pulls the speaker back — and the rest of the album is Lucius's world running unopposed, a sedation dressed as a *"beautiful life."* It's the inverse of the debut's flood: where [[Subject to Flooding|Subject to Flooding]] *dissolves* the divided self in water, *Lucius Lullaby* *sedates* it in a dream. Same severing, opposite mechanism — drowning versus the beautiful-life sleep.

There's a quieter symmetry that tells you where the floor is. The album's darkest line — a plain first-person thought of ending one's life — is sung **twice**: once at the bottom, in the title track (rock-bottom at thirty-five), and once again at the turn, in "Weirdness." Same despair, two places. The first time, the only voice that answers is the bargain that puts the speaker under. The second time, the blessing-voice lands. The album measures its own distance by what response that same line gets.

The turn, when it comes, is one pronoun. The angel-voice's *"**your** weirdness is the sunshine"* becomes *"all **my** weirdness is **my** sunshine."* Integration happens not by defeating the shadow but by **reclaiming his distorted light as your own.** That's the whole move, and it's why the album can resolve without anyone winning.

Because here's the thing the album refuses: **triumph, but not resolution.** It's tempting to read the loop back to Lucius at the end as the shadow simply winning — but that conflates two different things. What resolves is not the integration of Roy and Lucius, which stays open. What resolves is the **survival of the feeling** that was never sacrificed: "SOL" endures even with its words stripped away; the breath outlasts the bargain. The album resolves by *preserving what cannot be metabolized*, not by completing the cure. "Doesn't end in triumph" is not the same as "stays in shadow" — the shadow gets the last *track*, but the feeling gets the last thing the shadow can't consume.

## A framework reading

In the alchemical frame the analysis uses, the album is a sustained *nigredo* (the blackening) with a single turn to *albedo* (first light) at "Weirdness." Read that way, "SOL" at the end is the destination glimpsed rather than reached — its brevity *is* the doctrine, a glimpse and not a residence, because a long, triumphant ending would assert "love wins" in a way the arc deliberately refuses. The title track maps to the threshold between the lowest sphere and the one just above it on the Tree of Life; whether each later track climbs a further rung, or whether the structure is something else, is left open. As always, this is interpretive framing laid over the music — a lens, not a claim about intent.

## Suggested listening order

This is an album that *enacts* its arc, so play it straight through, in order — the seams between the paired tracks only land if you do. The five-beat throughline, if you want the map:

1. [[The Circle (Lucius Version)]] → [[Lucius Lullaby]] — the overture and the bargain
2. [[Welcome to the Circus]] — the Circus at full power
3. [[#1 Champion (la Conquistadora)]] — the first crack
4. [[Weirdness]] — the turn; the shadow's light reclaimed
5. [[SOL]] — the wordless destination
